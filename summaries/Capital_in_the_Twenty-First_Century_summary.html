Capital in the Twenty-First Century

Thomas Piketty

Introduction

The distribution of wealth is one of today's most contested questions, and the answers handed down by previous generations are unsatisfying. Malthus and Ricardo feared scarcity of land; Marx predicted infinite accumulation and apocalypse; Kuznets, writing in the optimistic glow of the postwar American boom, proposed a fairy-tale bell curve in which inequality would rise in early industrialization and then fall automatically as development progressed. None of these predictions survive contact with the long historical record, which only becomes visible once tax returns, estate records, and national accounts from more than twenty countries over three centuries are patiently assembled.

When that record is assembled, what emerges is that modern growth and the diffusion of knowledge have made it possible to escape the Marxist apocalypse, but they have not altered the deep structures of capital and inequality nearly as much as the postwar generation assumed. Two forces push toward convergence — chiefly the diffusion of knowledge and skills, supported by education and training — and two families of forces push toward divergence: the takeoff of top labor incomes (so far mostly an Anglo-Saxon phenomenon), and, more fundamentally, the accumulation and concentration of wealth whenever the rate of return on capital durably exceeds the rate of economic growth.

This second mechanism can be summed up in a single inequality, r > g, where r is the average annual rate of return on capital and g is the rate of growth of the economy. When r significantly exceeds g — as it did throughout most of history before 1914, and as is likely to be the case again in the twenty-first century as demographic and productivity growth slow — inherited wealth grows faster than output and wages, and past fortunes come to dominate those amassed by a lifetime of work. This has nothing to do with market imperfection; quite the contrary, the more perfect the capital market, the more likely r is to exceed g.

The book is built around two kinds of evidence: long-run series on income inequality drawn from tax records (the World Top Incomes Database), and long-run series on wealth built from estate records and national balance sheets. Britain and France — and especially France, whose Revolution produced an astonishingly modern cadaster and estate tax in 1791 — provide the richest continuous sources, supplemented by data from Germany, the United States, Japan, Sweden and beyond. The history of the distribution of wealth is never purely economic: it is always political, shaped by how societies view what is just, by relative power, and by the collective choices that follow.

The policy horizon that emerges is a progressive global tax on capital, coupled with international financial transparency, as the instrument best suited to regain democratic control over twenty-first-century capitalism while preserving economic openness. Such a tax is admittedly utopian in its purest form, but it provides a benchmark against which more modest regional experiments — beginning in Europe — can be measured. The alternative is the return of nationalist and protectionist reflexes, which are unlikely to produce anything but frustration.

Chapter 1: Income and Output

The division of national income between capital and labor has always been at the heart of distributional conflict, from eighteenth-century peasants and landlords to the Marikana platinum miners shot down in 2012 while striking for a doubling of their 500-euro monthly wage. Contrary to the textbook cliché of a stable two-thirds/one-third labor/capital split, the capital share has varied considerably: it rose in the first half of the nineteenth century, drifted thereafter, collapsed under the shocks of 1914–1945 to historic lows around 1950, and then rebuilt itself steadily, accelerating after the conservative revolutions of Thatcher and Reagan and the financial deregulation of the 1990s.

To study these movements carefully, a small vocabulary is needed. National income is domestic output (GDP minus capital depreciation of about 10 percent) plus net income received from abroad. Capital, used interchangeably with wealth, is defined as the sum of all nonhuman assets that can be owned and exchanged on a market — real estate, financial assets, business capital, land, natural resources — net of debt. Human capital is deliberately excluded because, outside of slave societies, it cannot be traded permanently. National wealth decomposes into private wealth plus public wealth, and also into domestic capital plus net foreign capital.

The ratio of the capital stock to the annual flow of national income, denoted β, is the central quantity of the book. In the developed countries today this ratio is typically between 5 and 6: with average national income of roughly 30,000 euros per capita per year, average private wealth runs to 150,000–200,000 euros per capita, split roughly equally between housing and financial/business assets. Tying the stock to the flow of income from capital is the first fundamental law of capitalism: α = r × β, where α is capital's share in national income and r the average rate of return. A ratio of β = 600% and a return of r = 5% automatically implies a capital share of α = 30%. This is an accounting identity rather than a causal claim, but it constrains the three variables to move together.

Globally, roughly 70 percent of world output is still produced in Europe and North America, but this share is falling back toward the 20–30 percent that matches their share of world population, as the great convergence process unfolds. When national income is compared using purchasing power parity rather than current exchange rates, per capita monthly income ranges from about 150–250 euros in sub-Saharan Africa and India to 2,500–3,000 euros in Western Europe, North America and Japan, with a global average near 600–800 euros. The historical record suggests that the main engine of convergence between poor and rich countries has not been capital flowing from rich to poor but the diffusion of knowledge, skills, and institutions — most emphatically demonstrated by Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, and China, which financed their own capital accumulation out of domestic savings while remaining open to trade and ideas.

Chapter 2: Growth: Illusions and Realities

Economic growth over the very long run is far slower, and far more recent as a broad human experience, than contemporary public debate suggests. From antiquity to 1700, both population and per capita output grew at rates below 0.1 percent per year; from 1700 to 2012, global output grew at an average of about 1.6 percent a year, split almost evenly between population growth (0.8 percent) and per capita output growth (0.8 percent). These rates look unimpressive at the scale of a year, but the law of cumulative growth transforms them at the scale of a generation or a century: 1 percent per year multiplies a quantity by 1.35 over 30 years and by 20 over three centuries. The same logic applies to cumulative returns on capital, and the small but persistent gap between r and g is precisely what gives the dynamics of inequality their bite.

Both population and per capita output growth follow a bell curve when plotted from antiquity to 2100. Demographic growth peaked near 2 percent in 1950–1990 and is expected to fall back toward 0.1–0.4 percent later in this century, with Europe, Japan, and eventually China and others approaching or crossing into negative territory. Economic growth per capita peaked later, buoyed first by European catch-up during the Trente Glorieuses of 1945–1975 and then by Asian and especially Chinese catch-up. No country at the world technological frontier has ever sustained per capita growth above 1.5 percent per year for long; the 4–5 percent European growth of the postwar decades was a catch-up phenomenon that ended automatically when convergence was complete.

This matters for inequality in two ways. First, strong growth — demographic or economic — mechanically reduces the weight of inherited wealth, because each new generation has to build much of its own economic position; slow growth automatically magnifies the weight of fortunes accumulated in the past. Second, the return to low growth for both population and output, which is the most likely scenario for the second half of the twenty-first century, sets up precisely the conditions under which r > g pushes toward the hyperconcentration of wealth characteristic of earlier centuries. The hope that modern growth would by itself deliver social mobility and meritocracy was largely a product of the unusually rapid postwar decades; such hope cannot be met by economic growth alone and will require specific institutions.

Inflation, finally, is best understood as a twentieth-century novelty. From 1700 to 1914, prices were essentially stable in Britain, France, the United States and Germany: a franc, a pound, or a dollar meant the same thing for generations, and novels from Austen through Balzac through Mahfouz could convey a character's social standing simply by stating his income. The shocks of 1914–1945 shattered this stability: French and German prices rose by factors of 100 and 300 respectively between 1913 and 1950. Inflation has since been contained to around 2 percent in the rich countries, but monetary references have lost their literary and social meaning, and inflation itself has become a crude and unpredictable mechanism for redistributing wealth, favoring sophisticated asset holders over small savers.

Chapter 3: The Metamorphoses of Capital

In Britain and France alike, the total value of national capital traced a striking U-shaped curve over the past three centuries. From 1700 to 1914, national capital hovered stably at six to seven years of national income. Between 1914 and 1950, it collapsed to just two or three years of national income under the combined shocks of two world wars, the Great Depression, inflation, rent control, nationalizations, and the destruction of foreign portfolios. Since 1950 it has climbed back steadily, reaching five to six years of national income by 2010, with France slightly above Britain. The shocks of the twentieth century, in other words, produced the illusion that capitalism had been structurally transformed; in reality, the deep ratio has largely returned to its pre-1914 level.

What has been transformed is the composition of capital, not its total weight relative to income. In the early eighteenth century, farmland represented four to five years of national income, or roughly two-thirds of national capital. Today farmland is worth less than 10 percent of national income and accounts for less than 2 percent of national wealth. Its place has been taken by housing, which rose from about one year of national income to more than three, and by industrial and financial capital and infrastructure. On top of this came the spectacular growth of foreign assets in the age of colonial empire: on the eve of World War I, Britain owned net foreign assets worth nearly two years of its national income and France more than one, enabling both countries to run permanent trade deficits while collecting dividends, interest, and rents from the rest of the world.

Public wealth has always been secondary to private wealth in both countries. Public assets typically hover between 50 and 150 percent of national income, matched or exceeded by public debts, leaving net public wealth close to zero — sometimes positive, sometimes negative, but always small compared with the enormous stock of private wealth. The history of British public debt is particularly instructive: after the Napoleonic wars, Britain carried a debt of nearly 200 percent of GDP and chose to honor it in full through a century of budget surpluses, which transferred resources from taxpayers to bondholders and entrenched a prosperous rentier class — exactly the society Jane Austen depicted. France, by contrast, repudiated much of its Ancien Régime debt in 1797 and later wiped out twentieth-century debts through inflation, which made rentiers disappear more quickly but arbitrarily expropriated small savers.

These two strategies — debt honored through surpluses and debt eroded through inflation — illustrate that the relationship between public debt, private wealth, and inflation has always been, at bottom, a question of who pays whom. In the nineteenth century the answer was clear: taxpayers paid rentiers handsomely. In the twentieth century, inflation repudiated these claims without ever being officially acknowledged. After 1980, France underwent a remarkable and poorly understood transformation, moving from a mixed economy in which the state owned roughly 25–30 percent of national wealth (a kind of capitalism without capitalists) to a private-ownership regime in which the state owns almost nothing and private wealth has risen to six years of national income.

Chapter 4: From Old Europe to the New World

The German case largely confirms the British and French pattern, with local variations. German national capital followed the same U-shaped trajectory, falling from about 6.5 years of national income in 1913 to less than three in 1950 and rebounding since. Germany drowned its public debt in inflation even more dramatically than France did — prices rose by a factor of 300 between 1913 and 1950, culminating in the hyperinflation of 1923 — which permanently shaped German aversion to inflation. German private wealth today stands slightly below the French and British levels, around four years of national income rather than five or six, a difference explained almost entirely by the lower stock market valuation of German firms. This lower valuation is not a sign of weakness but reflects the stakeholder model of Rhenish capitalism, in which workers, regional governments, and other constituencies share governance rights with shareholders.

The collapse of European capital between 1914 and 1945 cannot be explained primarily by physical destruction. Wartime destruction accounted for roughly a quarter of the total fall in France and a third in Germany; in Britain, where there was almost no destruction, national capital fell by nearly as much. The real drivers were the loss of foreign portfolios (especially catastrophic for Britain), very low national saving in the context of war and depression, and the adoption after 1945 of policies — rent control, nationalizations, financial regulation, progressive taxation — that held asset prices at historic lows and deliberately contained the economic power of capital owners. As the historian's phrase has it, the fall in the capital/income ratio was to a large extent the euthanasia of European capitalists, and it was partly a deliberate policy choice.

The United States presents a strikingly different trajectory. In the colonial era and early republic, national capital was worth barely three years of national income, despite the apparent abundance of land: land was cheap precisely because it was plentiful, and immigrants arrived with little. As Tocqueville observed in 1840, the relative scarcity of capital — and therefore the relative weakness of inherited wealth — gave American society a more egalitarian and democratic character than the Europe of the time, at least in the northern and western states. This changed over the nineteenth century as industrial and financial capital accumulated, bringing the US capital/income ratio to around 4.5–5 years by 1910. The shocks of the twentieth century affected the United States much more mildly than Europe, and its capital/income ratio has oscillated between four and five years throughout the past century — a much smaller U than the European one. This stability is one reason US textbooks (notably Samuelson's) could treat the capital/income ratio as a universal constant; the European experience proves otherwise.

The deepest asymmetry between Europe and America in this early period involved slavery. In 1800, slaves represented 20 percent of the US population and 40 percent of the population of the southern states. The market value of slaves in the South was roughly 2.5 to 3 years of the southern states' income; adding this to the value of land brought southern capital to a level comparable to or greater than that of Britain or France. The North was a society of small landowners with relatively little capital; the South was a society in which half the population owned the other half. The antebellum United States was thus not one world but two, and the complicated legacy of this contradiction — between egalitarian promise and racial domination — still shapes the structure of American inequality and the limits of the American welfare state.

Chapter 5: The Capital/Income Ratio over the Long Run

The long-run behavior of the capital/income ratio is governed by a simple dynamic relation, the second fundamental law of capitalism: β = s / g, where s is the net savings rate and g the rate of growth of the economy. If a country saves 12 percent of its income and grows at 2 percent, the capital/income ratio will tend in the long run toward 600 percent. Unlike the first fundamental law, this is not an accounting identity but an equilibrium relation: it is asymptotic, true in the long run after shocks have dissipated, and only for capital that can be accumulated (pure natural resources and valuation effects aside). It explains why small differences in growth rates can produce very large differences in the quantity of capital accumulated relative to income.

This law accounts very well for the facts. If s = 10 percent and g = 1.5 percent (a plausible long-run European scenario), then β tends toward about 7; if growth instead settles at 3 percent, as in the United States with higher demographic growth, then β tends toward only 3–4. The same savings rates applied to very different growth rates yield very different quantities of capital, and this difference in demographic dynamism is the main reason Europe's capital/income ratio has always been structurally higher than that of the United States. Lower growth does not make a society poorer; it simply means that past wealth, relative to current income, looms larger.

The rebound of private wealth in the rich countries since 1970, from two to three years of national income to four to seven years, can be decomposed into three complementary forces. The dominant force is slower growth coupled with continued high saving, acting through β = s / g. The second is the privatization of public wealth, which in France and Germany transferred a quarter to a third of national wealth from public to private hands over a few decades, and which in the former Soviet bloc took a much more extreme form. The third is the historical rebound of asset prices, especially real estate and stocks, which recovered from the artificially low levels of the postwar decades. Over the very long run, the accumulation of savings — the volume effect — dominates price effects, but on shorter horizons the price effect can be enormous, as Japan's 1990 bubble and Spain's 2007 bubble illustrate.

Projecting this logic into the twenty-first century with plausible savings rates and declining growth, the global capital/income ratio could plausibly approach 700 percent by 2100, approximately the level observed in Europe on the eve of World War I. The world would then look, in terms of capital intensity, rather like Belle Époque Europe — not because capitalism has failed, but because it is functioning normally in a low-growth regime. Whether this state of affairs also produces the extreme concentration of wealth characteristic of the Belle Époque depends on other factors: the distribution of savings, unequal returns to capital, and above all the tax and regulatory choices that societies make.

Chapter 6: The Capital-Labor Split in the Twenty-First Century

The share of capital in national income, α, also traces a U-shaped curve in Britain and France, though shallower than the curve of the capital/income ratio itself. Capital's share stood at 35–40 percent in the late eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, fell to 20–25 percent at mid-twentieth century, and has climbed back to 25–30 percent in the early twenty-first. These movements correspond to an average return on capital, r, of roughly 5–6 percent in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, rising to 7–8 percent in the mid-twentieth (when capital was scarce), and settling around 4–5 percent today. The pure return on capital, which strips out management labor and other informal costs, has been remarkably stable at around 4–5 percent across three centuries, with a slight secular decline.

Why does the return on capital not fall to zero as the stock of capital grows? The standard answer is the decreasing marginal productivity of capital, which depends on the elasticity of substitution between capital and labor in production. If that elasticity is less than one, capital's share falls when the capital/income ratio rises; if greater than one, capital's share rises when β rises; if exactly one, the capital share is constant (the Cobb-Douglas case that became dogma in postwar economics). The historical evidence over the very long run suggests an elasticity greater than one — roughly between 1.3 and 1.6 — which means the many uses of modern capital (housing, offices, infrastructure, machines, robots, patents) are flexible enough that additional accumulation does not drive the rate of return down as fast as the stock goes up. Over three centuries, the volume effect has dominated the price effect.

This has a sobering implication: as growth slows and the capital/income ratio climbs back to six, seven, or eight years of national income in the twenty-first century, capital's share of income is likely to rise rather than fall. The return on capital will decrease somewhat, but not enough to offset the growing weight of the stock. Capital's share could plausibly return to 30–40 percent globally, levels last seen in the nineteenth century. Human capital has not supplanted nonhuman capital in the way postwar optimists hoped: technology has increased the need for skills, but it has increased the need for buildings, machines, and financial assets in roughly equal measure.

Marx's prediction that infinite capital accumulation would drive the rate of profit to zero and eventually trigger capitalism's collapse depended implicitly on the assumption that productivity growth was zero over the long run. Once steady productivity growth is admitted, the dynamic equilibrium β = s / g gives a finite capital/income ratio rather than an infinite one, and Marx's apocalypse dissolves into a less dramatic but still unsettling long-run equilibrium in which capital's weight is very high and its returns significantly higher than growth. Technology has neither limits nor morality, and there is no natural force that inevitably reduces the importance of capital relative to labor. If a more just social order is desired, it will require specific institutions — progressive taxation above all — rather than a passive trust in the caprices of technology.

Chapter 7: Inequality and Concentration: Preliminary Bearings

Income inequality in any society decomposes into three pieces: inequality of labor income, inequality of ownership of capital and the income flowing from it, and the correlation between the two. Vautrin's lecture to Rastignac in Père Goriot captures the issue more vividly than any table: in 1810s Paris, a brilliant legal career could yield at most a few thousand francs a year after decades of effort, while marrying into an inheritance of a million francs brought in 50,000 francs a year immediately — ten times as much, without working. In such a society, study and work simply could not compete with inherited capital, and the entire moral question of meritocracy dissolves into cynicism.

To compare inequalities across societies, the book relies on deciles and centiles of the income and wealth distributions rather than synthetic indices like the Gini coefficient, which mix together fundamentally different kinds of inequality and obscure what is happening at the top. In the most egalitarian societies recorded — Scandinavia in the 1970s and 1980s — the top 10 percent of earners received about 20 percent of total wages; in France and Germany today they receive 25–30 percent; in the United States today they receive about 35 percent, with clear signs of continued rise. Wage hierarchies, in other words, are genuinely malleable: the same average wage is compatible with very different distributions.

Inequality of wealth is always and everywhere more extreme than inequality of labor income. The bottom 50 percent of the wealth distribution owns almost nothing — less than 5 percent of total wealth in every known society — while the top 10 percent owns at least 50 percent, often 60–70 percent, and in the most concentrated societies (Belle Époque Europe) as much as 90 percent. The top 1 percent alone owned more than half of all wealth in pre-1914 Europe. The most important structural transformation of the twentieth century was the emergence of what can be called a patrimonial middle class: a middle 40 percent of the wealth distribution that came to own roughly a quarter to a third of national wealth, where in the nineteenth century it had owned barely 5 percent. This is a fragile but historically unprecedented development, and it is easy to underestimate how much it has altered the social landscape.

Two very different configurations can produce the same very high level of total income inequality. The first is a hyperpatrimonial society of rentiers, where the top centile's income comes primarily from inherited capital; this was the Ancien Régime, Belle Époque Europe, and the antebellum American South. The second is a hypermeritocratic society or society of supermanagers, where the top centile's income comes primarily from extraordinarily high labor income — the United States of the early twenty-first century. These two logics can also combine: there is nothing to stop the children of supermanagers from becoming rentiers, and the concentration of wealth in the United States is in fact drifting toward such a combination, which would produce a form of inequality more extreme than any in recorded history.

Chapter 8: Two Worlds

The twentieth-century history of inequality in France is dominated by a single dramatic fact: the collapse of top capital incomes between 1914 and 1945, and the failure of those incomes to recover since. The share of the top decile in French national income fell from about 45–50 percent in the Belle Époque to 30–35 percent by the 1950s, where it has roughly remained. The share of the top centile fell even more sharply, from above 20 percent to 7–9 percent. The compression of inequality was due almost entirely to the fall of the rentier; wage inequality in France has been remarkably stable across the entire twentieth century, with the top 10 percent of earners consistently receiving about 25 percent of total wages.

The shocks responsible were not automatic or gradual: they were war, hyperinflation, the Great Depression, nationalizations, rent control, progressive taxation, and the deliberate regulatory climate of the postwar decades. The society of rentiers in which inherited wealth dominated the top centile gave way to a society of managers in which high incomes come mainly from labor until one climbs very high in the distribution. In 1932, income from capital was dominant above the top 0.5 percent; in 2005, one has to climb to the top 0.1 percent before capital income surpasses labor income. This is a real transformation, but it is a compression rather than an abolition: rentiers fell behind managers, rather than managers racing ahead.

The postwar period in France saw three distinct phases. Inequality rose sharply between 1945 and 1967 as reconstruction and rapid growth fed both profits and wage differentials; it then fell sharply between 1968 and 1983 as the Grenelle Accords indexed the minimum wage and successive governments boosted it annually so that its purchasing power rose by more than 130 percent while the average wage rose only by about 50 percent; and it has risen again since 1983, with a new phenomenon — the explosion of supermanager salaries — emerging in the 1990s and 2000s. The top centile's share of French wages rose about 30 percent in a little over a decade after 2000, still less dramatic than in the United States but clearly visible.

The American trajectory is more complex. In 1910 the United States was less unequal than Europe; by mid-century it had become slightly more unequal; and from 1980 onwards inequality has exploded. The share of the top decile in US national income rose from 30–35 percent in the 1970s to 45–50 percent in the early 2010s, matching or surpassing the Belle Époque levels. Nearly three-quarters of this increase was captured by the top 1 percent, and roughly half of that by the top 0.1 percent. Over the thirty years from 1977 to 2007 leading up to the financial crisis, the richest 10 percent of Americans captured three-quarters of total economic growth. This transfer of fifteen points of national income from the bottom 90 percent to the top 10 percent was four times larger than the much-discussed US trade deficit, and it likely contributed to the financial instability of 2008 by pushing middle-class households into heavy borrowing. Unlike in France, the rise in US inequality is due primarily to the explosion of top wages — the emergence of supermanagers — rather than to the revival of top capital incomes, though income from capital remains dominant at the very top.

Chapter 9: Inequality of Labor Income

The standard explanation of wage inequality is a race between education and technology: technology raises the demand for certain skills, education raises the supply, and the wage structure reflects their relative pace. This framework captures something real. In the United States, Goldin and Katz have shown that the wage gap between college graduates and those without college widened precisely when the growth of college degrees slowed. In France, the democratization of education raised the whole wage hierarchy by one notch without changing its internal structure: yesterday's high-school leavers are today's college graduates, and yesterday's college graduates are today's postgraduates, but the gaps between them have remained roughly constant. Over the long run, investing in education and skills is the most effective way to raise wages at the bottom and reduce wage inequality.

Institutions also matter, and often decisively. Wage inequality compressed in both France and the United States during the world wars through deliberate policy: the National War Labor Board in the United States approved raises only for the lowest paid. The French minimum wage played an enormous role in the compression of 1968–1983 and the reverse after 1983. In the United States, the real value of the federal minimum wage peaked in 1969 at the equivalent of about $10 in today's dollars and has been allowed to erode sharply since. Britain introduced a minimum wage only in 1999; Germany and Sweden set minimums through sectoral collective bargaining rather than legislation. These differences matter for the bottom half of the distribution, though they have less influence at the top.

At the top, the race between education and technology utterly fails to explain the observed pattern. The explosion of very high incomes since 1980 is a specifically Anglo-Saxon phenomenon — most extreme in the United States, somewhat less so in Britain, Canada and Australia, and very muted in continental Europe and Japan. Between the early 1980s and the early 2010s, the top 1 percent's share of national income roughly doubled in the United States (from around 9 to nearly 20 percent) while rising only modestly in France, Germany, Japan, or Sweden (by two or three points of national income). Since technological change has been broadly similar across these countries, the divergence cannot be technological. It reflects differences in institutions, social norms, and tax policy.

The most convincing account of the American supermanager phenomenon is that it reflects the collapse of institutional constraints on top pay rather than a sudden rise in executive productivity. When a corporate CFO's individual contribution to a firm with ten billion euros in revenue cannot be precisely measured, his compensation is determined by corporate hierarchies, compensation committees (whose members are themselves highly paid executives), and social norms of acceptability. Studies of pay for luck show that executive compensation rises most rapidly when sales and profits rise for reasons outside the firm's control — exactly the opposite of what a marginal productivity theory would predict. The dramatic decrease in top marginal income tax rates after 1980 in the English-speaking countries gave top managers a much stronger incentive to bargain aggressively for higher pay, since they got to keep most of the raise. This feedback loop between lower top tax rates, higher top salaries, and the political influence of top earners has amplified itself into the meritocratic extremism of the contemporary United States.

Chapter 10: Inequality of Capital Ownership

Inequality of wealth throughout the nineteenth century and up to World War I was extreme everywhere in Europe, and there was no sign it was decreasing before the war. In France, the top decile owned 80–90 percent of national wealth throughout the nineteenth century and close to 90 percent by 1910; the top centile owned 50–60 percent. In Paris, where wealth was even more concentrated, the top centile owned about 70 percent of aggregate wealth on the eve of World War I. Britain and Sweden showed similar or even higher levels. Belle Époque Europe was a world in which half the population died with essentially no wealth, a middle 40 percent owned almost nothing more, and a thin elite of rentiers owned everything.

Then came the shocks. The top decile's share of national wealth in France fell to 60–70 percent by the 1950s, and the top centile's share fell from 60 percent to about 20–30 percent. Britain and Sweden experienced similar collapses. In every case, essentially all of what the top centile lost went to the middle 40 percent, while the share of the bottom half remained minuscule. This is the emergence of the patrimonial middle class, the single most important structural transformation in the history of wealth distribution in the twentieth century — a group of people who collectively own a third of national wealth and individually hold between 100,000 and 400,000 euros in housing and savings. In the United States, the compression of wealth inequality was milder: the top decile's share fell from about 80 percent in 1910 to 65 percent in the 1970s before rising to above 70 percent today, while the top centile now owns around 35 percent.

The fundamental mechanism behind the hyperconcentration of wealth in traditional low-growth societies is the durable inequality r > g. When growth is on the order of 0.5–1 percent a year and the rate of return on capital is 4–5 percent, past wealth is recapitalized much faster than new wealth can be earned and saved. People with large fortunes need only reinvest a fraction of their capital income to see their wealth grow faster than the economy; inherited wealth thus dominates earned wealth, and the concentration of capital rises inexorably toward the extreme levels that make inheritance society possible. For most of recorded history, r has been between two and ten times greater than g; the twentieth century, when net after-tax returns on capital actually fell below the growth rate, was historically unprecedented.

The shocks of 1914–1945 destroyed foreign portfolios, triggered hyperinflation, introduced rent control and nationalizations, and above all made possible the progressive income and estate taxes that permanently lowered the net-of-tax return on capital. This combination of physical, political, and fiscal shocks produced the unique twentieth-century episode in which r minus g was negative and the concentration of wealth declined. None of these forces is automatic or permanent. As growth slows again in the twenty-first century and tax competition between states erodes progressive taxation of capital, all the ingredients are in place for the gap between r and g to widen again and for wealth concentration to drift back toward Belle Époque levels — unless specific political choices intervene.

Chapter 11: Merit and Inheritance in the Long Run

The annual flow of inheritance in France — total bequests and gifts expressed as a share of national income — itself traces a U-shaped curve. It stood at 20–25 percent of national income throughout the nineteenth century and up to 1914; it collapsed to barely 4–5 percent in the 1950s; and it has rebounded steadily since, reaching about 15 percent in 2010 and heading higher. This annual flow can be decomposed into three forces: by = μ × m × β, where β is the capital/income ratio, m the mortality rate, and μ the ratio of average wealth at death to average wealth of the living. Each of these three forces has its own U-shape, and they move together in a way that amplifies their combined effect.

The postwar generation's belief that inheritance had disappeared was shaped by the exceptional postwar low, not by a permanent structural shift. The rising life expectancy that makes people inherit later (raising the average age of inheritance from thirty in the nineteenth century to about fifty today) was expected to reduce the weight of inheritance, but wealth also ages: the ratio μ of wealth at death to wealth of the living has risen sharply, so that the dead in 2010 were on average more than twice as wealthy as the living once gifts are included. The rise of lifetime giving — now nearly equal in volume to bequests — is a key ingredient in this rebound, and it has pulled the effective age of transmission back down.

A compact way to measure the significance of inheritance in individual lives is to ask what fraction of each generation receives, in inheritance or gifts, an amount larger than the lifetime labor income of the bottom 50 percent of earners. In the nineteenth century, this fraction was about 10 percent; it fell to 2–5 percent for the cohorts born in the early and mid-twentieth century; and it has risen to roughly 12–15 percent for those born since 1970. In other words, around one in seven people in the current generation will inherit more than a low-wage worker earns across an entire working life, while roughly half of the population will inherit virtually nothing. Contemporary society is thus not a society of pure rentiers as in Balzac's Paris, but a society of petits rentiers — far less spectacular, harder to represent culturally, but affecting a broader and more socially significant group than in the past.

The deeper point is that the disappearance of inheritance was an illusion of the postwar decades, and the return of inheritance is already well under way in Europe (less so in the United States, because of higher demographic growth). Whenever r durably exceeds g, the arithmetic tilts inexorably in favor of past over present accumulation: capital saved in one generation grows faster than new capital can be earned and saved, and inherited wealth comes to dominate. Without specific institutions, the democratic hope that work and talent will outweigh birth is very difficult to sustain in a low-growth, high-return regime. Emile Durkheim in 1893 predicted that modern democracies would not long tolerate inherited wealth and would eventually end it at death; that prediction has not come true, but the tension he identified between democratic promise and patrimonial reality remains urgent.

Chapter 12: Global Inequality of Wealth in the Twenty-First Century

The Forbes global billionaire rankings, for all their methodological flaws, document a striking fact: the real rate of growth of the largest fortunes in the world has been between 6 and 7 percent a year since the 1980s, compared with about 2 percent for average global wealth and 1.4 percent for average global income. The number of billionaires per 100 million adults has risen from 5 to 30 between 1987 and 2013, and their share of global private wealth from 0.4 percent to 1.5 percent. The fortunes of pure entrepreneurs like Bill Gates and of pure heiresses like Liliane Bettencourt have grown at nearly identical rates, strongly suggesting that once a fortune passes a certain threshold, it grows according to a dynamic of its own, independent of any personal labor or talent.

The most revealing evidence for unequal returns to capital comes from the endowments of US universities, which publish detailed annual data. Over 1980–2010 the 500 universities with endowments below $100 million earned an average real return of 6.2 percent a year; the 60 with endowments above $1 billion earned 8.8 percent; Harvard, Yale, and Princeton together earned 10.2 percent. These differences reflect economies of scale in portfolio management: a $30 billion endowment can spend $100 million a year on specialized investment advisors and gain access to alternative investments — private equity, hedge funds, real estate, commodities — that are simply not open to smaller investors. The largest endowments earn higher returns without taking on proportionally more risk; they earn them because they can afford better management.

This phenomenon compounds the already powerful logic of r > g. If the top thousandth of the global wealth distribution can earn 6 percent a year on their capital while the rest of the world's wealth grows at 2 percent, their share of global wealth will more than triple in thirty years. Even a gap of a few percentage points, sustained over decades, leads to runaway concentration. Global wealth is already highly concentrated: the top 0.1 percent owns roughly 20 percent of global wealth, the top 1 percent around 50 percent, the top 10 percent between 80 and 90 percent, and the bottom half less than 5 percent — levels comparable to Belle Époque Europe.

The more realistic threat is thus not international divergence — the poor countries gradually buying the rich ones, or China owning Europe — but what can be called oligarchic divergence, in which the wealthiest individuals in every country, including the emerging economies, pull steadily further away from everyone else. European households currently own about 20 times as much wealth as the combined Chinese sovereign wealth funds and central bank reserves; the rich countries are not about to be bought out by anyone. What is real is the growing political and fiscal secession of the largest fortunes from the societies in which they live, often through tax havens. Inflation is a blunt and often counterproductive instrument against this process; only a progressive annual tax on wealth, coordinated internationally, can effectively contain it.

Chapter 13: A Social State for the Twenty-First Century

The central economic and political fact of the twentieth century is the construction of the social state. In the nineteenth century and up to 1914, taxes took less than 10 percent of national income in every rich country, and the government confined itself to its regalian functions: police, courts, army, foreign affairs, rudimentary infrastructure. Between 1920 and 1980, the tax share rose to between one-third and one-half of national income — 30 percent in the United States, 40 percent in Britain, 45–55 percent in continental Europe — and has since stabilized at that level. This historic transformation financed not a return of feudal privilege but the construction of universal public services and replacement incomes: roughly 10–15 percent of national income goes to health and education, another 10–15 (sometimes 20) percent to pensions and other transfers.

Modern redistribution is thus not primarily a transfer of income from the rich to the poor. It is a system of universal rights and equal access to fundamental goods: education for every child regardless of parental income, health care regardless of circumstance, retirement income regardless of savings. This logic of rights, which can be traced back to the 1789 Declaration's insistence that "social distinctions can be based only on common utility," is what the social state actually delivers. Taken together, the third fiscal revolution of the twentieth century — universal public education, universal health insurance, and public pensions — lifted the elderly out of the endemic poverty that had been normal until the 1950s and gave every child access to basic opportunities.

The great leap forward of the social state is finished; it will not happen again. A second doubling of the tax share to 70 or 80 percent of national income is neither realistic nor particularly desirable in the foreseeable future. The challenge now is to modernize and consolidate existing social states rather than to dismantle or dramatically expand them. Two priority areas are equal access to higher education, where the current reality bears little resemblance to the meritocratic rhetoric (the average parental income of Harvard students lies around the top 2 percent of the US distribution; at Sciences Po, around the top 10 percent of the French distribution), and the reform of pay-as-you-go pension systems in a world of low growth and aging populations.

The available evidence is deflating for the myth of American exceptionalism in social mobility. Intergenerational earnings mobility is in fact lower in the United States than in the Nordic countries or continental Europe, and it is likely to decline further as tuition fees at the most prestigious American universities track the rise of top incomes. Free tuition is not enough to solve the problem — as Bourdieu and Passeron showed fifty years ago, French grandes écoles concentrate public education spending on students from the most privileged social backgrounds — but tuition systems that depend on parental ability to pay are clearly incompatible with equal opportunity. A productive reform would combine the independence and energy of decentralized universities with substantial public financing and transparent admissions, on the model of public health insurance systems that leave producers autonomous while guaranteeing equal patient access.

Chapter 14: Rethinking the Progressive Income Tax

The progressive income tax was the great fiscal innovation of the twentieth century and the central instrument of the social state. Before 1914, the top marginal rate of income tax was zero or close to it in every country; even the countries that had adopted a progressive income tax before the war (Prussia in 1891, Britain in 1909, the United States in 1913) set top rates of no more than a few percent and applied them to almost no one. France passed its income tax on July 15, 1914, three weeks before the declaration of war, with a top rate of 2 percent; no one would have voted for it in peacetime. Progressive taxation at meaningful rates, in other words, is the child of war and political upheaval, not of peaceful democratic evolution.

After 1918, top rates shot up everywhere: 50 percent in France by 1920, 60 percent by 1924, 72 percent by 1925; even higher in the United States and Britain. The interwar and postwar decades saw the United States and Britain invent a distinctive institution: the confiscatory tax on incomes and inheritances deemed excessive. The top US federal income tax rate averaged 81 percent from 1932 to 1980, reaching 94 percent during World War II; in Britain it reached 98 percent on unearned income in the 1940s and again in the 1970s. The purpose of such rates was not to raise revenue — confiscatory brackets never yield much — but to reduce top incomes at their source and make their continued growth economically unattractive. The same logic was applied to estates, with US and British top rates reaching 70–80 percent for half a century. No continental European country ever matched these Anglo-Saxon peaks, except Germany briefly under the occupation.

The turning point came in the 1980s. Between 1980 and 1988, the top US income tax rate fell from 70 to 28 percent; Britain followed a similar path. This was a political choice, not a technical necessity. Its consequences have been spectacular. The close historical correlation between the decline of top marginal tax rates and the rise of top income shares across the rich countries strongly suggests that high top rates acted as a protective barrier against aggressive top-level bargaining, and their disappearance is a major part of the explanation for the supermanager phenomenon in the English-speaking world. Econometric analysis of pay for luck in US corporations supports the same conclusion: top executives respond strongly to the net-of-tax return on successful rent extraction.

The right policy response is not to dismantle progressive income taxation but to rebuild it and extend it to capital income. The optimal top marginal tax rate on extremely high incomes, once behavioral responses and the incentive structure of rent-seeking are modeled, is likely to be around 80 percent applied above thresholds of perhaps 500,000 or 1 million dollars a year. Such a rate would not bring in massive revenue — because the incomes it targets would decrease — but it would sharply reduce the incentive for top executives to bargain for outsized pay packages and slow the dynamics of divergence in the wage hierarchy. Combined with the inheritance tax and a new progressive tax on capital, the progressive income tax remains an essential pillar of a fair fiscal system; without it, the recent trend toward regressivity at the very top — where effective tax rates are often lower than for the middle class — will continue and undermine the legitimacy of the social state.

Chapter 15: A Global Tax on Capital

The ideal policy instrument for regulating twenty-first-century patrimonial capitalism is a progressive global tax on capital, coordinated internationally and supported by full financial transparency. Such a tax is admittedly utopian in the sense that no global authority exists to impose it, but it is a useful utopia: it is the standard against which more modest proposals can be measured, and it can be approached incrementally, beginning at the regional level, particularly in Europe. Rejecting the idea out of hand leaves only the alternatives of protectionism, capital controls, and nationalist withdrawal — blunt substitutes that are neither just nor effective.

Concretely, such a tax would be an annual levy on individual net wealth — financial assets, real estate, and business assets, net of debt — with progressive rates. One plausible schedule would apply 0 percent up to 1 million euros, 1 percent between 1 and 5 million euros, and 2 percent above 5 million euros, with perhaps a light rate of 0.1 percent on modest fortunes to bring them into the reporting system. Heavier rates — 5 or 10 percent — could apply to fortunes in the hundreds of millions or billions. Applied across the European Union, such a schedule would affect about 2.5 percent of the population and bring in revenue equal to roughly 2 percent of European GDP. This is a meaningful supplement to other revenues but, more importantly, it is a tool for regulating capitalism rather than a primary source of state finance.

The justifications for such a tax are twofold. The contributive justification responds to the fact that income is poorly defined for very wealthy individuals: a fortune of 10 billion euros earning 6–7 percent a year produces an economic income of 600–700 million euros, of which only a fraction is typically declared on income tax returns, since the rest accumulates inside family trusts and holding companies. Capital is a far better measure of contributive capacity at the top than declared income. The incentive justification is that a tax on wealth rewards dynamic use of capital and penalizes passive hoarding, encouraging assets to flow toward more productive uses. Both justifications support a capital tax as a complement, not a substitute, for the progressive income and inheritance taxes.

The critical precondition is financial transparency: automatic exchange of banking information across borders, including with tax havens, and the establishment of a kind of global cadaster that tracks who owns what. The US Foreign Account Tax Compliance Act (FATCA) of 2010, for all its gaps, is a concrete step in this direction; the European Union's various directives on savings taxation have been much more timid. Serious enforcement will require real sanctions on countries and banks that refuse to cooperate, such as tariffs on their exports. The goal is not trade war but the end of a situation in which economic integration allows the wealthiest to shop around for tax treatment, thereby siphoning off the fiscal base on which the social state depends. Without such coordination, individual European states acting alone (as Italy discovered in 2012) cannot effectively tax capital without triggering flight.

Chapter 16: The Question of the Public Debt

The rich countries of the world find themselves simultaneously extremely wealthy and fiscally distressed. Average public debt in the rich countries is now around 90 percent of GDP, the highest level since 1945, while average private wealth is around 600 percent of GDP. Europe, the richest region of the world in private terms, is also the region with the deepest public debt crisis — a striking paradox that points to the real question: public debt is not a problem of absolute wealth but of its distribution between public and private hands. Reducing a large public debt can be accomplished by three main methods, which can be combined in various proportions: an exceptional tax on private capital, inflation, or austerity.

An exceptional progressive tax on capital is the most just and efficient solution. A flat 15 percent levy on private European wealth would raise almost an entire year of national income, enough to pay off all outstanding public debt; a more realistic, progressive levy of 0 percent below 1 million euros, 10 percent between 1 and 5 million, and 20 percent above could reduce European debt by 20 points of GDP in one stroke. Such a tax is essentially a civilized version of a debt haircut: it targets ultimate owners of wealth in proportion to their capacity to pay, rather than whoever happens to hold the bonds when the crisis strikes. France used precisely such an exceptional progressive capital tax in 1945 to reduce its public debt, with rates reaching 25 percent.

Inflation has historically been the method most widely used to eliminate public debt, and it is much better than austerity; a modest rise in inflation from 2 to 5 percent a year reduces the real value of debt significantly while also nudging idle savers to invest in real assets. But inflation is a crude instrument that redistributes wealth unpredictably and often regressively: diversified portfolios at the top largely escape it, while small savers holding cash, savings accounts, and non-indexed bonds bear the brunt. Germany's twentieth-century experience, in which prices rose by a factor of 100 million in 1923, permanently shaped German aversion to this approach and helps explain why the European Central Bank, uniquely among major central banks, resists any loosening of its 2 percent target.

Austerity is the worst option, as the British nineteenth-century experience illustrates most vividly: after the Napoleonic wars, Britain honored its entire public debt of nearly 200 percent of GDP through a century of primary budget surpluses during which it spent more on debt service than on education — a policy in the interest of bondholders but arguably a factor in Britain's subsequent relative decline. Austerity at the current European level could similarly take decades and squeeze public investment in the areas most needed for the future. Central banks have played an indispensable role as lenders of last resort during the 2008 crisis, preventing a collapse comparable to the 1930s, but they cannot substitute for fiscal and democratic choices: they redistribute wealth quickly through loans, but they cannot decide who should bear the final cost of adjustment. That can only be done through democratic institutions equipped with the information, the tools, and the political legitimacy to tax capital and coordinate policy across borders — exactly what is currently missing in Europe.

Conclusion

The central contradiction of capitalism, as revealed by three centuries of data, can be stated in one line: r > g. When the private rate of return on capital durably exceeds the rate of growth of income and output, wealth accumulated in the past grows faster than wages and current output. The past devours the future. The entrepreneur inevitably tends to become a rentier, more and more dominant over those who own nothing but their labor. Once constituted, capital reproduces itself faster than output increases.

This contradiction is not the consequence of any market imperfection, and it will not be dissolved by freer markets or more perfect competition. On the contrary: the more perfect the capital market, the more likely r is to exceed g. The twentieth century, the one period in recent history when the net-of-tax return on capital was lower than the growth rate, was the product of wartime destruction, a unique political climate, exceptional growth, and a brief experiment with heavily progressive taxation. In the twenty-first century, as growth slows everywhere and tax competition erodes progressive taxation, there is every reason to expect r > g to reassert itself and wealth concentration to drift back toward historic highs, amplified by the higher returns available to the largest fortunes through scale effects in portfolio management.

The appropriate response is not to abolish private property, not to close borders, and not to hope that technology or competition will take care of the problem on their own. It is a progressive annual tax on capital, coordinated internationally and supported by automatic sharing of banking data. Such a tax can contain the unlimited growth of global wealth inequality while preserving competition, incentives, and economic openness. It can expose wealth to democratic scrutiny and provide the information necessary to regulate the global financial system. The difficulty is that it requires a level of international cooperation and regional political integration that does not yet exist. For the small countries of Europe in particular, national withdrawal offers only frustration; only regional political integration can provide effective regulation of twenty-first-century patrimonial capitalism.

Economics, finally, should be understood as a branch of the social sciences rather than as an autonomous mathematical discipline. The history of the distribution of wealth is always and everywhere a political history, shaped by wars, revolutions, institutions, and the prevailing sense of what is just. Refusing to engage with money, its measurement, and its history rarely serves the interests of the least well-off. Those who have a lot of it never fail to defend their interests; those who have little cannot afford to leave the study of capital to the specialists. The modest goal of this work has been to offer a few keys from the past for the choices that democratic societies will have to make in the century ahead — choices that have been, and will always be, too important to leave to economists alone.
