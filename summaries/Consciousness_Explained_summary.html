Consciousness Explained

by Daniel C. Dennett

Prelude: How Are Hallucinations Possible?

Dennett opens with the classic brain-in-a-vat thought experiment—the scenario in which evil scientists remove your brain and feed it fabricated sensory signals—but uses it for a purpose rarely attempted: not to generate skeptical doubt, but to uncover the astonishing complexity of the brain's perceptual machinery. He points out that reproducing a convincingly real sensory world for a disembodied brain would require providing the correct inputs to millions of nerve endings simultaneously, a task that is not merely "technically difficult" but practically impossible given what we know about the brain's architecture.

The deeper lesson is that the brain does not passively receive and display a world; it actively constructs one from fragmentary signals. Hallucinations—in which vivid perceptions occur with no corresponding external stimuli—demonstrate that the machinery of experience operates from the inside out. Dennett uses this to introduce heterophenomenology, his proposed scientific method for studying consciousness: treating subjects' verbal reports as data about a fictional world they inhabit, rather than as direct windows onto inner states. This methodology aims to take the "what it is like" of experience seriously without assuming that introspective reports are infallible or that they refer to non-physical entities.

Counterpoints

Critics such as David Chalmers argue that heterophenomenology sidesteps, rather than dissolves, what he calls the "hard problem of consciousness"—the question of why any physical processing is accompanied by subjective experience at all. By treating first-person reports as data about a fictional world, Dennett may be presupposing that phenomenal consciousness is nothing over and above functional and behavioral facts, which is precisely what is in dispute. Thomas Nagel similarly contends that no third-person empirical method can fully capture the subjective character of experience, the "what it is like" that defines consciousness.

Chapter 2: Explaining Consciousness — Pandora’s Box

Dennett frames human consciousness as "just about the last surviving mystery"—the one domain that still leaves sophisticated thinkers baffled in the way that cosmology, genetics, and evolutionary biology once did. He argues that every prior scientific revolution required replacing intuitive concepts with counterintuitive ones (disease was once a spiritual affliction; life once required a vital force), and that consciousness will be no different. Demystification is not destruction—it is the precondition for genuine understanding.

A central analogy runs through the chapter: love and money are phenomena that depend on their concepts in a way that diseases and earthquakes do not. If everyone forgot what money was, money would cease to exist as money (the engraved paper slips would remain, but not the institution). Love similarly has transformed as our concepts of it have changed. Dennett proposes that consciousness may be such a concept-dependent phenomenon—one whose nature is partly constituted by how we think about it—and that the scientific story we end up with may alter, but need not eliminate, what is genuinely valuable about conscious experience.

The chapter also confronts those who resist demystification on aesthetic or moral grounds—who fear that explaining consciousness would be a kind of vandalism, robbing life of its deepest mystery. Dennett acknowledges the loss but insists the gains in understanding—scientific, moral, and social—are worth it. He wants to show not only that consciousness can be explained, but that explaining it changes our picture of what we are in ways that are ultimately enriching.

Counterpoints

The analogy between consciousness and concept-dependent phenomena like money is philosophically contentious. John Searle argues that consciousness is a biological phenomenon—a product of specific neurobiological processes—and thus more like a disease or an earthquake than like money: it exists independently of what anyone thinks about it. If Dennett's analogy is wrong, then the "concept-dependent" framing may mislead us about the kind of investigation needed. Additionally, comparing demystification to "growing up out of romantic love" may be seen as begging the question: whether the gains outweigh the losses depends on what consciousness actually turns out to be.

Chapter 3: A Visit to the Phenomenological Garden

Dennett surveys the intellectual landscape of phenomenology—the philosophical tradition dedicated to describing the contents of conscious experience. He distinguishes the broad, scientific use of "phenomenology" (the descriptive study of any phenomenon before its theoretical explanation) from Husserl's Phenomenology (with a capital P), which sought to provide a new foundation for all knowledge through the disciplined introspective practice of the epoché, a bracketing of theoretical assumptions to reveal pure conscious objects called noemata. Like Impressionism in art and Introspectionism in psychology, Husserlian Phenomenology failed to establish a single, settled method—practitioners found irreconcilable differences where they expected consensus.

The chapter introduces the key term qualia—the qualitative, "what it is like" properties of experience: the redness of red, the painfulness of pain, the taste of coffee. Philosophers have treated qualia as the bedrock of consciousness, the phenomena any theory must ultimately account for. Dennett, however, begins here to place the concept under pressure. He notes the "phenomenological garden" is full of disputed species: people disagree whether mental imagery is genuinely pictorial, whether peripheral vision is detailed or sparse, whether we have a continuous "stream" of consciousness at all. These disputes suggest that our introspective access to our own phenomenology is less transparent than tradition assumes.

Counterpoints

Many philosophers—including Frank Jackson (via his famous Mary's Room thought experiment) and David Chalmers—insist that qualia are the most certain data point we have about consciousness. The failure of Husserlian Phenomenology to reach consensus about the structure of experience does not imply that phenomenal properties themselves are illusory or reducible. The debates Dennett cites (about mental imagery, peripheral detail) may be disputes about cognitive architecture that leave the existence of qualia untouched.

Chapter 4: A Method for Phenomenology

The central problem of phenomenology, Dennett argues, is that every author who writes about consciousness makes a first-person-plural presumption: the assumption that "you and I" share the same inner world and can straightforwardly confirm each other's introspective reports. Descartes wrote his Meditations expecting readers to replicate his inner observations; Locke called his method the "historical, plain method," as if simply noticing the obvious. Yet contradiction and controversy have bedeviled these attempts from the beginning. Either we are not as alike as we assume, or introspection is less reliable than tradition holds.

Dennett proposes heterophenomenology as the corrective method. Rather than either accepting subjects' reports as infallible records of inner states, or dismissing them as unreliable noise, heterophenomenology treats subjects as intentional systems—entities whose behavior is best predicted by attributing beliefs, desires, and experiences to them. A subject's reports are taken as evidence for a heterophenomenological world: the world as it seems to that subject, constructed from the outside by an objective theorist. This world may or may not accurately reflect the subject's actual neural states, but it is data. The method captures first-person authority without presupposing infallibility, and opens consciousness to the same kind of third-person scientific scrutiny as any other natural phenomenon.

Counterpoints

Thomas Nagel and Colin McGinn argue that heterophenomenology, however useful as a scientific heuristic, fundamentally cannot account for the subjective feel of experience. Treating reports as data about a "fictional world" risks converting a first-person phenomenon into a purely third-person object of study, thereby bypassing the very thing that needs explaining—the inner light of consciousness itself. Ned Block's distinction between access consciousness (information available for reasoning and report) and phenomenal consciousness (the qualitative feel) suggests heterophenomenology may track the former while leaving the latter entirely unaddressed.

Chapter 5: Multiple Drafts versus the Cartesian Theater

Dennett identifies the fundamental architectural assumption underlying most theories of mind: the idea of a Cartesian Theater—a central place in the brain where sensory information converges to be "presented" to the conscious self. This metaphor, he argues, is not just wrong but incoherent. There is no single brain region that serves as a grand central station for perception; information arrives at different brain areas at different times, is processed in parallel by specialist systems, and is never gathered into one place for a unified showing.

The replacement Dennett proposes is the Multiple Drafts model. All conscious experience consists of parallel, multitrack processes of interpretation and elaboration—what he calls "editorial revisions"—that occur continuously throughout the brain. There is no single moment at which a percept becomes "official"; rather, multiple drafts of the narrative are always in play, and which one achieves functional prominence depends on timing, attention, and subsequent processing. The model is illustrated by the phi phenomenon (apparent motion between two spatially separated flashes) and especially color phi, in which subjects report seeing a moving dot change color between two positions—even though the brain could not have "known" the second color before the first dot had even disappeared. The apparent temporal impossibility dissolves once we abandon the idea that consciousness is a live feed to an inner theater.

The Multiple Drafts model also reinterprets the question "when did consciousness occur?" as a bad question: there is no single moment of awareness, only an ongoing revision process. Dennett introduces the key distinction between "Stalinesque" and "Orwellian" explanations of perceptual anomalies—whether the brain falsifies the experience before it is "shown," or revises the memory of it after the fact. He argues the distinction has no empirical purchase: from the subject's perspective, both produce the same result, and there is no "fact of the matter" about which happened.

Counterpoints

Neuroscientists such as Francis Crick and Christof Koch accepted that no single neuron is the "seat of consciousness," but proposed instead that consciousness correlates with a specific coalitional neural process—a "neural correlate of consciousness" (NCC)—that does functionally play the role of a final integrating step. Critics argue that Dennett's rejection of the Cartesian Theater conflates the metaphysical claim (a Cartesian homunculus) with the functional claim (a brain system that integrates information for reportable, unified experience). The latter may be defensible even if the former is not. Bernard Baars's Global Workspace Theory posits exactly such an integration mechanism without invoking a homunculus.

Chapter 6: Time and Experience

This chapter deepens the Multiple Drafts model by working through a series of notoriously puzzling experiments involving temporal anomalies in perception. The color phi phenomenon, the cutaneous rabbit (in which evenly timed taps on the arm are perceived as a rabbit hopping along the skin), and experiments on backward masking all suggest that consciousness does not report events as they occur in real time, but rather constructs a post-hoc narrative. In the cutaneous rabbit, subjects report feeling a tap between two stimulated positions on the arm—even though the brain receives the second-position signal only after the first position has already been stimulated. The brain is, in effect, "predicting" the intervening events and inserting them into the experiential record.

Dennett uses these phenomena to attack what he calls "the Cartesian materialist's" assumption: that there must be a moment at which the brain "settles" on a conscious verdict, after which subsequent neural events are merely post-experiential. He argues this leads to paradoxes of apparent retrocausation (the brain seems to know the future) that dissolve once we accept that there is no such privileged moment. The experiments are also used to illustrate the Stalinesque/Orwellian distinction again: since there is no "cerebroscope" that could independently verify which interpretation is correct, the distinction carries no explanatory weight. What matters is the functional organization that produces the behavioral and verbal reports, not a ghost-image of experience floating free of that organization.

Counterpoints

Philosopher Paul Churchland accepts much of Dennett's empirical story but argues that eliminating the Cartesian Theater does not dissolve the explanatory gap—it merely relocates it. Other critics, notably Max Velmans, argue that Dennett's treatment of backward masking and the cutaneous rabbit overgeneralizes: the existence of post-hoc revisions in memory does not prove there are no genuine prior phenomenal states that get revised. The claim that all experience is retrospective construction, rather than something that is also partially concurrent, remains empirically contested.

Chapter 7: The Evolution of Consciousness

Dennett steps back from the brain to take an evolutionary perspective on consciousness. Using Valentino Braitenberg's Vehicles as inspiration—thought experiments that build from the simplest stimulus-response machines up to entities with something like memory, fear, and curiosity—Dennett argues that consciousness is not a single thing that either exists or does not, but rather a family of capacities that accumulated over evolutionary time through a series of individually useful steps. The key transitions include the evolution of sensory discrimination, representation (internal states that stand for external conditions), and finally self-representation and meta-cognition.

A crucial concept introduced here is the Joycean machine—named for the novelist's stream-of-consciousness technique. Dennett proposes that human consciousness is best understood as a virtual machine running on the hardware of the brain: a software layer of habits, memes, and cultural practices that organizes the parallel specialist brain circuits into something resembling a serial narrative. This virtual machine was not designed from scratch; it was assembled opportunistically from pre-existing specialist circuits by the twin processes of genetic evolution and cultural evolution (meme transmission).

Dennett also introduces Richard Dawkins's concept of memes—units of cultural information that replicate, vary, and are selected in minds as genes are in organisms. Memes—words, tunes, catchphrases, concepts—literally colonize brains, shaping their neural structure and thereby shaping the kind of consciousness those brains can support. The human mind is not just a brain with some culture painted on top; it is a brain that has been redesigned by culture to host and transmit memes, and this symbiosis is part of what makes human consciousness so distinctive.

Counterpoints

The memetics framework has been criticized heavily by biologists and cognitive scientists. David Hull and others argue that memes lack the fidelity, longevity, and fecundity required for Darwinian evolution; cultural transmission involves active reconstruction rather than copying, making the replication analogy misleading. On the deeper evolutionary narrative: Derek Denton, Antonio Damasio, and others argue that Dennett's account underemphasizes the role of bodily feeling and homeostatic emotion in the evolution of consciousness, focusing too exclusively on cognitive and linguistic capacities.

Chapter 8: How Words Do Things with Us

This chapter investigates the relationship between language and consciousness—specifically, how the acquisition of language transforms the brain's processing architecture. Dennett quotes Marx and Nietzsche to the effect that consciousness arises from the necessity of communication, and adds Helen Keller's own testimony: before language, she reports, she "lived in a world that was a no-world," with no power of thought, no comparison of mental states. Language does not merely label a pre-existing inner world; it constitutes that world in crucial ways.

Dennett focuses on the concept of the Central Meaner—the supposed inner agent who "means" all the things we say, who intends our words and owns our thoughts. Building on psycholinguist Willem Levelt's model of speech production, he argues that the architecture of language production does not require or support the existence of such a Boss. The Conceptualizer in Levelt's model is a "reification in need of further explanation"—a placeholder for the very problem the model is supposed to solve. The Joycean machine, with its shifting coalitions of specialist circuits, handles the executive functions of communication without any central author pulling the strings.

The chapter also addresses the role of inner speech in consciousness. Dennett proposes that much of what we call "thinking" is a kind of internalized version of interpersonal communication—we talk to ourselves in the way we would talk to others, and this self-stimulation loop helps organize, stabilize, and extend the brain's computational powers. The capacity for inner speech, acquired through cultural learning, effectively gives the brain a powerful new tool for self-management—one that is not innate but is so ubiquitous in humans that it feels like a native feature of mind.

Counterpoints

Researchers in animal cognition—including Josep Call and Michael Tomasello—document sophisticated forms of planning, self-recognition, and problem-solving in non-human primates that appear to occur without language, suggesting that at least some hallmarks of consciousness do not depend on the linguistic virtual machine Dennett describes. The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis (linguistic relativity) in its strong form has been largely rejected: language shapes thought, but does not create it from nothing. Critics worry that Dennett's language-centric account of consciousness may be too parochial—accurate for linguistically saturated human minds but poorly generalizable to infants, animals, or the severely language-impaired.

Chapter 9: The Architecture of the Human Mind

Dennett pauses to take stock of the Multiple Drafts model and defend it against the charge of incoherence. The model describes a brain organized as a "pandemonium" of parallel specialist processes—each doing its own thing, competing and cooperating, with no permanent boss. Yet the brain does not produce behavioral chaos; it produces remarkably coherent, purposeful action. Dennett explains this by distinguishing between the underlying parallel hardware and the emergent virtual serial machine—a von-Neumann-like sequential processing structure that arises from the dynamics of the competing specialists, not from any single controlling circuit.

The chapter surveys contributions from psychology, neurobiology, and Artificial Intelligence that support this picture. Dennett engages with models from Jerry Fodor's modularity thesis, Marvin Minsky's "society of mind," and Michael Gazzaniga's split-brain research, arguing that each captures part of the picture. The brain's basic specialists are evolutionary inheritances shared with other animals (face recognition, threat detection, motor planning), but they have been recruited into new roles—especially for reading, writing, and language—that their original design did not anticipate. The result is not bedlam because cultural microhabits of thought impose structured tendencies on the chaos, tendencies that are themselves partly the product of selection (both cultural and biological).

Counterpoints

Jerry Fodor, whose modularity thesis Dennett draws on, rejected the overall Multiple Drafts architecture on the grounds that central cognition—the integration of modules for reasoning and belief fixation—is not modular and cannot be explained by competitive parallel pandemonium. Fodor famously argued that "global" cognitive processes remain deeply mysterious precisely because there is no good computational story for how local modules give rise to centrally coherent thought. The pandemonium model, critics argue, is good for explaining perceptual preprocessing but struggles to account for the unity and coherence of deliberate reasoning.

Chapter 10: Show and Tell

Part III opens with a challenge from cognitive science that might appear to rehabilitate the Cartesian Theater: mental imagery research. Roger Shepard's classic mental rotation experiments showed that subjects take longer to judge whether two figures are the same shape the further apart their orientations—as if they were literally rotating an image in their minds. Stephen Kosslyn extended this work into a comprehensive model of a quasi-pictorial visual buffer in the brain—an internal CRT screen on which images are displayed and manipulated by a homunculus-like viewer. Dennett argues this interpretation is a seductive but ultimately mistaken extension of a metaphor.

The author reconstructs what a genuine pictorial representation in the brain would require: actual filling-in, pixel by pixel, with properties analogous to the colors and shapes of physical images. He argues the brain does not work this way. Instead, it uses sparse, functionally adequate representations—more like recipes for constructing images than like pictures themselves. When a subject "sees" a mental image in vivid detail, this does not require that all that detail be explicitly encoded in a pixel-like structure; it requires only that the relevant information be available on demand when the subject interrogates it. The apparent richness of mental imagery is, in part, a user illusion—the same kind of illusion that makes a computer desktop seem to contain pictures and documents, when in fact it contains only data structures and processes.

Counterpoints

Stephen Kosslyn and Zenon Pylyshyn engaged in a decades-long debate over the nature of mental imagery, with Kosslyn defending the quasi-pictorial account and Pylyshyn arguing for a fully propositional (non-pictorial) view. Subsequent neuroimaging work by Kosslyn and others has provided some empirical support for a genuinely topographic representation in the primary visual cortex during imagery tasks—suggesting that imagery may be more "pictorial" in a literal neural sense than Dennett allows. Dennett's claim that the apparent richness of imagery is a user illusion remains influential but contested among cognitive neuroscientists.

Chapter 11: Dismantling the Witness Protection Program

This chapter directly attacks the intuition at the heart of the Cartesian Theater: the idea that conscious experience requires a Witness—an inner observer for whom the show is put on, and whose presence is what makes processing "conscious" rather than merely mechanical. Dennett argues this intuition, while deeply felt, is the product of cognitive illusions that the Multiple Drafts model can expose and explain. The key empirical case is blindsight: patients with damage to their primary visual cortex who, when asked to guess the location or identity of visual stimuli they claim not to see, perform significantly above chance. They process visual information without having access to it in the normal way—a condition Dennett calls "partial zombiehood."

Blindsight demonstrates that unconscious visual processing can be behaviorally effective without any accompanying phenomenal experience, while also showing that what we call "conscious seeing" involves something additional to mere information processing. Dennett uses this to argue not that there is a mysterious extra ingredient called consciousness, but that the normal state involves multiple access routes to a wider range of cognitive systems—routes that blindsight patients have lost. Consciousness, on this account, is not a spotlight that illuminates otherwise dark processing; it is a functional configuration in which information is globally available to multiple cognitive systems simultaneously.

The chapter also demolishes the philosophical notion of "raw feels" or sense data—the idea that experience consists of privately held qualitative items that stand between the world and the mind's judgment. These hypothetical raw feels are the props that give the Witness something to witness. Without them, the need for a central observer dissolves.

Counterpoints

Ned Block argues that blindsight, far from dissolving the hard problem, actually illustrates the distinction between access consciousness and phenomenal consciousness. Blindsight patients lack phenomenal consciousness for stimuli they can access functionally—which is exactly what you would expect if phenomenal consciousness is something over and above informational access. David Chalmers agrees: the very fact that blindsight is experienced as strange and incomplete by patients (they know something is missing) suggests that phenomenal consciousness is not simply global informational availability but something else that global availability normally accompanies.

Chapter 12: Qualia Disqualified

Dennett tackles the most resistant philosophical concept in the consciousness literature: qualia. He describes the state of the literature as a "tormented snarl" of thought experiments and in-jokes, and proposes to start over rather than unsnarl it. His target is the traditional picture: qualia are intrinsic (they have their nature independently of any relational or functional context), ineffable (they cannot be fully described in any public language), private (only their subject can know them directly), and directly apprehensible (consciousness of a quale is self-certifying). He argues that each of these properties, under scrutiny, is either incoherent or empirically unsupported.

The centerpiece argument involves color experience and inverted qualia. The classic thought experiment: could it be that what you call "seeing red" has the subjective character that I associate with "seeing green," even though our behavioral responses to colors are identical? Dennett argues this scenario is not merely unconfirmable but incoherent. The very idea of a "private" shade of color that is entirely detached from all functional and dispositional properties is incoherent, because there would be no fact of the matter distinguishing "red inverted to green" from a situation in which all the dispositional facts are the same. The qualia that philosophers want to exist—the intrinsic "what it's like" stripped of all functional clothing—would, if they existed, be causally inert and therefore explanatorily irrelevant.

Dennett replaces qualia with what subjects actually have: complex dispositional states of the brain that dispose them to make certain discriminations, associations, and reports. These are real, they are different from person to person, and they are what subjects are actually referring to when they use introspective language, even though they mistakenly believe they are referring to private intrinsic properties.

Counterpoints

This is among the most contested chapters in the book. Frank Jackson's Mary's Room thought experiment presents the intuition most directly: Mary, a neuroscientist who has lived her whole life in a black-and-white room, knows all the physical facts about color vision. When she first sees a red tomato, she learns something new—what red looks like. If she learns something new, qualia cannot be reducible to functional/dispositional facts. David Chalmers, Sydney Shoemaker, and Joseph Levine have all defended versions of qualia realism against Dennett's eliminativist challenge. The "inverted spectrum" scenario, even if not strictly coherent in Dennett's framework, is taken by most philosophers as a powerful intuition pump that places the burden of proof on the functionalist to explain it away, not merely to assert its incoherence.

Chapter 13: The Reality of Selves

Having dismantled the Cartesian Theater and the traditional account of qualia, Dennett turns to the final component of the traditional picture: the self. He surveys the two most common responses to the problem of the self: the dualist claim that the self is a non-physical soul, and the eliminativist claim that the self is nothing at all, a philosophical fiction. Dennett proposes a middle path: the self is real, but it is a center of narrative gravity—an abstraction, like a center of mass, that is defined by the stories we tell about ourselves and that has genuine predictive and explanatory power without corresponding to any single physical object.

The self is "spun" by the brain's narrative machinery: we are inveterate storytellers, and the protagonist of the stories we tell about ourselves—the character who has the memories, holds the beliefs, makes the decisions—is the self. This character is not a fiction in the sense of being false; it is a fiction in the sense of being an abstraction with causal efficacy, the way the center of a hurricane is "real" but cannot be pointed to. Evolution produced the narrative-spinning capacity because organisms that model themselves—that can represent their own past and project their own future—are better able to plan and adapt. The self is thus both an evolutionary product and a cultural elaboration.

The chapter also addresses the gradations of self across the animal kingdom (do dogs have selves? lobsters?), drawing the conclusion that selfhood admits of degrees and that many animals have rudimentary selves without having the full narrative self that language makes possible for humans. The self, like consciousness generally, is not a binary property but a family of capacities that exist along a continuum.

Counterpoints

Antonio Damasio argues in Descartes' Error and subsequent work that the self has a genuinely biological substrate in the brain's interoceptive systems—a "proto-self" and "core self" rooted in the body's homeostatic regulation—that precedes and grounds the narrative self Dennett describes. On Damasio's account, Dennett's narrative gravity is the highest, most culturally elaborated layer of selfhood, built on a foundation of bodily self-representation that is not primarily linguistic. Buddhist philosophers and phenomenologists like Dan Zahavi also challenge the purely narrative account, arguing for a "minimal self"—a pre-reflective sense of ownership of experience—that is not constituted by stories.

Chapter 14: Consciousness Imagined

The final chapter draws out the most provocative implication of the Multiple Drafts model: if consciousness is the activity of a virtual machine running on a biological brain, then a suitably programmed non-biological system—a robot or computer—could in principle be conscious. Dennett anticipates two responses: those who find this obvious (we are machines, after all, and we are conscious) and those who find it a reductio ad absurdum of the whole theory. He rebuts the second group by distinguishing "can't imagine a conscious robot" from "can't imagine how a robot could be conscious"—the latter is the real challenge, and the rest of the book has been an attempt to address it.

Dennett engages with John Searle's Chinese Room argument—the thought experiment in which a person who knows no Chinese manipulates symbols according to rules and produces correct Chinese outputs, apparently without understanding anything. Searle concludes that syntax (symbol manipulation) is insufficient for semantics (meaning), and that therefore no computer program could ever be conscious or intentional. Dennett counters that the Chinese Room thought experiment relies on our inability to imagine the full complexity of the system: if we take the entire system (including all the lookup tables, the person, the rules) as the unit of analysis rather than just the person inside, the "understanding" may well be present at the system level even if no single component understands.

The book closes with a defense of heterophenomenology as the appropriate stance toward both human and machine consciousness: we attribute consciousness where the intentional stance is indispensable for prediction and explanation, and we remain agnostic about "what it is really like inside" until we have better empirical tools. Dennett acknowledges the theory is incomplete—it is a sketch of where a full scientific account must go, not the full account itself—but insists that the sketch is correct in its broad strokes: there is no Cartesian Theater, there are only Multiple Drafts; there is no central homunculus, only a virtual machine; there are no intrinsic qualia, only complex dispositional states; and there is no soul, only a center of narrative gravity.

Counterpoints

John Searle has maintained that the "systems reply" to the Chinese Room (Dennett's preferred response) conflates syntax with semantics at a higher level of abstraction without explaining how meaning actually arises. The reply, Searle argues, merely asserts that the whole system understands without providing any account of why complex symbol manipulation should give rise to genuine intentionality. Roger Penrose argues from mathematical logic (Gödel's incompleteness theorems) that human consciousness involves non-computational processes, making any Turing-machine model of consciousness fundamentally inadequate. More broadly, critics note that Dennett's closing claim—that all the traditional ingredients of consciousness have been dissolved—will seem to many readers like a triumphant announcement that the house has been tidied while the furniture was quietly removed.
