Guns, Germs, and Steel

The Fates of Human Societies

Jared Diamond

Prologue: Yali's Question

Diamond opens with a conversation he had in 1972 with a New Guinean politician named Yali, who asked him a deceptively simple question: "Why is it that you white people developed so much cargo and brought it to New Guinea, but we black people had little cargo of our own?" This question—why did wealth and power become distributed as they are, rather than in some other way—becomes the central inquiry of the book. Diamond rejects racist biological explanations, arguing that New Guineans are likely more intelligent on average than Westerners, due to harsher natural selection for intelligence in small-scale societies and the developmental advantages of active childhoods.

The author surveys and dismisses several common explanations: cold-climate theories (which fail because major innovations arose in warm regions), irrigation-civilization theories (irrigation followed rather than preceded political centralization), and proximate-cause explanations (guns, germs, and steel are themselves things that need explaining). Diamond's thesis is encapsulated in one sentence: "History followed different courses for different peoples because of differences among peoples' environments, not because of biological differences among peoples themselves."

Counterpoints

Critics like James Blaut and Victor Davis Hanson have argued that Diamond's environmental determinism underestimates the role of cultural factors—political institutions, religious values, and individual agency—in shaping history. Others note that Diamond's argument about New Guinean intelligence, while well-intentioned, relies on anecdotal evidence and speculative evolutionary reasoning.

Chapter 1: Up to the Starting Line

Diamond surveys human evolution and the peopling of the continents from 7 million years ago through 11,000 B.C. He traces the spread of Homo erectus out of Africa around 1.8 million years ago, the emergence of modern humans, and the Great Leap Forward around 50,000 years ago—when humans first produced art, sophisticated tools, and complex culture. The colonization of Australia/New Guinea (around 40,000 years ago) required watercraft and was followed by the extinction of large native animals (megafauna), likely caused by human hunting.

The Americas were colonized last, probably around 11,000 B.C. via the Bering land bridge, by Clovis hunters who spread rapidly southward. As in Australia, the arrival of humans coincided with mass extinctions of large mammals—mammoths, giant ground sloths, horses, and camels—which eliminated potential candidates for later domestication. Diamond concludes that as of 11,000 B.C., no continent had an obvious head start, making the subsequent divergence of societies all the more puzzling.

Counterpoints

The megafauna extinction debate remains unresolved. Some researchers, including Donald Grayson, argue that climate change at the end of the Ice Age played a larger role than human hunting. The pre-Clovis debate has also shifted since Diamond wrote: sites like Monte Verde in Chile are now widely accepted as pre-dating Clovis, suggesting the Americas may have been populated earlier than Diamond's preferred timeline.

Chapter 2: A Natural Experiment of History

Diamond uses Polynesian societies as a "natural experiment" to demonstrate how environments shape human societies. All Polynesian peoples descended from a common ancestral population that colonized Pacific islands starting around 1200 B.C. Yet within centuries, vastly different societies emerged—from the hunter-gatherers of the Chatham Islands (the Moriori) to the proto-empires of Hawaii and Tonga.

The chapter's most vivid example is the 1835 Maori conquest of the Moriori. Both groups were Polynesian, but the Maori had developed agriculture, dense populations, military technology, and political hierarchy on resource-rich New Zealand, while the Moriori had reverted to a peaceful hunter-gatherer lifestyle on the small, cold Chatham Islands. When 900 armed Maori invaded, they slaughtered and enslaved the Moriori effortlessly. Diamond shows that the key variables—island climate, area, geological type, isolation, and marine resources—determined everything from population density to political complexity.

Chapter 3: Collision at Cajamarca

Diamond recounts the dramatic capture of the Inca emperor Atahuallpa by Francisco Pizarro's 168 Spanish soldiers at Cajamarca in 1532. Despite being outnumbered 500 to 1, Pizarro captured Atahuallpa, killed thousands of Incas, and suffered no Spanish casualties. The proximate factors behind this lopsided outcome were: steel weapons and armor, horses, guns, epidemic diseases (smallpox had already killed the previous emperor and divided the empire in civil war), maritime technology, centralized political organization, and writing.

Diamond emphasizes that literacy gave the Spanish critical advantages: Pizarro modeled his ambush on Cortés's conquest of the Aztecs, which he knew about through written accounts. Atahuallpa, lacking writing and any knowledge of other civilizations, walked into a transparent trap. The chapter establishes the proximate causes of European conquest but frames the book's real question: why did these advantages lie with Europeans rather than with Native Americans?

Chapter 4: Farmer Power

Diamond argues that food production—agriculture and animal husbandry—was the single most important ultimate cause behind the development of guns, germs, and steel. Farming supports 10 to 100 times more people per acre than hunting-gathering, creating the population densities needed for complex societies. Food surpluses enabled full-time specialists: kings, bureaucrats, soldiers, priests, metalworkers, and scribes.

Domestic animals provided not only meat and milk but also fertilizer, plow traction, land transport, military vehicles (horses and camels), and—crucially—epidemic diseases that evolved from animal germs and devastated populations with no prior exposure. The chapter maps the causal chain from food production through population density, political centralization, technology, and military power to the outcomes observed in historical conquests.

Chapter 5: History's Haves and Have-Nots

Diamond identifies the few places where food production arose independently: the Fertile Crescent, China, Mesoamerica, the Andes/Amazon, and the eastern United States, with possible additional centers in Africa's Sahel, tropical West Africa, Ethiopia, and New Guinea. He shows that the Fertile Crescent had the earliest definite dates (around 8500 B.C.) and explains the methods archaeologists use—radiocarbon dating, mapping of wild ancestor ranges, genetic analysis—to determine where and when domestication occurred.

In some regions, food production arrived through the diffusion of crops and livestock from these centers (as in Egypt and Europe). In others, arriving food producers replaced indigenous hunter-gatherers entirely (as in Australia and the Americas in recent centuries). Diamond sets up the key puzzle: why did food production arise independently in some areas much earlier than others, and why not at all in some ecologically suitable regions?

Chapter 6: To Farm or Not to Farm

Diamond challenges the assumption that adopting farming was an obvious improvement. Early farmers were often smaller, less well-nourished, more disease-ridden, and shorter-lived than the hunter-gatherers they replaced. The transition was not a conscious invention but an unconscious, gradual process driven by countless individual decisions about how to allocate time and effort. Five factors drove the shift: declining wild food availability, increasing availability of domesticable wild species, development of food production technologies, rising population density making hunting-gathering less viable, and competitive advantages of food producers over neighbors.

Diamond emphasizes that the shift was neither sudden nor uniform. There was no sharp divide between hunter-gatherers and farmers; many societies combined both strategies for millennia. The transition happened independently in different parts of the world at very different times, and some societies in contact with farmers deliberately chose not to adopt farming.

Chapter 7: How to Make an Almond

Diamond explains how wild plants were unconsciously transformed into crops. Early humans didn't set out to domesticate plants; instead, by gathering the biggest, sweetest, or most accessible wild plants and discarding their seeds near human camps, they unknowingly selected for traits useful to humans. Key changes included larger seeds and fruits, loss of bitterness (as in almonds, where a single gene mutation eliminates cyanide), loss of natural seed-dispersal mechanisms (non-shattering wheat stalks, non-popping pea pods), and changes in reproductive biology (self-pollinating varieties that breed true).

The chapter traces the sequence of domestication in the Fertile Crescent: first cereals and pulses (easy, fast-growing annuals), then fruit trees grown from cuttings (olives, grapes, figs), and finally difficult fruit trees requiring grafting (apples, pears, cherries). Diamond explains why some apparently valuable wild plants—like oak trees (whose bitterness is controlled by many genes rather than one) and strawberries (which birds disperse more effectively than humans)—resisted domestication for millennia.

Chapter 8: Apples or Indians

Diamond asks why the Fertile Crescent became the earliest and most productive center of plant domestication. He identifies several advantages: a Mediterranean climate that favors annual plants investing heavily in large, storable seeds; an unusually high concentration of the world's 56 largest-seeded wild grasses (32 of 56 occur in the Fertile Crescent's Mediterranean zone, versus 1 in California, 2 in South America); a high percentage of self-pollinating plants (convenient for early farmers); and a wide range of altitudes providing diverse habitats and staggered harvest seasons.

Diamond contrasts this with New Guinea (which lacked cereals and large-seeded grasses, relying instead on root crops like taro) and the eastern United States (whose indigenous domesticates like sunflower and goosefoot were nutritionally inferior to Fertile Crescent cereals). He also compares the easy domestication of wheat and barley with the difficulty of developing corn from teosinte—its wild ancestor looked completely different and required thousands of years of selection to become productive.

Counterpoints

Andrew Sherratt and others have argued that Diamond overstates the Fertile Crescent's advantages and underestimates the sophistication of agricultural systems in other regions. The development of rice paddies in China and raised-field agriculture in the Americas were extraordinary achievements that rivaled anything in the Fertile Crescent. Dorian Fuller has also shown that independent domestication was more widespread than Diamond suggests.

Chapter 9: Zebras, Unhappy Marriages, and the Anna Karenina Principle

Diamond applies the Anna Karenina principle—"Domesticable animals are all alike; every undomesticable animal is undomesticable in its own way"—to explain why only 14 large mammal species were ever domesticated out of 148 candidates. The Major Five (cow, sheep, goat, pig, horse) all originated in Eurasia. For an animal to be domesticable, it must satisfy all of six criteria: suitable diet, fast growth rate, ability to breed in captivity, tractable disposition, resistance to panic, and a social dominance hierarchy that humans can exploit.

Most large mammals fail on at least one criterion. Zebras are vicious and unpredictable biters. Hippos and grizzly bears are too dangerous. Gazelles and deer panic in enclosures. Cheetahs won't breed in captivity. Africa and the Americas had far fewer domesticable species than Eurasia, which explains why those continents never developed the cavalry, plowing, and epidemic diseases that gave Eurasians decisive advantages.

Counterpoints

Juliet Clutton-Brock and other domestication scholars argue that Diamond's criteria are somewhat circular—we know which animals were domesticable because they were domesticated. Modern experiments have shown that many "undomesticable" species (including foxes, in the famous Belyaev experiment in Russia) can be domesticated with sustained selective breeding. The question may be less about inherent suitability and more about whether early peoples had sufficient motivation and opportunity.

Chapter 10: Spacious Skies and Tilted Axes

Diamond presents one of his most influential arguments: that the orientation of continental axes profoundly affected the spread of food production. Eurasia's major axis runs east-west, meaning crops and livestock could spread thousands of miles along similar latitudes with similar climates, day lengths, and seasons. The Fertile Crescent's crops spread readily to Europe, Egypt, and the Indus Valley at roughly 0.7 miles per year. In contrast, the Americas and Africa have north-south axes, forcing crops to cross different climate zones, which dramatically slowed or blocked their spread.

This explains why the llama was never transferred from the Andes to Mesoamerica (blocked by tropical lowlands), why Mesoamerican corn took thousands of years to become productive in the eastern United States (requiring adaptation to different day lengths), and why the Sahara and tropical forests blocked the spread of Fertile Crescent crops into sub-Saharan Africa. Diamond shows that the same axis effect operated for the spread of writing, wheels, and other technologies.

Counterpoints

Ricardo Duchesne and others have pointed out that the axis theory cannot explain differences within Eurasia—for instance, why Europe rather than China or India led the way to industrialization, given that all three lie along the same east-west axis. Diamond acknowledges this limitation but defers it to the Epilogue. Peter Bellwood also notes that north-south spread was not always blocked: the Austronesian expansion moved rapidly through many latitudes.

Chapter 11: Lethal Gift of Livestock

Diamond explains how epidemic diseases—smallpox, measles, flu, tuberculosis, plague—evolved from diseases of domestic animals and devastated populations that lacked prior exposure. These "crowd diseases" require large, dense populations to sustain themselves and could only evolve in agricultural societies with livestock. The chapter traces how microbes spread (through coughing, diarrhea, insect bites, sexual contact) and why they sometimes kill their hosts (an unintended by-product of strategies to maximize transmission).

The key insight is that Eurasians, who had lived with domestic animals for millennia, evolved partial immunity to these diseases, while peoples of the Americas, Australia, and Pacific islands had no such exposure. When Europeans arrived, their germs killed an estimated 95 percent of Native Americans—far more than European weapons ever did. Conversely, the Americas had few infectious diseases to transmit back, because the Americas had almost no domestic animals from which human diseases could evolve.

Counterpoints

David Jones and others have argued that Diamond oversimplifies the role of disease in the Americas. While epidemic diseases were devastating, their impact was compounded by colonial violence, forced labor, displacement, and social disruption. Some scholars argue the 95% mortality figure is exaggerated and that pre-contact population estimates are uncertain. Paul Kelton has shown that disease often spread along trade routes disrupted by colonial incursions rather than simply radiating naturally.

Chapter 12: Blueprints and Borrowed Letters

Diamond examines the origins and spread of writing, which arose independently only a few times in human history: Sumerian cuneiform (before 3000 B.C.), Mesoamerican scripts (before 600 B.C.), and possibly Chinese and Egyptian writing. All other writing systems were either borrowed directly or inspired by contact with existing systems. Diamond distinguishes three strategies: alphabets (one sign per sound), logograms (one sign per word), and syllabaries (one sign per syllable).

The chapter traces how Sumerian cuneiform evolved from clay accounting tokens, and how writing spread through blueprint copying (directly adapting an existing system) and idea diffusion (being inspired by the concept of writing to create a new system). Diamond argues that writing's development was closely tied to food production: the earliest writing arose in the earliest centers of agriculture, because only dense, complex societies with bureaucratic needs generated the demand and surplus labor to invent and maintain writing systems.

Chapter 13: Necessity's Mother

Diamond challenges the popular view that technology progresses through heroic invention by lone geniuses. Instead, he argues that most inventions arise from tinkering and curiosity, with applications discovered only later—making the adage "necessity is the mother of invention" backwards. The phonograph, automobile, and internal combustion engine were all invented before anyone knew what to do with them. Diamond proposes that "invention is the mother of necessity": once a device exists, society finds uses for it.

Technology develops cumulatively and autocatalytically, not in isolated leaps. Each invention builds on predecessors and stimulates further innovations. This means the key factor determining a society's technology is not the brilliance of its individual members but its population size, connectivity to other societies, and access to accumulated knowledge. Societies in large, well-connected landmasses (like Eurasia) accumulated technology faster than isolated or small populations.

Counterpoints

Joel Mokyr and others have argued that Diamond underestimates the role of institutional factors in technological development. Europe's patent systems, competitive state system, and culture of open inquiry were crucial for the Scientific and Industrial Revolutions. China had large populations and extensive trade networks but its centralized government sometimes actively suppressed innovation (as with the Ming Dynasty's ban on oceangoing ships).

Chapter 14: From Egalitarianism to Kleptocracy

Diamond traces the evolution of political organization from small bands (dozens of people, egalitarian, no formal leadership) to tribes (hundreds, with informal leaders), chiefdoms (thousands, with hereditary chiefs and social stratification), and finally states (over 50,000, with centralized bureaucracy, taxation, and monopoly on force). Each transition was driven by increasing population density, which was in turn driven by food production.

Diamond poses the provocative question of why people tolerate kleptocratic elites who extract wealth from commoners. He identifies four strategies elites use to maintain power: disarming the populace and arming the elite; redistributing tribute in popular ways; maintaining order and resolving conflicts; and constructing an ideology or religion that justifies the elite's position. The chapter connects political complexity to military advantage: states can mobilize standing armies, build ships, and organize wars of conquest in ways that bands and tribes cannot.

Counterpoints

James Scott (Against the Grain) has argued that early states were often fragile, parasitic, and actively resisted by populations who preferred the freedom of non-state life. The transition from egalitarian to hierarchical societies may not have been as inevitable or desirable as Diamond implies. David Graeber and David Wengrow (The Dawn of Everything) have challenged the entire band-tribe-chiefdom-state progression as an oversimplification that ignores the enormous political creativity of pre-state societies.

Chapter 15: Yali's People

Diamond applies his framework to Australia and New Guinea. Aboriginal Australians remained hunter-gatherers with the world's simplest technologies, not because of any intellectual deficiency, but because Australia lacked domesticable plants and animals, had poor soils, unpredictable rainfall, and was isolated from the agricultural innovations of Asia. New Guinea, by contrast, independently developed agriculture (based on root crops like taro and yams) but lacked cereals, large domestic animals, and metal ores, limiting its political and technological development.

The chapter shows how geography determined which technologies crossed the water barrier between Indonesia and Australia/New Guinea. Tropical crops from Southeast Asia could reach New Guinea's highlands but not Australia's different climate. The dingo (descended from Southeast Asian dogs) reached Australia but no food crops did. Diamond uses these cases to demonstrate that the same peoples produced radically different outcomes depending on their environments.

Chapter 16: How China Became Chinese

Diamond examines how China—today one of the world's most linguistically and culturally homogeneous large nations—achieved this unity through the expansion of food-producing peoples from the north (millet farmers of the Yellow River valley) and south (rice farmers of the Yangtze valley). These expansions replaced or absorbed hundreds of earlier, distinct ethnic groups. The chapter traces how China's east-west river systems and lack of major internal geographic barriers facilitated cultural and political unification, in contrast to Europe's fragmented geography.

Diamond connects China's agricultural advantages (early domestication of rice, millet, pigs, and silkworms) to its subsequent technological achievements (paper, printing, gunpowder, compass) and its massive demographic expansions into Southeast Asia, driving the replacement of indigenous hunter-gatherers.

Chapter 17: Speedboat to Polynesia

Diamond traces the Austronesian expansion—one of the most dramatic population movements in human history—from its origins in southern China through Taiwan, the Philippines, Indonesia, and out into the Pacific. Austronesian-speaking farmers, equipped with outrigger canoes, pigs, chickens, and root crops, replaced indigenous hunter-gatherers across Island Southeast Asia and colonized previously uninhabited Pacific islands. The expansion reached as far as Madagascar (off the coast of Africa) and Easter Island (the most remote inhabited island on Earth).

Diamond explains why Austronesians colonized some areas but not others: they succeeded wherever their tropical farming package worked, but were stopped by the Aboriginal Australians (whose continent's climate was unsuitable for tropical crops) and by the New Guinea highlanders (who had already developed their own agriculture and dense populations).

Chapter 18: Hemispheres Colliding

Diamond synthesizes his arguments to explain the European conquest of the Americas. He compares Eurasian and Native American food production as of 1492: Eurasia had 13 large domestic mammals, the Americas had only one (the llama/alpaca, confined to the Andes). Eurasia had highly productive cereals, while the Americas' leading crop (corn) was far harder to domesticate and spread. Eurasia's east-west axis allowed rapid diffusion; the Americas' north-south axis and geographic barriers (Panama, deserts, tropical forests) fragmented agricultural zones and prevented the spread of innovations.

These differences compounded over millennia: Eurasia developed metallurgy, writing, oceangoing ships, epidemic diseases, horses, and centralized states earlier and more extensively. The result was a crushing asymmetry when the two hemispheres finally collided. Diamond emphasizes that the outcome was not due to any innate superiority of Europeans but to the environmental luck of living on a continent with better-suited wild species and a more favorable axis orientation.

Chapter 19: How Africa Became Black

Diamond applies his framework to explain Africa's complex ethnic, linguistic, and genetic diversity. He traces the Bantu expansion—the spread of Bantu-speaking farmers from their homeland in West Africa across sub-Saharan Africa over the past 5,000 years, replacing or absorbing indigenous Khoisan-speaking hunter-gatherers (Bushmen) and Pygmies. The Bantu succeeded because they possessed iron tools, Bantu crops (adapted to tropical conditions), and the demographic advantage of food production.

Diamond explains why Africa south of the Sahara lagged behind Eurasia: the Sahara Desert formed a massive barrier to the southward spread of Fertile Crescent crops and livestock; Africa's north-south axis meant crops had to cross climate zones; tropical diseases like malaria and sleeping sickness impeded colonization of some areas; and Africa had fewer domesticable large mammals (though it did have cattle). He notes that Africa's germs (especially malaria and yellow fever) protected Africans from European colonization far more effectively than Native American germs protected the Americas.

Counterpoints

Christopher Ehret and other Africanist historians have criticized Diamond for underestimating the complexity of African agricultural and political history. The Bantu expansion was not a simple wave of replacement but involved complex interactions, trade, and cultural exchange over millennia. Jan Vansina has argued that Diamond's treatment of Africa is oversimplified and Euro-centric in its framing.

Chapter 20: Who Are the Japanese?

Diamond examines Japan as a case study in how continental influences shaped an island society. He traces the origins of the Japanese people to two waves of migration: the Jomon people (indigenous hunter-gatherers who arrived over 14,000 years ago) and the Yayoi farmers (who arrived from Korea around 400 B.C. bringing rice agriculture, bronze and iron tools, and a new language). The Yayoi expansion replaced or absorbed the Jomon across most of Japan, except in the far north (where the Ainu people, likely descendants of the Jomon, survived).

Diamond uses genetic, linguistic, and archaeological evidence to argue that the Japanese are primarily descended from Yayoi immigrants rather than from the indigenous Jomon, making Japan another example of food producers replacing hunter-gatherers—a pattern seen throughout the book from the Fertile Crescent to Polynesia to sub-Saharan Africa.

Epilogue: The Future of Human History as a Science

Diamond restates his central argument: the striking differences among human societies are due to differences in environments, not in peoples. He summarizes the chain of causation: geographic and biogeographic differences in available domesticable plants and animals led to different rates of food production, which led to differences in population density, political complexity, technology, and disease, which determined the outcomes of collisions between societies.

Diamond acknowledges important unresolved questions, particularly why different parts of Eurasia developed differently (e.g., why Europe rather than China led industrialization), the role of cultural factors independent of environment, and the role of individual leaders. He advocates for developing human history as a historical science, analogous to evolutionary biology or geology, that seeks broad patterns and ultimate causes rather than just cataloguing events.

Counterpoints

The question Diamond defers—why Europe rather than China industrialized first—is precisely what many critics consider the most important question. Kenneth Pomeranz (The Great Divergence) argues that Europe's access to New World resources and coal deposits, not geographic fundamentals, explains the divergence. Niall Ferguson and David Landes emphasize institutional and cultural factors. Diamond's framework may explain the broad sweep of the last 13,000 years but struggles with the last 500.
