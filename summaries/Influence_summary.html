Influence, New and Expanded

The Psychology of Persuasion

Robert B. Cialdini

Preface

Influence opens in a nonacademic, conversational register by choice, a deliberate departure from the insular habits of mid-twentieth-century social psychology, which produced strong research but "no shipping department" for getting its findings out to the people who might actually use them. The style is retained across editions, but every claim remains anchored in properly conducted psychological research, illuminated rather than replaced by interviews, quotes, and field observation.

The current edition preserves the book's tested core while updating it to reflect advances in the study of persuasion, compliance, and change, with extra attention to how the influence process works in real-world settings rather than laboratories. An expanded Reader's Reports feature draws on firsthand accounts from readers who recognized the principles working on (or for) them in daily life, reinforcing that the levers of influence operate constantly in ordinary professional and personal situations.

Three elements appear for the first time. Specially marked eBoxes in each chapter illustrate how social media and e-commerce platforms have enthusiastically absorbed the lessons of persuasion science. Endnotes have been expanded into a more inclusive, narrative account of related research. Most significantly, a seventh universal principle of social influence has been added—the principle of unity—describing how people who can be convinced that a communicator shares a meaningful personal or social identity with them become remarkably more susceptible to that communicator's appeals.

Introduction

The study of compliance begins with a simple, personal puzzle: why does a request stated in one way get refused, while the same request stated slightly differently succeeds? Laboratory experiments alone can't answer it. To understand the psychology of yes, one has to look beyond college students in psychology buildings to the compliance professionals—salespeople, fundraisers, marketers, recruiters, advertisers, con artists—whose livelihoods depend on knowing which techniques actually work. Three years of participant observation inside sales organizations, advertising and public relations agencies, and fundraising operations, combined with interviews and examination of sales manuals, reveal that although thousands of tactics exist, the great majority fall within seven basic categories, each governed by a single fundamental psychological principle that gives the tactic its power.

The seven principles are reciprocation, liking, social proof, authority, scarcity, commitment and consistency, and unity. Each produces a distinct kind of automatic, mindless compliance—a willingness to say yes without thinking first—and the accelerating pace and informational overload of modern life makes this form of unthinking responding more prevalent, not less. Understanding the how and why of automatic influence therefore becomes increasingly important.

The chapters are sequenced according to Gregory Neidert's Core Motives Model of Social Influence, which holds that certain principles are better suited to certain persuasive goals. For cultivating a positive relationship, reciprocation, liking, and unity come first. For reducing uncertainty, social proof and authority follow. For motivating action once a relationship and confidence are in place, consistency and scarcity finish the sequence. These associations are not exclusive, but failing to deploy the aligned principle when it is available is a considerable mistake.

Chapter 1: Levers of Influence — (Power) Tools of the Trades

A surprising number of human behaviors operate like the fixed-action patterns ethologists observe in animals. A mother turkey will mother anything—even a stuffed polecat—that emits the "cheep-cheep" sound of her chicks, and ignore or attack her own offspring if the sound is absent. A single trigger feature activates an entire preset sequence of behavior: click, run. Humans have analogous programs. In Ellen Langer's famous copy-machine study, people let a requester cut the line 94% of the time when she said "May I use the Xerox machine, because I'm in a rush?" and 93% of the time when she said "May I use the Xerox machine because I have to make some copies?"—the word because alone triggered compliance, even when no real reason followed.

These shortcuts exist because the modern environment is too complex for full analysis of every person, event, and choice. People rely on judgmental heuristics—rules of thumb such as expensive = good, if an expert said so it must be true, or follow the crowd—that work well most of the time but leave them exposed to error and exploitation. A shop owner's allotment of unsellable turquoise jewelry sold out the moment a scribbled instruction to halve the price was misread as "times 2" and the prices doubled; well-to-do tourists unsure of turquoise quality used price as a proxy for worth. A drink advertised as cognition-enhancing produces more problem-solving when it costs $1.89 than when it costs $0.89, and a pain reliever "priced" at $2.50 per dose dulls shocks more than the identical reliever "priced" at $0.10.

Controlled, thoughtful responding happens when people have both the desire and the ability to analyze carefully; otherwise the click-run approach takes over. The form and pace of modern life increasingly denies people both—as demonstrated by "Captainitis," the pattern in which copilots and crew members fail to correct obvious, fatal errors by flight captains because "if an expert says so, it must be true." A multitude of profiteers have learned to exploit these automatic programs, and, like the mimic firefly whose counterfeit courtship signal lures the males of another species to their deaths, they mimic trigger features to activate the right behavior programs at the wrong times. Once activated, the process operates like jujitsu: the profiteer exerts almost no personal force and instead commissions the power of a natural psychological principle, which makes the manipulation nearly invisible even to the target.

A workhorse example is the contrast principle—the tendency to perceive two sequential items as more different than they actually are. A room-temperature bucket feels hot to a hand just removed from cold water and cold to a hand just removed from hot. Retail clothiers instruct salespeople to sell the suit before the sweater, because accessories look cheap after a big purchase; real-estate agents show "setup" properties first so that the house they intend to sell looks terrific; car dealers negotiate the price of the vehicle before adding options so that each add-on seems trivial against the larger figure. Each of these tactics cashes in on a natural feature of perception while leaving the victim feeling that the decision arose from nothing but their own good judgment.

Chapter 2: Reciprocation — The Old Give and Take

The rule of reciprocation is one of the most potent and widespread levers in human culture. People feel obligated to repay, in kind, what another person has provided them, and the obligation is installed so early and so deeply that failing to meet it invites contempt—"moocher," "ingrate," "freeloader." A university professor sending Christmas cards to strangers gets cards returned from people who have never heard of him; the Ethiopian Red Cross, in the midst of its own 1985 famine, sends $5,000 to Mexican earthquake victims because Mexico had aided Ethiopia against Italian invasion in 1935. In Dennis Regan's classic experiment, a confederate who left the room and returned with an unsolicited Coke for his "fellow subject" later sold those subjects twice as many raffle tickets as he sold to subjects he hadn't "treated"—and the pull of the favor overwhelmed whether the subject actually liked him.

Three features make the rule especially exploitable. First, it is overpowering: it overrides liking, suspicion, and ideological distaste (a CIA officer wins a reluctant Afghan tribal elder's cooperation with a gift of Viagra). Second, it enforces uninvited debts: a gift one never asked for still triggers the obligation, which is why charities mail unsolicited address labels, key rings, and greeting cards, and why survey researchers get more responses by including a $5 "gift" than by offering a $50 payment. Third, it can trigger grossly unequal exchanges—a 10-cent Coke pulls in 50 cents' worth of raffle tickets, a jumper-cable favor yields the loan of a new car—because the discomfort of indebtedness is so psychologically heavy that people will agree to large return favors just to make the feeling stop. The rule is supercharged when the initial gift is personalized or customized to the recipient's needs or preferences (a fast-food customer greeted with a small cup of yogurt matched to hunger buys more food than one given a key ring of equal retail value).

Reciprocation also governs concessions, producing the rejection-then-retreat (or door-in-the-face) technique: begin with an extreme request that will be refused, then retreat to the smaller request desired all along. The retreat reads as a concession, pressuring the target to make a reciprocal concession by agreeing. A Boy Scout selling tickets to a circus for $5 is refused, then successfully sells $1 chocolate bars. A request for college students to chaperone juvenile delinquents on a zoo trip triples in compliance (17% to 50%) when preceded by a declined request to mentor them for two years. The tactic carries a second engine—perceptual contrast, which makes the smaller request look even smaller after the larger one. Together these forces plausibly explain the Watergate break-in: G. Gordon Liddy's $250,000 plan looked like a "bare-bones" concession only because it followed earlier proposals of $1 million and $500,000 that included kidnap squads, chase planes, and blackmail yachts, leaving Mitchell and Magruder feeling they owed Liddy "a little something" in return.

The rejection-then-retreat technique has two remarkable side effects: targets feel more responsible for and more satisfied with agreements forged through the requester's concession, which makes them not only comply but follow through and willingly agree to further requests (blood donors give their phone numbers for future contact; community-agency volunteers actually show up for their shifts). The proper defense is not to refuse all favors and concessions—that would shut down the honored network of obligation that binds human societies—but to accept genuine favors in good faith while mentally redefining exploitation attempts as the compliance tactics they are. The rule says favors must be met with favors; it does not demand that tricks be met with favors.

Chapter 3: Liking — The Friendly Thief

People prefer to say yes to individuals they like, and compliance professionals exploit the liking rule directly by first getting targets to like them and indirectly by leveraging existing friendships. The Tupperware home party is the classic illustration: although a Tupperware demonstrator runs the sales mechanics, the true requester is the hostess—a friend to every partygoer—whose social bond with the guests is twice as predictive of purchases as preference for the product itself. The same logic drives charity canvassers who recruit volunteers to solicit their own neighbors, Shaklee's "endless chain" method in which a salesperson enters armed with the referral "Mr. So-and-so suggested I call on you," and modern refer-a-friend promotions that turn social ties directly into commissions.

Several factors reliably produce liking and are exploited accordingly. Physical attractiveness generates a halo effect, causing observers to assign attractive people traits of honesty, talent, kindness, and intelligence and to comply with them more readily; attractive political candidates receive more than twice as many votes as unattractive ones, yet voters almost unanimously deny the influence. Similarity—in dress, background, opinions, interests, even name initials or fingerprint type—produces liking and compliance automatically, which is why salespeople are trained to scan a customer's trade-in for shared hobbies and why negotiators, dating coaches, and advertisers deliberately mirror their targets' body language and verbal style. Compliments work even when recognized as manipulative and even when delivered by a computer; Joe Girard, the Guinness Book's "Greatest Car Salesman," mailed every one of his 13,000 former customers a monthly card reading simply "I like you" and outsold everyone in the industry. Contact and cooperation breed liking under pleasant conditions, but produce more hostility under competitive or frustrating ones—which is why simple school desegregation frequently increases rather than decreases prejudice.

The Sherif summer-camp studies showed the solution: when rival boy groups (the Eagles and Rattlers) were thrown into zero-sum competitions, they came to hate each other, but when they had to cooperate toward common goals that no group could achieve alone (pulling a stuck food truck, fixing the water supply, pooling money to rent a movie), the hostility dissolved into friendship. Elliot Aronson's jigsaw classroom applies the same principle to schools, requiring children from different groups to teach each other separate pieces of the material needed for an exam, and it reliably produces cross-group friendship and improved performance. Compliance practitioners exploit the same cooperation instinct routinely—new-car salespeople "do battle" with the sales manager on the customer's behalf, and police interrogators use Good Cop/Bad Cop to cast one officer as a trusted ally against a raging colleague, eliciting confessions through the relief and gratitude the suspect feels toward his "savior."

Liking is also manufactured through conditioning and association. The weatherman is hated for bad weather and praised for sunshine because bad news infects the teller (a "horns effect," the inverse of the halo); advertisers link products to attractive models, celebrities, popular causes, and even credit-card logos, which increase spending on unrelated purchases simply by being present. Gregory Razran's luncheon technique shows that people come to like ideas and people they encounter while eating—a finding grounded in Pavlovian conditioning—which explains the ubiquity of political fundraising dinners and business lunches. Sports fans ride the same machinery in reverse, chanting "we're number one" after victories and switching to "they lost" after defeats, basking in reflected glory because, by the association principle, a victory by a connected other is a personal victory. Because the factors producing liking operate unconsciously, the best defense is not to resist any single factor but to stay alert to the feeling of excessive liking and, when it appears, to mentally separate the requester from the merits of the request.

Chapter 4: Social Proof — Truths Are Us

The principle of social proof holds that people determine what is correct by looking at what other people think is correct. Label a menu item "most popular" and its sales jump 13 to 20 percent; tell pub-goers the porter is the brewery's best-selling beer and porter sales double; let Netflix publish which shows are being watched the most and those shows become more watched still. The principle operates because observing many others performing an action provides an efficient shortcut to deciding that the action is valid, feasible, and socially acceptable. It guides purchases, charity donations, tax and parking-ticket payment, face-mask wearing during a pandemic, environmental behavior, and even phobia remission (fearful children who watch other children play happily with a dog overcome their own fear within days).

The classic demonstration of how social proof compensates for uncertainty comes from Festinger, Riecken, and Schachter's infiltration of a doomsday cult led by "Marian Keech," which predicted a flood that would be averted by flying-saucer rescue. Before the prediction failed, the believers shunned publicity and refused converts. After it failed and their entire belief system was suddenly vulnerable, they reversed themselves completely and became aggressive missionaries—because physical proof of their beliefs had been destroyed, and the only remaining source of validation was social proof: the more people who could be convinced, the truer the belief would seem. The principle operates most powerfully under three optimizing conditions: uncertainty (ambiguous situations, unfamiliar settings), the many (more performers of an action increase its perceived validity, feasibility, and social acceptance), and similarity (the actions of peers carry far more weight than those of dissimilar others).

Uncertainty plus a group of similarly unsure observers produces pluralistic ignorance, in which bystanders in an emergency interpret each other's stillness as evidence that no emergency exists. The tragic pattern underlies the Kitty Genovese case and many others, and the defense for a victim is to eliminate the uncertainty: single out one specific bystander, describe the need, and assign responsibility—"You, sir, in the blue jacket, I need help. Call 911 for an ambulance." Similarity produces what can be called peer-suasion. David Phillips's studies of the Werther effect show that following highly publicized suicide stories, suicides, single-car "accidents," and single-plane crashes spike dramatically in the same geographical areas—with young victims copying young suicides and older victims copying older ones. The same dynamics of uncertainty plus peer modeling help explain the Jonestown mass suicide: relocated to an alien jungle where the only similar others were fellow Temple members, the residents followed the early obeyers in orderly, herd-like fashion.

Compliance professionals systematically counterfeit social proof—nightclub owners manufacture lines, bartenders salt tip jars, preachers plant ringers in audiences, review sites teem with faked testimonials, TV producers insert laugh tracks, and advertisers stage "unrehearsed interviews" with hired actors. Public-service campaigns commit the Big Mistake of decrying an undesirable behavior as widespread—littering, drug use, tax evasion, teen suicide—and inadvertently normalize it. A sign at Arizona's Petrified Forest depicting three thieves stealing wood nearly tripled theft, while a sign depicting a single thief reduced it. Communicators lacking current social proof can still harness its power honestly by citing future social proof—trends of increasing support, which audiences presume will continue. The autopilot of social proof is too useful to disconnect entirely, but it should be checked periodically against objective facts, prior experience, and personal judgment, and those who deliberately falsify the evidence deserve active retaliation.

Chapter 5: Authority — Directed Deference

Stanley Milgram's obedience experiments remain the defining illustration of the pull of authority. Ordinary adult volunteers, told they were teachers in a memory study, delivered what they believed to be ever-increasing electric shocks to a screaming, pleading, eventually silent learner—two-thirds of them going all the way to the maximum 450 volts—purely because a lab-coated researcher insisted the experiment must continue. Sex, age, occupation, and personality were irrelevant; the behavior was driven by the subjects' inability to defy an instruction from what appeared to be a legitimate authority. When the researcher ordered a stop and the fellow "subject" demanded continuation, 100 percent of subjects quit. The power lies in deep socialization: societies reward obedience to legitimate authorities—parents, teachers, judges, physicians, employers—because such authorities usually possess greater knowledge and hold the power to allocate rewards and punishments. Obedience is adaptive most of the time, which is precisely why it can slide into click-run responding.

The medical domain is rich with cases of unthinking deference. A physician's scrawled order for "place in R ear" leads a nurse to administer ear drops rectally. In a classic study, 95 percent of regular staff nurses prepared to administer a patently dangerous, unauthorized dose of a drug to a patient solely on the say-so of a voice on the phone claiming the title "doctor"—violating hospital policy, exceeding the maximum dose, and responding to a caller they had never met. Annual medical errors in the United States now exceed the deaths from all accidents combined, and much of the problem traces to patients, nurses, pharmacists, and junior doctors failing to question the "boss" of a case.

Because the automatic response attaches to the symbols rather than the substance of authority, those symbols can be counterfeited easily. Titles make people seem physically taller (each step up from student to professor adds roughly a half inch of perceived height), which is why con artists and TV doctors in Vicks Formula 44 ads ("I'm not a doctor, but I play one on TV") harvest deference they haven't earned. Clothing—Leonard Bickman's security-guard uniform nearly doubles compliance with arbitrary requests—and the business suit produce the same effect; the combination is deployed by swindlers in the "bank examiner" scheme. Trappings such as prestige cars, designer labels, and luxury watches also generate deference: motorists take significantly longer to honk at a luxury car blocking a green light than at an old economy model, and people judge strangers in higher-quality apparel as more competent in less than a second.

A credible authority is perceived as both expert and trustworthy. Expertise creates its own halo effect (a therapist's diplomas lift ratings not just of proficiency but of kindness and interest). Trustworthiness is usually thought to develop slowly, but it can be established rapidly through a clever tactic: mentioning a weakness in one's case early on, before rival attorneys, competing candidates, or skeptical customers do. Warren Buffett opens Berkshire Hathaway annual reports with candid admissions of mistakes; Domino's launched a wildly successful pizza revival by admitting its previous product had been bad; Avis built an enduring identity around "We're #2. We try harder." The waiter Vincent, observed in an expensive restaurant, doubled his tips by telling each table that their first-chosen dish "isn't as good tonight" and recommending a slightly cheaper one—establishing himself as an expert, a trustworthy informant seemingly arguing against his own interests, and, through reciprocity, a generous ally, before upselling wine and dessert. The defense is to pose two questions whenever authority is invoked: Is this authority truly an expert? and How truthful can I expect this expert to be?

Chapter 6: Scarcity — The Rule of the Few

Opportunities seem more valuable when they are less available. This is the core of the scarcity principle, and it operates in collectibles markets (where "precious mistake" stamps are prized for their rarity), in phone behavior (where a ringing phone out-prioritizes a live conversation because the call will vanish if unanswered), in iPhone launches (where buyers trade $2,800 handbags for a spot in line), and in divorce mediation (where reframing "Agree and we'll have a deal" as "We have a deal; all you have to do is agree" dramatically increases acceptance by converting a potential gain into a potential loss). The principle is intertwined with loss aversion—Kahneman's prospect theory finding that people are more motivated to avoid losing something of value than to gain something of equivalent value. Pamphlets urging breast self-exams work better when phrased in terms of what will be lost rather than gained; PGA golfers deliberate longer over putts to avoid bogey than to earn birdie; cafeteria food is rated suddenly better when a fire makes it unavailable for two weeks.

Two familiar compliance tactics exploit the principle directly. The limited-number tactic warns of dwindling supply ("only five left in the state," "our last one"), driving hesitant buyers into commitments at exactly the point when the product seems least available and most desirable. Booking.com's sales skyrocketed the moment it began displaying how few hotel rooms were still available at a given price. The deadline tactic imposes time limits ("Exclusive, limited engagement ends soon!"), with the ultimate deadline being "right now"—favored by door-to-door vacuum-cleaner salesmen, boiler-room stock swindlers, and any high-pressure seller hoping to keep prospects from taking time to think.

Scarcity draws its force from two sources. First, rare items are usually higher quality, so people use availability as a shortcut to value. Second, as opportunities become less available, freedoms are lost, and psychological reactance (Jack Brehm's theory) kicks in: when a freedom is restricted or threatened, people want it, and the things associated with it, more than before. The pattern appears in the terrible twos, who defy parents on principle, and in teenagers, for whom forbidden romances produce the Romeo and Juliet effect—parental interference with a relationship intensifies the partners' love for each other. It appears in adults too: Dade County's phosphate-detergent ban turned ordinary cleaners into prized contraband, and residents came to believe phosphate detergents even poured better. Censored information gains persuasive power without even being read (students became more sympathetic to a banned speech against coed dorms without hearing it). Exclusive information is more persuasive information—a beef-importing company's customers bought six times as much when told both that beef would be scarce and that the scarcity news itself was scarce.

Scarcity is most powerful in two conditions: when the scarcity is new (recently restricted freedoms are fought for more fiercely than freedoms that have always been limited, which is why James Davies's analysis finds revolutions occurring after a period of rising expectations followed by a sudden reversal—the American, French, and Russian revolutions, the US civil-rights riots of the 1960s, and the failed Soviet coup against Gorbachev all fit the pattern), and when the scarce resource is the object of competition (a chocolate-chip cookie one had to share via social demand was rated higher than the same cookie made scarce by accident, and Barry Diller paid a record-shattering $3.3 million for a single TV showing of The Poseidon Adventure because ABC, CBS, and NBC were forced into an auction). The defense is difficult because scarcity produces emotional arousal that impairs thinking—the very condition under which jujitsu-style tactics work best. The best protection is to use the rising arousal itself as a signal to stop, calm down, and ask why the item is wanted: if for the prestige of possession, scarcity is a valid guide to price; but if for utility, the item will not taste or drive or function any better because of its limited availability.

Chapter 7: Commitment and Consistency — Hobgoblins of the Mind

Once people take a stand or make a choice, they encounter personal and interpersonal pressures to behave consistently with it. This drive for commitment and consistency is deep enough to cause bettors to become more confident in their horse right after placing the bet, voters to believe more strongly in their candidate right after casting the ballot, and beachgoers who have agreed to "watch my things" to chase down thieves they would otherwise have ignored. Amazon's "Pay to Quit" program offers workers up to $5,000 to leave, not primarily to weed out the unhappy but to convert everyone who declines into a more committed employee via a hard personal choice. Consistency is valued because it is socially approved, because it is generally adaptive, and because it functions as a cognitive shortcut: once a decision has been made, there is no need to re-examine the relevant facts. But it also operates as a foolish fortress against unwelcome realizations—attendees at a Transcendental Meditation recruitment lecture whose logical basis had just been demolished hurried to hand over their money precisely because the argument had been demolished, locking in a decision before further thought could undermine it.

The trigger that activates the consistency program is commitment. Once a stand is taken, later behavior aligns itself stubbornly with that stand—which is why compliance professionals work so hard to extract initial commitments, however trivial. Toy companies run heavy pre-Christmas ads for special toys, deliberately undersupply retailers, let parents substitute other gifts to keep their promises, then re-run the ads after Christmas so the kids can whine "you promised" their parents back into the stores. The foot-in-the-door technique, demonstrated by Freedman and Fraser, showed that residents who had agreed to display a tiny "Be a Safe Driver" sign or to sign a "Keep California Beautiful" petition later agreed at startling rates to place a huge, ugly "Drive Carefully" billboard on their front lawns—because the small act had changed how they saw themselves. Solicitors who begin phone calls with "How are you feeling this evening?" nearly double their success at later charity requests, because people who have publicly stated they're doing fine find it awkward to plead stinginess.

Commitments are most effective at changing self-image when they are active, public, effortful, and freely chosen. Chinese Communist interrogators during the Korean War used exactly this combination, getting American POWs to write small pro-Communist statements, then to sign them, then to read them aloud, then to enter voluntary essay contests for trivial prizes where the winning entries happened to include minor pro-Communist concessions—and from these small, owned, written, public steps, the prisoners drifted into full "collaborator" identities. The same features explain why Amway pushes sales staff to write down and display their goals, why hazing rituals from Thonga initiation rites to fraternity Hell Week are fiercely defended by the groups that practice them (Aronson and Mills showed that the more painful or embarrassing the initiation, the more valuable the group seems to those who endured it), and why Freedman's severe-threat experiment showed that children refrained from playing with a forbidden robot six weeks later only when they had been given a mild reason, not a heavy threat—leaving them free to conclude they hadn't played with it because they themselves chose not to.

Commitments "grow their own legs": people invent additional justifications for a decision they've already made, so when the original inducement is removed, the decision stands. The low-ball technique exploits this directly. Car dealers offer a great price, get the buyer to commit, file forms, take the car home, develop new reasons to love it, and then discover an "error" that raises the price by $700—and the buyer nearly always pays. Researchers used the same technique to get students to show up for a 7 a.m. experiment: asked about participation before being told the time, 56 percent agreed and 95 percent of those showed up, compared to only 24 percent when the time was disclosed upfront. Low-balling works for socially beneficial purposes too: Iowa homeowners who committed to energy conservation in exchange for newspaper publicity kept conserving (and conserved more) even after the publicity was canceled, because the commitment had already generated its own supports. Simple reminders of prior commitments regenerate and even strengthen them—signs above hospital sinks pointing to the link between hand washing and patient protection raised hand-hygiene compliance by 45 percent, and posters in which doctors publicly pledged against antibiotic overprescription cut inappropriate prescribing by 27 percent. The defense is to listen for two internal signals: the stomach sign of knowing one is being trapped by a foolish consistency, and the heart-of-hearts sign revealed by asking "Knowing what I now know, if I could go back in time, would I make the same commitment?" and trusting the first flash of feeling that arises.

Chapter 8: Unity — The "We" Is the Shared Me

Some "we" relationships go beyond mere similarity to shared identity—the feeling that another person is not just like me but one of me. The principle of unity says people say yes to those they consider part of their in-group, and the categories that produce such feelings are the tribal ones: race, ethnicity, nationality, family, and political and religious affiliation. Inside a "we"-group, influence is cheaper: members favor each other's welfare, imitate each other's choices, and feel each other's outcomes as their own. A Nazi concentration-camp guard executing every tenth prisoner in a line turned aside and shot the eleventh because he had recognized the tenth as someone from his hometown—a horrifying expression of how strong localism-based "we"-ness can be. In contrast, Ali Reda surpassed Joe Girard's legendary car-sales record by selling intensely into his tight-knit Arab American community in Dearborn, and Bernard Madoff's Ponzi scheme snared financially sophisticated victims because he and his recruiters were drawn from the same Jewish social networks—an affinity scheme that fooled even foxes.

"We"-ness shapes politics (where "blue lies" told for the in-group are judged more moral than truths that weaken it, and where party loyalists will rank political allies ahead of medically deserving strangers for kidney treatment), sports (where supposedly neutral referees call an extra 10 percent of international soccer decisions for players from their home country, and where a Balkan soldier mute for thirty years broke his silence only to curse a referee deciding against his hometown team), and personal relationships (where the most successful romantic-dispute technique is neither coercive threats nor logical argument but partnership raising—"You know, we've been together for a long time, and we care for one another; I'd appreciate it if you'd do this for me"). The effect reaches contagion yawning, which occurs most frequently between kin and friends, and even crosses species between dogs and their owners.

Unity arises from two main kinds of factors. The first is belonging together: kinship (people help genetically close relatives more, and communicators can evoke the same effect with familial language such as "brothers," "sisterhood," "motherland," "heritage"—as Warren Buffett did in his fiftieth-anniversary letter to shareholders, saying he would tell them "what I would say to my family today") and place (home, locality, and region). Chiune Sugihara, the Japanese consul in Lithuania, defied direct orders to write thousands of travel visas saving Polish Jews from the Nazis, traceable to a childhood of watching his parents care for diverse strangers in the family inn as though they were kin. Rabbi Shimon Kalisch saved the Jewish refugee community in Japanese-controlled Shanghai with a single penetrating observation to the wartime High Command: "Because we are Asian, like you"—shifting the officers' in-group boundaries to exclude their Nazi allies and include the Jews.

The second factor is acting together: responding in synchrony or coordination through song, dance, march, ritual, story-reading, finger-tapping, or mutual eye contact. Groups that sing or march in unison develop shared identities, greater liking for each other (even across racial lines, when white subjects synchronously mimic the actions of Black actors in a video), and greater willingness to sacrifice for each other. Music is uniquely suited to producing such coordination because it operates through System 1 (fast, emotional, intuitive) rather than System 2 (slow, rational, analytical). Other routes to acting together include repeated reciprocal exchange (Arthur and Elaine Aron's thirty-six escalating questions produce the feelings of love in forty-five minutes between strangers), shared suffering (Hitler personally intervened to protect a Jewish judge named Ernst Hess because they had fought together and been wounded within twenty-four hours of each other at the Somme; and Irish donors flooded Navajo COVID-19 relief funds, reciprocating a Choctaw gift of 1847 that had itself been inspired by Choctaw memory of the Trail of Tears), and co-creation, including the powerful practice of asking for advice, which puts the advisor in a merging state of mind and makes them feel linked to the party receiving counsel. Unity can be exploited too: in-group protection is why corporate Codes of Conduct written in "we" language produce more illegal conduct than codes written in formal language, and why labor unions and religious hierarchies so often shield their worst members. The defense against such exploitation is to build a no-tolerance commitment directly into the organization's "we"-based self-definition—a sign in the window.

Chapter 9: Instant Influence — Primitive Consent for an Automatic Age

A recurring theme across the seven principles is that decisions about people and requests are often made from a single representative piece of information rather than from full analysis of all available evidence. This is primitive automaticity: the same click-run pattern that lets a mother turkey mother anything that cheep-cheeps also lets humans say yes to anything that carries the trigger features of reciprocation, liking, social proof, authority, scarcity, commitment and consistency, or unity. Lower animals rely on such shortcuts because of limited mental capacity. Humans rely on them because the modern environment has become too complex, too fast, and too information-saturated for full deliberation to be the default; when rushed, stressed, uncertain, distracted, or fatigued, people revert to the same single-cue responding. The unnerving implication is that having built a civilization of unprecedented complexity, people must increasingly deal with it in the manner of the animals long ago transcended.

John Stuart Mill is said to have been the last man to know everything there was to know in 1873. Today most human information is less than fifteen years old, scientific knowledge in fields such as physics doubles every eight years, about two million journal articles are published annually, and the informational power of a single cell phone exceeds that of an entire university of a few years ago. The Information Age is not the Knowledge Age—information must be processed to become knowledge, and processing capacity has not kept pace. As a result, shortcut responding to single trigger features will become more prevalent, not less, and so will the compliance tactics that exploit it.

The proper response is not to abandon the shortcuts, which are genuine necessities rather than luxuries, but to defend their fitness for duty through active retaliation against those who falsify the signals. Compliance professionals who play by the honest rules of shortcut responding—accurately citing the social proof of a best-selling product, accurately disclosing a scarce supply, accurately claiming relevant expertise—are allies in an efficient system of exchange and should be rewarded. Profiteers who counterfeit signals by hiring actors to play "ordinary consumers," manufacturing fake lines outside nightclubs, planting phony online reviews, or concocting fictitious shortages deserve aggressive pushback: refusing to buy, boycotting brands, writing manufacturers, shaming the deceivers on social media. The stakes are not merely personal. Each successful counterfeit erodes the reliability of a rule of thumb that everyone else also depends on to navigate an increasingly overloaded world, and that erosion cannot be allowed without a fight.
