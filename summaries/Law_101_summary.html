Law 101

Everything You Need to Know About American Law, Fourth Edition

Jay M. Feinman

Chapter 1: There Are No Secret Books — You Can Understand the Law

The opening chapter establishes the book's central premise: the law is not a secret code accessible only to professionals. The author argues that law reflects life—it deals with the same kinds of issues everyone encounters daily, from promises and accidents to property ownership and personal liberty. Using vivid examples—the Stella Liebeck McDonald's coffee case, the Guantanamo Bay detainee cases, virtual property in Second Life, and the Trayvon Martin shooting—the chapter demonstrates how law touches almost every aspect of modern existence.

Feinman introduces several key insights that form the book's perspective. First, law is not in the law books—it lives in the conduct of judges, lawyers, and citizens, as illustrated by the gap between posted speed limits and actual driving behavior. Second, there are no simple answers in law; legal problems resist easy solutions because life is complicated and we are often of two minds about issues. Third, law is a battleground of political conflict, where fundamental questions about social resources and values are constantly being negotiated. Finally, people make the law—it is not some natural, inevitable force but a human creation for which we all bear responsibility.

The chapter maps out the structure of the book, which mirrors the first-year law school curriculum: constitutional law, civil rights, civil procedure, torts, contracts, property, criminal law, and criminal procedure. Feinman promises that the book will engage readers in the process of lawmaking rather than just describing rules, asking them to grapple with the same tough questions that lawyers and judges face.

Counterpoints

While Feinman's populist approach to legal education is admirable, critics like Richard Posner and other law-and-economics scholars might argue that legal reasoning is more technical and specialized than this chapter suggests. The claim that "anyone can understand the law" may underestimate the genuine complexity of statutory interpretation, procedural requirements, and doctrinal analysis that make professional legal training necessary. Additionally, the chapter's perspective that "people make the law" and that law is inherently political could be seen as overly dismissive of rule-of-law principles and the genuine constraints that legal precedent, text, and institutional norms place on legal actors.

Chapter 2: Constitutional Law and Constitutional Politics

This chapter explores how the U.S. Constitution—a document of fewer than 4,400 words—structures American government and generates the most hotly debated topics in law. Feinman identifies four distinctive features of constitutional law: it defines the relationship between government and citizens rather than between individuals; it relies on a single, narrow source text; it raises fundamental political and value questions more overtly than other areas of law; and the authority of courts to interpret it is itself contested.

The chapter traces the great debate between originalism—the view that the Constitution should be interpreted according to its framers' intent or original public understanding—and nonoriginalism, or the idea of a "living Constitution" that evolves with changing circumstances. Justice Scalia championed originalism as providing determinate results, while critics pointed out the difficulty of reconstructing historical intent and the impossibility of applying eighteenth-century understandings to modern technologies and social conditions. The chapter suggests that constitutional interpretation inevitably involves an act of choice, constrained but not determined by text, history, and legal culture.

Feinman examines the foundation of judicial review through the landmark case Marbury v. Madison (1803), in which Chief Justice John Marshall claimed for the Court the power to determine the constitutionality of legislation. The chapter then traces the expansion of federal power through the commerce clause, from Marshall's broad interpretation in Gibbons v. Ogden through the New Deal transformation, up to modern limits imposed in cases like United States v. Lopez and NFIB v. Sebelius (the Affordable Care Act case). The discussion of federalism explains how the relationship between state and federal power has evolved from dual sovereignty to modern preemption doctrine, and how Congress uses conditional spending to influence state policy.

The chapter also examines executive power, particularly the president's authority as commander-in-chief. From the Steel Seizure Cases during the Korean War through post-9/11 detention policies and the Guantanamo Bay cases, the chapter illustrates the ongoing tension between national security imperatives and constitutional limits on government power.

Counterpoints

The chapter's treatment of originalism may not do full justice to sophisticated modern originalist scholarship. Scholars like Jack Balkin and Lawrence Solum have developed "living originalism" and "original public meaning" approaches that are more nuanced than the strict constructionism Feinman critiques. Additionally, the chapter's suggestion that constitutional interpretation is essentially political choice constrained only by culture and tradition may understate the genuine constraining force of legal doctrine, institutional norms, and the text itself. Legal process theorists like Henry Hart and Albert Sacks would argue that the institutional competence of courts and their commitment to reasoned elaboration of principle provides more meaningful constraint than Feinman acknowledges.

Chapter 3: First Freedoms — Constitutional Rights

This chapter addresses the individual rights protected by the Constitution, distinguishing between process rights (how the government must behave) and substantive rights (areas where the government cannot intrude regardless of procedure). The chapter begins with due process, explaining how the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments protect life, liberty, and property. The concept of "property" has expanded from tangible assets to include government entitlements such as welfare benefits, public employment, and professional licenses, as established in Goldberg v. Kelly (1970). The Mathews v. Eldridge balancing test determines how much process is due by weighing the individual's interest, the risk of error, and the government's burden.

The chapter's treatment of equal protection explains the three-tiered system of judicial review: rational relationship for ordinary economic legislation, strict scrutiny for suspect classifications like race or infringements on fundamental rights, and intermediate review for classifications like gender. The discussion traces affirmative action from Regents v. Bakke through Grutter v. Bollinger and Fisher v. University of Texas, showing how the Court has struggled to balance remedial and diversity interests against formal equality principles.

Freedom of speech receives extensive treatment, from the "marketplace of ideas" theory articulated by Justice Holmes to modern controversies. The chapter explains key doctrines: the prohibition on prior restraints, the requirement of content neutrality, and regulations of the time, place, and manner of speech. Specific topics include flag burning (Texas v. Johnson), corporate speech (Citizens United), commercial speech, defamation (New York Times v. Sullivan), obscenity (the Miller test), and incitement (Brandenburg v. Ohio). The chapter also addresses the religion clauses, including the establishment clause (school vouchers, public religious displays, the "plastic reindeer doctrine") and the free exercise clause (from Sherbert v. Verner to Employment Division v. Smith).

The chapter concludes with the right of privacy, tracing its development from Meyer v. Nebraska and Pierce v. Society of Sisters through the contraception cases (Griswold v. Connecticut, Eisenstadt v. Baird) to the abortion cases (Roe v. Wade and Planned Parenthood v. Casey). The discussion of Lawrence v. Texas (striking down sodomy laws) and United States v. Windsor (invalidating the Defense of Marriage Act) illustrates how privacy rights have continued to expand, while District of Columbia v. Heller confirmed an individual right to own guns under the Second Amendment.

Counterpoints

The chapter's treatment of the right to privacy could be challenged on both liberal and conservative grounds. Conservative scholars like Robert Bork have argued that the right of privacy is a judicial invention with no basis in constitutional text, creating an unchecked power for judges to constitutionalize their personal preferences. On the other hand, progressive scholars like Robin West argue that the privacy framework is inadequate because it conceptualizes rights as freedom from government rather than positive entitlements to government assistance, leaving many people—particularly women and the poor—without meaningful constitutional protection. The chapter's presentation of Citizens United also reflects the controversial nature of treating corporate spending as protected speech, a view sharply contested by scholars who argue it distorts democratic governance.

Chapter 4: Your Day in Court — The Litigation Process

This chapter demystifies civil procedure by following the real case of the Robinson family, whose Audi caught fire after a rear-end collision in Oklahoma. Feinman explains that litigation is rare—of 10,000 potential grievances, only about 50 result in lawsuits and perhaps 2 or 3 go to trial. The chapter introduces key concepts of subject matter jurisdiction (a court's authority over the type of case) and personal jurisdiction (authority over the parties), using World-Wide Volkswagen Corp. v. Woodson to illustrate the constitutional "minimum contacts" requirement from International Shoe v. Washington.

The chapter explains the dual court system of state and federal courts, with federal courts exercising either federal question jurisdiction (cases arising under federal law) or diversity jurisdiction (cases between citizens of different states). The Robinsons' strategic inclusion of New York defendants to destroy diversity jurisdiction and keep their case in plaintiff-friendly Oklahoma state court illustrates how procedural rules drive litigation strategy. The chapter also covers conflict of laws and the messy question of which state's law applies when a transaction has connections to multiple jurisdictions.

The chapter walks through each stage of the litigation process: pleading (the complaint, answer, and motions to dismiss), discovery (depositions, interrogatories, document production), pretrial proceedings (summary judgment, settlement negotiations, alternative dispute resolution), trial (the adversary process, presentation of evidence, jury instructions), and post-trial proceedings (motions for judgment as a matter of law, new trials, appeals). At each stage, Feinman highlights how the system balances the goals of implementing substantive law, providing fair process, and operating efficiently.

The discussion of juries is particularly illuminating. Feinman explains how amateur jurors must sift through conflicting evidence, understand complex legal instructions, and apply law to facts. He describes the mechanisms of jury control—evidence rules, the law-fact distinction, instructions, and motions for judgment as a matter of law—while noting the ongoing debate about whether juries are effective fact-finders or "antiquated, inefficient" anachronisms. The chapter concludes with the appeals process and the principle of res judicata (finality).

Counterpoints

The chapter's balanced presentation of the litigation system may understate the degree to which the system is inaccessible to many Americans. Legal scholars like Deborah Rhode have documented a substantial "justice gap," with studies showing that low- and middle-income Americans cannot afford legal representation for many civil matters. The contingent fee system, which Feinman presents as democratizing access to justice, has been criticized by some for creating perverse incentives for attorneys and inflating litigation costs. Additionally, the chapter's focus on formal litigation may give insufficient attention to the growing importance of mandatory arbitration clauses in consumer and employment contracts, which increasingly prevent individuals from accessing courts altogether.

Chapter 5: Auto Accidents, Scalding Coffee, and Medical Malpractice — Personal Injuries and Tort Law

The tort law chapter opens with the famous Stella Liebeck McDonald's coffee case, using it to frame the broader tort reform debate. Tort reformers argue that courts have gone overboard in compensating victims, creating a "tort tax" that raises costs and undermines personal responsibility. Defenders counter that tort law has dramatically improved safety—from crashworthy cars to safer medical devices—and that the system functions as designed in cases like Liebeck's, where McDonald's had ignored 700 prior complaints about scalding coffee.

The chapter explains three categories of tort liability. Intentional torts like battery protect bodily integrity against deliberate invasions; the case of Garratt v. Dailey (a five-year-old pulling a chair from under an elderly woman) illustrates how "intent" requires only substantial certainty that harmful contact will occur, not necessarily a desire to cause harm. Negligence, the most common category, involves failure to exercise reasonable care. The Learned Hand formula (B < PL) provides an algebraic framework: liability exists when the burden of precaution is less than the probability of harm multiplied by the severity of the injury. The reasonable person standard personifies this formula as an idealized individual who always acts prudently.

The chapter covers important limitations on the duty of care, including immunities, restricted duties for economic harm and emotional distress, and the controversial no-duty-to-rescue rule. Comparative negligence has largely replaced the harsh contributory negligence doctrine, allowing the jury to apportion fault between plaintiff and defendant. The discussion of strict products liability—from the exploding Coca-Cola bottle in Escola to the defective Shopsmith in Greenman v. Yuba Power Products—explains how manufacturers can be held liable even without proof of negligence, and traces the evolution of the consumer expectations and risk-utility tests for design defects.

The chapter's treatment of damages uses the tragic case of Keva Richardson, paralyzed in a rear-end collision, to illustrate both economic damages (medical expenses, lost earnings) and noneconomic damages (pain and suffering, loss of enjoyment of life). It explains the single-judgment rule, the individualized nature of damage awards, and the controversy over punitive damages and damage caps in tort reform legislation.

Counterpoints

The chapter's defense of tort law, while acknowledging reform arguments, may not fully reckon with empirical evidence about the system's inefficiency. Studies have shown that the tort system returns only about 46 cents of every dollar to plaintiffs, with the rest consumed by legal fees and administrative costs. Economists like W. Kip Viscusi have argued that regulatory approaches can achieve safety improvements more efficiently than case-by-case litigation. Additionally, behavioral economists challenge the deterrence rationale by showing that individuals and firms often do not respond rationally to tort incentives—people underestimate low-probability risks and overreact to vivid, high-profile cases. The insurance function of tort law is also questionable; first-party insurance and social insurance programs may distribute losses more efficiently than the tort system's third-party liability mechanism.

Chapter 6: When Is a Deal a Deal? — Businesses, Consumers, and Contract Law

The chapter begins by establishing that contract law governs the making, keeping, and breaking of promises—from casual dinner invitations to billion-dollar construction agreements. Contract law serves two fundamental functions: providing a dispute resolution mechanism for exchanges projected into the future, and demonstrating society's commitment to freedom and autonomy through the enforcement of voluntary agreements. Feinman uses the case of Hurley v. Eddingfield (1901), where a doctor refused to treat a dying patient, to explore the limits and complications of freedom of contract—both the freedom to contract and the freedom from contract.

The chapter explains contract formation through the objective theory of assent: contracts are formed when a reasonable person would conclude that a promise has been made, regardless of the promisor's subjective intent. The case of Embry v. Hargadine, McKittrick Dry Goods Co. illustrates how an employer's casual words can create a binding contract if reasonably understood as a promise. The Statute of Frauds requires certain contracts (land sales, contracts lasting over a year, sales of goods over $500) to be in writing, though courts have created numerous exceptions to avoid injustice. The chapter also addresses capacity to contract, explaining special protections for minors and mentally incapacitated persons.

The treatment of consideration—the doctrine requiring an exchange to make a promise enforceable—reveals one of contract law's most peculiar features. Gift promises are unenforceable for lack of consideration, while even absurd exchanges are binding. The chapter traces the decline of consideration through the reliance principle (enforcing promises reasonably relied upon even without an exchange) and the abandonment of the preexisting duty rule. Feinman also addresses unfairness in contracts through doctrines of duress and unconscionability, and discusses form contracts, remedies for breach (expectation damages, specific performance), and defenses like impossibility and frustration of purpose.

Counterpoints

The chapter's emphasis on freedom of contract may not sufficiently account for the power imbalances in modern contracting. Legal scholars like Margaret Jane Radin have argued that the proliferation of non-negotiable "boilerplate" contracts—from software licenses to employment agreements—has transformed contract law from a regime of genuine consent into one of unilateral corporate rule-making. The idea that clicking "I agree" constitutes meaningful assent to pages of impenetrable legal terms strains the foundational principle of voluntary agreement. Additionally, behavioral law and economics research by scholars like Oren Bar-Gill shows that consumers systematically fail to read, understand, or rationally evaluate contract terms, undermining the assumption that contract enforcement respects autonomous choice.

Chapter 7: You Are What You Own — The Law of Property

The chapter challenges the intuitive notion of property as absolute ownership of things. Feinman argues that property is really about relationships among people with respect to valuable resources, constituted by a bundle of rights: the liberty to use, the right to exclude, the power to transfer, and immunity from damage. Each of these rights is subject to significant limitations—zoning regulations limit use, antidiscrimination laws restrict exclusion, and environmental regulations constrain transfer.

The chapter provides three justifications for private property: it encourages productive activity by providing security and enabling efficient transfer of resources; it protects political liberty by establishing independence from government control (as Thomas Jefferson argued); and it contributes to human flourishing by enabling people to create a sense of self and belonging. The discussion of types of property moves from the basic distinction between real property (land) and personal property (everything else) to intellectual property—patents, copyrights, and trademarks—which illustrates how property is created and defined by law rather than naturally occurring.

Among the most important topics covered are property acquisition (purchase, adverse possession, finding), real estate transactions, inheritance and trusts, concurrent ownership (joint tenancy, tenancy in common, tenancy by the entirety), landlord-tenant law, and the eminent domain power of the government to take private property for public use with just compensation. The chapter also explores cutting-edge questions about Internet property, including virtual property in online games and the debate over how intellectual property law should apply to digital content, file-sharing, and remix culture.

Counterpoints

The chapter's three justifications for private property have been challenged on multiple fronts. The economic efficiency argument faces the tragedy of the anticommons problem, identified by scholar Michael Heller: too many property rights can block productive use just as effectively as too few. The political liberty argument is complicated by evidence that concentrated property ownership can undermine democracy rather than support it, as when wealthy corporations dominate political discourse. And the personal flourishing argument must contend with the reality that for billions of people worldwide, the absence of secure property rights—not their presence—defines their relationship with resources. Hernando de Soto has argued that informal property systems in developing countries represent enormous "dead capital" that cannot be leveraged for economic advancement.

Chapter 8: From Insanity to In Cold Blood — Criminal Responsibility and Criminal Law

The chapter defines criminal law as fundamentally about moral condemnation and punishment based on blameworthiness. Unlike civil wrongs, crimes are offenses against the public good, prosecuted by the state regardless of the victim's wishes. The author distinguishes criminal law from civil litigation, juvenile proceedings, and administrative sanctions, and separates substantive criminal law (what behavior is criminal) from criminal procedure (how crimes are investigated and adjudicated).

Feinman presents two main justifications for criminal punishment: utilitarian approaches (deterrence, incapacitation, rehabilitation, and denunciation) and retributivist approaches (punishment is deserved because the criminal has done something morally wrong). The chapter explains that deterrence—the dominant utilitarian justification—assumes rational actors who weigh costs and benefits, but notes its limits with psychopaths, crimes of passion, and offenders who believe they won't be caught. Rehabilitation has declined as a goal since studies in the 1970s questioned its effectiveness. Retributivism serves as a check on deterrence, preventing disproportionate punishments.

The chapter covers the essential elements of criminal liability: a voluntary criminal act (actus reus) and a culpable mental state (mens rea). The Model Penal Code's four mental states—purposely, knowingly, recklessly, and negligently—are illustrated through a hunting hypothetical. The chapter discusses self-defense (including the controversial "Stand Your Ground" laws), the insanity defense (tracing the M'Naghten test through modern formulations), attempt and conspiracy, and specific crimes including homicide (murder, voluntary and involuntary manslaughter, felony murder) and rape (including the evolving definitions of consent).

Counterpoints

The chapter's treatment of criminal responsibility may not sufficiently address the structural inequities embedded in the criminal justice system. Michelle Alexander's work on mass incarceration has shown how criminal law has been applied disproportionately to communities of color, raising questions about whether the system truly serves retributivist or deterrent goals or instead perpetuates racial and economic inequality. The chapter's focus on individual blameworthiness also sidesteps the growing scholarly interest in social determinants of crime—poverty, trauma, lack of opportunity—which complicate the moral framework of retribution. Additionally, neuroscience research increasingly challenges traditional notions of free will and voluntary action, suggesting that the criminal law's concept of mens rea may rest on scientifically questionable foundations.

Chapter 9: Protecting the Innocent, Freeing the Guilty — Criminal Procedure

The chapter frames criminal procedure as a constant balancing act between controlling crime and protecting individual rights. The "criminal procedure revolution" of the 1960s applied the Bill of Rights to state criminal proceedings through selective incorporation under the Fourteenth Amendment. The chapter walks through the entire criminal process: investigation, arrest, booking, charging, initial appearance, preliminary hearing, grand jury indictment, arraignment, pretrial motions, trial, sentencing, and appeals.

The treatment of search and seizure under the Fourth Amendment is particularly detailed. The chapter explains the evolution from physical trespass to expectation of privacy as the basis for Fourth Amendment protection (Katz v. United States), while noting the Court's inconsistent applications—homes are protected from thermal imaging but backyards are not protected from aerial photography, and people have no privacy interest in their garbage. The chapter examines the warrant requirement, the probable cause standard, and numerous exceptions including plain view, search incident to arrest, and stop and frisk under Terry v. Ohio. Post-9/11 developments including the PATRIOT Act and NSA surveillance programs receive attention.

The chapter covers the privilege against self-incrimination and Miranda warnings, the exclusionary rule (excluding illegally obtained evidence) and its controversial exceptions, plea bargaining (which resolves roughly 95% of criminal cases), the right to a jury trial, the adversary process, and sentencing (including the shift from indeterminate to determinate sentencing, mandatory minimums, and sentencing guidelines). The discussion of the death penalty traces its constitutional history from Furman v. Georgia through modern challenges, noting the decline in executions and growing concerns about wrongful convictions. The chapter concludes with postconviction remedies, particularly habeas corpus petitions.

Counterpoints

The chapter's balanced treatment of criminal procedure may not adequately convey the severity of the system's failures. The Innocence Project has documented over 375 DNA exonerations since 1989, revealing systemic problems with eyewitness identification, false confessions, and forensic science errors. Scholars like William Stuntz in The Collapse of American Criminal Justice have argued that the focus on procedural rights has paradoxically contributed to mass incarceration by channeling legal challenges into procedural technicalities while leaving prosecutors virtually unchecked in their substantive charging power. The discussion of plea bargaining may also understate the degree to which the practice has replaced trials rather than supplemented them—some scholars argue this represents a fundamental distortion of the adversarial system the chapter celebrates.

Conclusion

The brief conclusion invites readers to apply what they have learned by identifying legal issues in everyday news and life. Feinman asks readers to do what judges, lawyers, and law students do: identify the relevant areas of law, consider the applicable rules and principles, weigh competing arguments, and think critically about what justice requires. The chapter serves as both a practical exercise and a philosophical statement—that understanding law is not about memorizing rules but about developing the capacity to see different sides of a question, articulate competing values, and reason toward just outcomes. The author's final message is welcoming: "Congratulations—and welcome to the law."
