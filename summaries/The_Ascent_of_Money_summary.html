The Ascent of Money

A Financial History of the World

Niall Ferguson

Introduction

Money is everywhere and yet poorly understood. The gap between the ultra-wealthy—a Goldman Sachs CEO earning $68.5 million while nearly a billion people live on a dollar a day—exemplifies the inequalities that have long provoked hostility toward finance. Yet this hostility rests on a misreading: poverty is not caused by the presence of financial institutions but by their absence. The evolution of credit and debt was as essential to civilization as any technological breakthrough, from ancient Babylon through the Italian Renaissance to the modern United States.

By 2006, Planet Finance had grown to dwarf Planet Earth. Total derivatives outstanding reached $473 trillion—more than ten times global GDP. Two trillion dollars changed hands on foreign exchange markets every single day. New instruments proliferated: mortgage-backed securities, collateralized debt obligations, credit default swaps. The financial sector's share of US GDP climbed from 2.3 per cent in 1947 to 7.7 per cent by 2005, and more than a fifth of Harvard's graduating class expected to enter banking.

Then, in the summer of 2007, the subprime mortgage crisis demonstrated one of the perennial truths of financial history: sooner or later every bubble bursts. Complex securities backed by the mortgages of low-income American borrowers were sold to German regional banks and Norwegian municipalities, and when those borrowers defaulted, the losses rippled across the global system. Northern Rock suffered the first British bank run since 1866. The proximate cause of economic pain was financial, not industrial.

Three core insights animate the entire work. First, poverty is a consequence of missing financial infrastructure, not exploitative finance. Second, the financial system amplifies human psychology—booms and busts are products of emotional volatility as much as of cold calculation. Third, financial crises are genuinely difficult to predict because the system is complex and many of its internal relationships are non-linear and chaotic. Financial history resembles an Andean horizon: not a smooth upward curve but a jagged series of peaks and valleys, shaped by an evolutionary process in which new instruments and institutions emerge, compete, and sometimes go extinct.

Chapter 1: Dreams of Avarice

Moneyless societies have been a perennial utopian dream, from communist theorists to religious reactionaries. But even a brief acquaintance with real hunter-gatherer peoples—like the Nukak-Makú who emerged from the Colombian rainforest—reveals the grim reality: without money and trade, subsistence is brutal and violence is endemic. The Inca Empire offers a more sophisticated example of a moneyless economy built on forced labour and central planning. When Francisco Pizarro conquered Peru in 1532, what he sought was not land or labour but precious metal—silver above all—because silver could be converted into money, and money was portable power.

The mountain of Potosí in what is now Bolivia embodied the Spanish Empire's most potent monetary fantasy: a hill of solid silver ore. Between 1556 and 1783 it yielded 45,000 tons of pure silver, worked by indigenous conscripts and African slaves under conditions that Augustinian monks described as a mouth of hell. Yet all this silver ultimately weakened Spain. The so-called price revolution of 1540–1640 demonstrated that an increase in the money supply, if not matched by an increase in goods, merely drives up prices. The Spanish crown had failed to grasp a fundamental truth: money is not metal—it is trust inscribed. Its value is whatever someone else will give in exchange for it.

Earlier and more durable foundations for money were laid in ancient Mesopotamia, where clay tablets recorded loans of grain and silver five thousand years ago. These tablets functioned as promissory notes—the direct ancestors of the modern banknote, which still bears the promise to "pay the bearer on demand." What mattered was not the medium (clay, silver, paper, or electronic digits) but the credibility of the promise. The Babylonian family bank of the Egibi, operating across five generations, illustrated that sophisticated credit networks could flourish long before coinage. The concept of compound interest itself appears in Babylonian mathematical exercises from the reign of Hammurabi.

When European commercial life revived after the dark ages, it did so partly by borrowing the mathematical tools of the Islamic world. The Pisan mathematician Fibonacci, in his 1202 Liber Abaci, introduced Hindu-Arabic numerals, decimals, fractions, and the calculation of present value to Europe—giving merchants the tools to price loans and convert currencies. The Venetian ghetto then illustrated the cultural obstacles to lending: Jews were the principal moneylenders because Christian canon law condemned usury, forcing credit to the social margins. Shakespeare's Merchant of Venice captures three enduring realities: the power of lenders to charge extortionate rates in primitive markets, the importance of courts in resolving disputes, and the vulnerability of minority creditors to hostile majorities. Glasgow loan sharks charging 25 per cent a week—over eleven million per cent annually compounded—demonstrate that these dynamics persist wherever formal banking is absent.

The Medici of Florence showed how banking could transcend the loan-shark model. Starting as currency changers at a street bench, Giovanni di Bicci de' Medici built an empire of related partnerships diversified across cities. The key innovations were bills of exchange (allowing interest to be disguised as currency profit), diversification among branch partnerships, and meticulous double-entry bookkeeping. The bank earned 32 per cent annual returns with just seventeen employees. Banking power translated directly into political power: by the mid fifteenth century, Cosimo de' Medici was, as Pope Pius II noted, "king in everything but name." The Medici story established a template for the entire subsequent history of high finance: banks do not merely hold money—they create it, and in doing so they accumulate the influence to shape states.

Chapter 2: Of Human Bondage

After the creation of credit by banks, the birth of the bond market was the second great revolution in the ascent of money. Governments issue bonds to borrow from a broader range of lenders than banks alone; the bond market in turn sets long-term interest rates for the entire economy. When bond prices fall, yields rise, raising costs for every borrower from home-buyers to sovereign states. James Carville's famous remark—that he wanted to come back as the bond market because "you can intimidate everybody"—captures the political reality: a government that loses the confidence of bond investors faces a painful trio of choices: default, spending cuts, or tax rises.

The bond market was born in the city-states of medieval Italy, where perpetual war with hired condottieri like the Essex mercenary Sir John Hawkwood drove Florence into deficits that multiplied one hundredfold between 1300 and 1427. The solution was the monte comune: wealthier citizens were forced to lend money to their own government, receiving interest in return. Because these forced loans (prestanze) could be sold on a secondary market, they were liquid assets—effectively the world's first tradeable government bonds. The system worked because the people who issued the bonds were largely the same people who bought them: oligarchical power and financial interest were aligned. Venice developed a parallel system of prestiti, whose price tracked military fortune with remarkable fidelity—crashing from 80 to 10 during the worst years of the Ottoman wars.

Northern European towns and then national monarchies developed annuities (rentes) as an alternative route around the Church's usury ban. But absolute rulers repeatedly defaulted: Spain suspended payments to creditors nine times between 1557 and 1662. Britain's Glorious Revolution of 1688 created the institutional foundation that made reliable public borrowing possible: parliamentary oversight of royal finances, an end to currency debasement, and eventually the Consolidated Fund and permanent national debt. French royal finance, by contrast, continued on a hand-to-mouth basis of debasements, sold offices, and opaque accounts—a divergence that would prove fatally consequential for the Bourbon monarchy.

The Rothschilds turned the bond market into a global system. Nathan Mayer Rothschild's audacious gamble after Waterloo—buying British consols at their nadir when everyone else was selling, and holding them until prices rose 40 per cent—exemplified the family's strategy: using superior information networks and nerve to profit from sovereign risk. Between 1815 and 1859 the London house issued more than half of all sovereign bonds sold in Britain. Their insistence that most bonds be denominated in sterling and interest paid in major financial centres set the template for modern international finance. The Rothschilds also demonstrated the ultimate ambiguity of the bond market's power: those who make money financing wars gain a stake in peace, eventually becoming a force for financial stability rather than conflict.

The American Civil War illustrated how access to bond markets could determine the outcome of wars. The Confederacy's attempt to back bonds with cotton—an ingenious form of commodity-linked debt—ultimately failed when New Orleans fell to Union forces in April 1862, severing investors' ability to take physical possession of the collateral. Without a functioning international bond market, the Confederacy printed 1.7 billion unbacked paper dollars, generating 4,000 per cent inflation by war's end. The Union's greenbacks, by contrast, retained roughly half their gold value. The fall of New Orleans, not Gettysburg, was the true financial turning point of the Civil War.

Chapter 3: Blowing Bubbles

The joint-stock limited-liability company was the third great innovation in the ascent of money. It solved a fundamental problem: long-distance ventures requiring large capital outlays and years before profits materialize could not be financed by individuals or partnerships alone. The Dutch VOC (United East India Company), chartered in 1602, was the world's first publicly owned corporation with shares traded on a secondary market. Its capital of 6.45 million guilders—raised from 1,143 subscribers, including servants and artisans—dwarfed its nearest rival, the English East India Company. Crucially, shareholders could neither demand their money back nor vote on major decisions, compelling the VOC to create the first real stock market as the only exit route.

The VOC's share price traced a century-long ascent from 100 to a peak of 786, tracking the rise of Dutch commercial empire. This was not a bubble: it reflected genuine profitability, sustained dividends, and military dominance of the Asian spice trade. The contrast with John Law's Mississippi Company is stark. Law, a convicted Scottish murderer and financial genius who ended up controlling France's entire monetary system, orchestrated the first true stock-market bubble. His System fused a monopoly trading company, a central bank issuing paper money, and the management of the entire French national debt into a single entity under his personal control. By late 1719, Mississippi shares had risen from 500 to over 10,000 livres; millionaires were coined and the rue Quincampoix thronged with dukes and princes speculating in shares.

The bubble collapsed because Law's bank printed money to finance the purchase of its own shares, generating inflation that eroded confidence in both the currency and the equity. When he tried to arrest the decline with a deflationary decree in May 1720, public outcry forced its reversal within six days. Law fled France; the share price fell to 1,000 livres by December. The damage extended far beyond personal fortunes. Law's bubble fatally set back France's financial development, putting Frenchmen off paper money and stock markets for generations. The French monarchy's fiscal crisis remained unresolved, lurching through abortive reforms until royal bankruptcy precipitated revolution. The contemporaneous South Sea Bubble in Britain was less destructive precisely because the South Sea Company never gained monopoly control over the Bank of England.

Stock markets embody a five-stage cycle common to all financial bubbles: displacement (new profitable opportunities arise), euphoria (rising prices reinforce optimism), mania (first-time investors and fraudsters pile in), distress (insiders sell), and revulsion (the bubble bursts). Three conditions recur across historical bubbles: asymmetric information between insiders and outsiders, cross-border capital flows that amplify speculation, and easy credit creation by central banks. The Wall Street crash of 1929 confirmed this framework at catastrophic scale. Milton Friedman and Anna Schwartz established that the Federal Reserve's failure to counter banking collapses—allowing a contraction of credit by 47 per cent between 1929 and 1933—turned a correction into the Great Depression. The lesson: central banks must act aggressively as lenders of last resort.

Enron, lauded as America's Most Innovative Company for six consecutive years before its 2001 bankruptcy, was the Mississippi Company reborn. Like Law, Ken Lay exploited political connections, financial innovation (mark-to-market accounting, off-balance-sheet special-purpose entities), and a loose monetary environment to sustain an elaborate fraud. The Federal Reserve's low interest rates of the 1990s inflated Enron's stock price just as Law's Banque Royale had inflated Mississippi shares. The Dutch East India Company remains the counter-example: built on sound money from the Amsterdam Exchange Bank, genuinely profitable trade, and gradually reformed corporate governance, its share price rose steadily over a century without a bubble. Sound money and transparent governance are the preconditions for equity markets that create real wealth rather than financial illusions.

Chapter 4: The Return of Risk

The most basic financial impulse is to save against an uncertain future. But even the most sophisticated insurance systems periodically fail catastrophically, as Hurricane Katrina demonstrated in 2005. Nearly three-quarters of New Orleans's housing stock was damaged; 1.75 million property and casualty claims were filed; and disputes between private wind insurers and the federal flood programme left hundreds of thousands of policy-holders without coverage. The episode revealed a deeper tension: private insurance companies retreat from zones of high risk, yet governments are reluctant to extend public insurance indefinitely. The history of insurance is a long struggle between the desire for financial security and the hard reality that the future contains irreducible uncertainty as distinct from calculable risk.

Modern insurance rests on six mathematical breakthroughs of the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries: Pascal and Fermat's probability theory; Graunt and Halley's life tables; Bernoulli's Law of Large Numbers; de Moivre's normal distribution; Daniel Bernoulli's concept of utility; and Bayes's method of inference. It took clergymen to turn theory into practice. Two Church of Scotland ministers, Robert Wallace and Alexander Webster, used Halley's life tables in 1744 to create the world's first actuarially sound insurance fund for the widows and children of ministers. Their projections for the fund's growth over seventeen years were accurate to within one pound. This Scottish Ministers' Widows' Fund became the template for modern insurance, pension funds, and the institutional investors who now dominate global capital markets.

As private insurance proved unable to cover the needs of the poorest citizens, welfare states emerged. Bismarck introduced compulsory state health insurance and old-age pensions not from altruism but from political calculation: "A man who has a pension for his old age is much easier to deal with than a man without that prospect." Japan carried the warfare-welfare state furthest: coverage expanded almost a hundredfold during the Second World War, and by the 1970s Japan had the world's longest life expectancy and most equal income distribution, while spending only 9 per cent of national income on social security—roughly half the British level. The welfare state proved most effective when embedded in a culture of social conformity and familial support.

The crisis of welfare states in the 1970s opened the door to radical privatization of risk. Chile's Pinochet regime, advised by Milton Friedman and the "Chicago Boys," introduced Personal Retirement Accounts in 1981—mandatory private pension savings managed by competing fund management companies. Over 70 per cent of Chilean workers voluntarily switched from the state system; the annual rate of return exceeded 10 per cent; the domestic savings rate rose to 30 per cent of GDP. The reform also created a new class of property-owners with a stake in market stability. Chile's experience was imitated across Latin America and beyond. Yet the same logic ultimately reaches its limit: the poorest, the self-employed, and the casually employed remain outside the system, reliant on a minimum government guarantee.

At the apex of risk management stand hedge funds and financial derivatives. Futures contracts, which originated in Chicago's grain markets, allowed farmers to lock in prices at planting time. Options, swaps, and weather derivatives extended this principle to financial risks of every kind. The total notional value of over-the-counter derivatives reached $596 trillion by late 2007. But hedging requires capital: the financial world has effectively divided into the hedged—large corporations and wealthy individuals who can afford to buy protection—and the unhedged, who rely on relatively blunt insurance policies or the welfare state. The same technological sophistication that allows elite hedge funds to profit from volatility leaves ordinary households with no protection beyond insurance and savings in an unhedged bet on a single asset: their home.

Chapter 5: Safe as Houses

The English-speaking world's obsession with property ownership is not a natural cultural trait but a deliberate political creation, born from the Great Depression. Before the 1930s, fewer than two-fifths of American households owned their homes; mortgages were short-term, non-amortizing, and regionally restricted. When the Depression struck, over half a million foreclosures occurred in 1932 and 1933 alone. The Roosevelt administration's response was institutional transformation: the Federal Housing Administration introduced federally backed long-term, fully amortizing, low-deposit mortgages; the Home Owners' Loan Corporation refinanced distressed borrowers; and Fannie Mae created a national secondary market for mortgages in 1938. These policies raised homeownership from 40 per cent to 60 per cent by 1960 and made residential investment a principal engine of US growth.

But the property-owning democracy was racially exclusive. Red-lining—the Federal Home Loan Bank Board's practice of marking predominantly black neighbourhoods with a D rating and denying them access to federally backed mortgages—meant that black Americans paid significantly higher interest rates and were systematically excluded from wealth accumulation. The Detroit riots of 1967 were partly an expression of rage at this financial discrimination. Only after the Civil Rights Acts and the Community Reinvestment Act of 1977 did lenders come under legal pressure to serve minority communities. The political impetus to extend home ownership, however well-intentioned, gradually removed the discipline that sound mortgage lending requires.

The Savings and Loan crisis of the 1980s was the first warning that deregulated property finance could become catastrophically self-destructive. S&Ls were freed to invest in anything, pay any deposit rate, and raise unlimited brokered deposits—while their deposits remained federally insured up to $100,000. The result was a classic case of moral hazard: operators like Danny Faulkner in Dallas ran land-flip schemes in which parcels bought for $3 million were sold days later for $47 million, with loans provided by the same S&L. The final cost to taxpayers was $153 billion. Yet from the wreckage of S&L insolvency, Wall Street firms extracted opportunity. Lewis Ranieri's invention of mortgage-backed securities—securitization—converted pools of mortgages into tradeable bonds, dismantling the relationship between lender and borrower and beginning the process that would end in 2007.

Property is commonly believed to be a one-way bet. The evidence over two decades is more ambiguous: American stocks consistently outperformed property in both the UK and the US between 1987 and 2007, and house prices can fall sharply—by 18 per cent in Britain between 1989 and 1995, and by over 60 per cent in Japan between 1990 and 2000. The subprime crisis that began in Detroit illustrated the fatal flaw of treating property as a substitute for pensions and insurance. Subprime ARMs—adjustable-rate mortgages with teaser rates that reset sharply after two years—were sold to households with no income, no job, and no assets. Lenders pocketed origination fees and immediately sold the loans on to Wall Street, which bundled them into CDOs and sold tranches rated triple-A to pension funds in Norway and Australia. When the teaser periods expired and rates reset, defaults cascaded through the system. The subprime butterfly flapped its wings in Detroit and triggered a global financial hurricane.

Chapter 6: From Empire to Chimerica

Global capital flows are not a new phenomenon. In the three decades before 1914, British investors held assets equivalent to 150 per cent of UK GDP abroad, and nearly half of all securities quoted on the London Stock Exchange were foreign bonds. The city-states of Italy, the Dutch Republic, and Victorian Britain each developed financial systems that enabled overseas investment at unprecedented scale. The first age of financial globalization was, however, inseparable from empire: the enforcement of property rights, the rule of law, and debt contracts depended on political and military power. Jardine Matheson's opium trade, backed by the Royal Navy, exemplifies the coercive underside of Victorian international finance. China suffered a "great divergence" from Western living standards partly because its financial system—unified imperial control, easy money printing, and coinage surpluses from trade—never developed the competitive capital markets that drove European growth.

The optimism of Edwardian investors proved catastrophically misplaced. Norman Angell's 1910 argument that a war between great powers had become economically impossible—because of the "delicate interdependence of our credit-built finance"—was refuted within four years. The assassination of Franz Ferdinand triggered a financial crisis that sucked liquidity from global markets within days. The world's major stock exchanges closed for up to five months; exchange rates gyrated; the bill market seized up. British investors who held gilt-edged securities to 1920 suffered inflation-adjusted losses of 46 per cent; holders of Austrian, Ottoman, and Russian bonds lost everything. The first age of globalization had taken a generation to build; it was blown apart in days. Two generations were required to repair the damage.

The Bretton Woods system (1944–1971) resolved the post-war "trilemma" by fixing exchange rates and permitting capital controls, deliberately sacrificing free capital mobility for domestic policy autonomy. When Nixon severed the dollar's link to gold in 1971 and petrodollars began to recirculate through Western banks in the 1970s, a second age of globalization began. The IMF and World Bank became the enforcers of the Washington Consensus—fiscal discipline, market interest rates, trade and capital liberalization—imposing conditionality on debtor nations from Latin America to East Asia. George Soros's hedge fund, by shorting the pound in 1992 and forcing sterling out of the European Exchange Rate Mechanism, demonstrated that speculative capital—mobilizing more than the Bank of England's entire reserves—could overpower sovereign monetary policy.

Long-Term Capital Management embodied the hubris of quantitative finance. Founded by Nobel laureates Myron Scholes and Robert Merton, LTCM applied the Black-Scholes options pricing formula across 7,600 simultaneous positions, borrowing at 19-to-1 leverage on the assumption that diverse strategies could not all go wrong simultaneously. By August 1998, after the Russian debt default, correlations across markets converged toward one—precisely the scenario the models said was vanishingly unlikely. LTCM lost $550 million on a single day, 15 per cent of its capital. The Federal Reserve Bank of New York orchestrated a $3.625 billion bail-out by fourteen banks. The lesson: models trained on five years of data miss the fat tails of financial history—the 1929 crash, the 1917 Russian default, the 1914 crisis.

The resolution of the post-2000 financial boom lay in Chimerica—the symbiosis between China's export-driven savings surplus and America's debt-financed consumption. China accumulated trillions in dollar reserves, lending them back to the United States at suppressed interest rates. This "savings glut" was the underlying cause of the housing bubble, the hedge fund explosion, and the flood of capital into subprime CDOs. By 2007 the United States needed to borrow $800 billion a year; China's current account surplus covered more than a quarter of it. But Chimerica contained structural tensions: currency manipulation allegations, commodity price spikes driven by Chinese demand, and the risk that a deterioration in US-China political relations could replicate the breakdown of the first age of globalization in 1914. The symbiosis that looked like a marriage made in heaven may prove to be nothing more than a chimera.

Afterword: The Descent of Money

Financial history is the result of four millennia of institutional evolution. Money begat banks; banks begat bond markets; bond markets begat equity markets; equity markets enabled insurance funds and pension funds; and from the twentieth century, derivatives and securitization extended the frontier of financial innovation into new territories. Economies that combined all these institutions consistently outperformed those that did not, because financial intermediation allocates capital more efficiently than feudalism or central planning. The Western financial model spread first through imperialism, then through globalization—not because of ideological imposition alone, but because it worked.

Yet the ascent has never been smooth. Three fundamental reasons account for the persistent instability of financial systems. The first is radical uncertainty: as Frank Knight distinguished in 1921, most of what matters in economic life is not calculable risk but genuine uncertainty, for which no historical frequency distribution exists. Keynes extended this point: the prospect of a European war, or interest rates twenty years hence, admits no scientific basis for probability calculation. Financial models that treat uncertainty as calculable risk are systematically blind to the tail events that matter most. The second reason is human psychology: behavioural finance has demonstrated a catalogue of cognitive biases—availability bias, hindsight bias, overconfidence, confirmation bias, loss aversion—that cause real investors to behave nothing like the rational agents of neoclassical economics. A loss carries roughly two and a half times the psychological weight of an equivalent gain.

The third reason is evolutionary dynamics. Financial history resembles biological evolution: institutions mutate, compete for resources, and face extinction. New species of financial firm—hedge funds, private equity partnerships, sovereign wealth funds—emerge and proliferate; old species like savings and loan associations and state-owned banks contract or disappear. Schumpeter's "creative destruction" is not merely a metaphor but a structural feature of capitalist finance. Crises function as evolutionary selection events, eliminating unfit institutions. The danger arises when regulators prevent this selection by declaring institutions "too big to fail": the Japanese banking sector of the 1990s, kept alive despite insolvency, became an economic dead hand that suppressed growth for a decade.

The 2007–2008 crisis was not an unprecedented rupture but the latest iteration of a pattern as old as the Medici. Defaults on subprime mortgages by low-income households in Detroit and Memphis destroyed the value of triple-A-rated CDOs sold to pension funds in Norway; Northern Rock suffered the first British bank run since 1866; Bear Stearns was sold at fire-sale price in a deal underwritten by the Federal Reserve. Write-downs of over $318 billion had been acknowledged by May 2008, with perhaps twice that amount still concealed. The evolutionary analogy remains apt: like past forest fires in the markets, incredible new growth is likely to spring up in the wake of the crisis. The trajectory of financial history, despite its jagged and irregular peaks, remains unquestionably upward—provided the mirror of finance is allowed to reflect both the beauty and the blemishes of humanity's endless struggle to manage an uncertain future.
