The Blank Slate

The Modern Denial of Human Nature

by Steven Pinker

Preface

Pinker opens by noting that most readers assume the nature-vs-nurture debate has been settled through reasonable compromise—"both matter." But the passages he quotes as examples of this supposed middle ground actually come from three of the most vilified books of the decade: The Bell Curve, The Nurture Assumption, and A Natural History of Rape—books whose authors were picketed, shouted down, and condemned in Congress simply for suggesting that heredity plays any role in behavior. The paradox reveals the book's central theme: in modern intellectual life, acknowledging that human nature exists has been treated as a morally dangerous act, not a scientific hypothesis to be evaluated on its merits.

Pinker clarifies his actual position: he is not arguing that genes are everything and culture is nothing. Rather, he seeks to expose why the extreme environmentalist position is treated as moderate, while the genuinely moderate position—that nature and nurture interact in complex ways—is treated as extreme and even immoral. The refusal to acknowledge human nature, he argues, distorts science, corrupts public policy, and perpetuates intellectual dishonesty. This book aims to unsnarl the moral and political assumptions that have turned human nature into a taboo subject.

Counterpoints

Critics have argued that Pinker caricatures the positions of his opponents, few of whom actually hold a strict "blank slate" view. Scholars like David Buller (Adapting Minds) and Steven Rose have contended that evolutionary psychology's claims often outrun its evidence, and that the "taboo on human nature" is less pervasive than Pinker suggests. The book's rhetorical framing—presenting moderate hereditarian views as embattled by unreasonable opposition—has been criticized as itself politically motivated, stacking the deck before the empirical argument begins.

Chapter 1: The Official Theory

Pinker introduces the three interlocked doctrines he calls the "official theory" of human nature dominating modern Western intellectual life. The Blank Slate (tabula rasa) holds that the mind at birth has no innate content or organization—everything we are is inscribed by experience and culture. The Noble Savage, derived from Rousseau, holds that humans in their natural state are peaceful and virtuous, and that civilization is the source of greed, violence, and inequality. The Ghost in the Machine, rooted in Descartes's dualism, holds that the mind is separate from the body—a free, autonomous agent unconstrained by biology.

These three doctrines, the chapter argues, are not independent; they reinforce each other and together form a secular faith. If the mind is blank, then human nature can be remade by changing institutions. If humans are naturally good, then evil is always a product of corrupt social arrangements, never of the creatures themselves. If the mind is free of biology, then moral responsibility is possible and social engineering can succeed. Pinker traces these ideas from ancient philosophy through John Locke, Rousseau, and Descartes, showing how they became the default assumptions of 20th-century social science, progressive politics, and the humanities.

Chapter 2: Silly Putty

Using the Danish linguist Otto Jespersen's openly sexist and racist scholarship as a starting point, Pinker traces how 20th-century intellectuals overcorrected against the excesses of eugenics, Social Darwinism, and colonialism. The reaction was understandable: the biological determinism of the Victorian era had been used to justify horrific injustices, and the Nazis' ideology of racial hierarchy culminated in genocide. In this context, rejecting biology seemed morally urgent. Figures like Franz Boas argued that observed racial and cultural differences were products of history, not heredity. His student Margaret Mead claimed to have found cultures (in Samoa) where sexual jealousy and adolescent turmoil were absent—proving, she argued, that these were cultural artifacts, not human universals.

Behaviorists like John B. Watson and B.F. Skinner went further, insisting that all behavior could be explained as learned responses to environmental stimuli, making concepts like innate personality, instinct, or mental structure scientifically unnecessary. The result, Pinker argues, was the "silly putty" vision of human psychology—a formless substance that social forces can mold into any shape. This was intellectually convenient for progressive politics but, as subsequent research would show, empirically untenable.

Counterpoints

Some scholars have defended Boas and Mead more charitably than Pinker allows. While Derek Freeman's rebuttal of Mead's Samoan research is widely cited, anthropologists like Paul Shankman have argued Freeman's critique was itself methodologically flawed and ideologically motivated. More broadly, critics note that the overcorrection Pinker describes was not mere intellectual error but a rational response to the misuse of biology in colonial and genocidal projects—a context that Pinker's narrative somewhat underweights.

Chapter 3: The Last Wall to Fall

Four converging fields of science are dismantling the last conceptual barrier between biological and cultural explanations of human behavior. Cognitive science, born from the 1950s "cognitive revolution," introduced the computational theory of mind: beliefs, memories, desires, and reasoning can be understood as information processing in a physical system, bridging the ancient gap between mind and matter without requiring a ghostly soul. Neuroscience has identified dedicated brain circuits for language, face recognition, social reasoning, and emotion—structures that are present cross-culturally and are not learned from scratch.

Behavioral genetics, using twin and adoption studies, has demonstrated that personality traits, intelligence, and many behavioral tendencies are substantially heritable—not infinitely malleable by parenting or education. Evolutionary psychology has provided the theoretical framework: the mind contains numerous psychological mechanisms shaped by natural selection to solve specific adaptive problems. Together, these fields are filling in the Blank Slate, showing that cultural variation is real but constrained by universal features of a mind that is far from empty at birth.

Counterpoints

Critics including David Buller and Cordelia Fine have argued that evolutionary psychology's specific claims about mental modules are often empirically weak—based on studies with small samples, unreplicated findings, and flexible methodology. The field has faced a replication crisis. Pinker's presentation of these four fields as uniformly converging on his conclusions is disputed; many researchers within cognitive science and neuroscience reject strong innatist interpretations of their work. The concept of the massive modularity of mind, central to evolutionary psychology, remains controversial among cognitive scientists.

Chapter 4: Culture Vultures

The sociological tradition, associated with Emile Durkheim and the anthropologist A.L. Kroeber, treats culture as a domain governed by entirely different laws from individual psychology or biology—"social facts" that exist at a different level and cannot be reduced to individual minds. This tradition dominated 20th-century social science and created the academic firewall between the humanities and the natural sciences. Pinker argues that the firewall is misconceived: culture is not independent of psychology but is made possible by it.

Cultural transmission depends on evolved mental capacities for imitation, language, social learning, and the ability to infer other people's intentions and beliefs. Ideas spread because minds are already equipped with preferences, intuitions, and emotional responses that make certain ideas "sticky" and others forgettable. Cultures do not write on blank slates; they propagate through minds that arrive pre-equipped with aesthetic preferences, moral intuitions, sexual jealousies, and social motivations. Far from threatening the study of culture, evolutionary psychology provides its necessary foundation.

Chapter 5: The Slate's Last Stand

Three scientific developments are sometimes cited as vindicating the Blank Slate despite the converging evidence against it. First, the Human Genome Project found only ~34,000 human genes—far fewer than the 50,000–100,000 predicted—leading some to conclude the genome is too "small" to specify innate mental structure. Second, connectionist neural network models can learn statistical patterns from input without explicit built-in rules, suggesting that a general learning mechanism might suffice for all of cognition. Third, evidence of neural plasticity—the brain rewiring with experience—is taken to imply that the brain is nearly infinitely malleable.

Pinker systematically refutes each claim. The lean genome argument confuses genes with what they produce: genes have complex regulatory and combinatorial properties that allow far more biological complexity than a simple count suggests. Connectionist networks don't actually work without built-in architectural biases and learning rules—they have innate structure of their own. Neural plasticity is real but radically over-interpreted: all learning necessarily changes the brain, but that trivial fact says nothing about whether the brain can be reorganized into any configuration. The evidence, on the contrary, shows that plasticity operates within tight genetically specified constraints.

Counterpoints

Cognitive scientists sympathetic to developmental systems theory, such as Annette Karmiloff-Smith, argue that Pinker understates how much of mental structure emerges from the interaction of general neural mechanisms with structured experience. Critics of the modularity thesis note that neural plasticity research—including cases of dramatic cortical reorganization following sensory deprivation—does suggest more flexibility than Pinker's framework allows. The debate between "nativist" and "constructivist" accounts in cognitive development remains genuinely open.

Chapter 6: Political Scientists

Part II opens by documenting the politically motivated attacks on scientists who explored biological influences on behavior. Pinker recounts the campaigns against E.O. Wilson (whose 1975 Sociobiology argued that human social behavior has evolutionary roots), Richard Herrnstein (who argued that IQ is partly heritable), Arthur Jensen, and others. These attacks went far beyond scholarly disagreement: they included disruption of lectures, coordinated smear campaigns, and efforts to have research programs defunded or shut down. A left-wing "radical science movement," energized by 1970s Marxism, portrayed any biological explanation of human behavior as ideologically equivalent to Nazism.

The chapter documents specific episodes: a group led by Richard Lewontin and Stephen Jay Gould published a manifesto against sociobiology in The New York Review of Books; a colleague poured water over Wilson at a scientific meeting. Napoleon Chagnon's field research on the Yanomamö, showing that violence played a significant role in their society, was met with a coordinated campaign to discredit him personally. Pinker frames these episodes not as aberrations but as symptoms of a systematic conflation of political ideology with scientific inquiry—a "moral exhibitionism" that continues to shape academic culture.

Counterpoints

Historians of science like Ullica Segerstrale have offered more nuanced accounts of the sociobiology wars, in which both sides had genuine scientific disagreements alongside ideological ones. The "radical scientists" were often raising legitimate methodological objections about adaptationist just-so stories, inadequate experimental controls, and the misuse of scientific authority in policy debates. Anne Fausto-Sterling and others have argued that the charges of political motivation were not one-sided—that sociobiology and evolutionary psychology also attracted adherents partly because their conclusions resonated with existing political preferences.

Chapter 7: The Holy Trinity

Why do intellectuals cling so tenaciously to the Blank Slate, the Noble Savage, and the Ghost in the Machine even as scientific evidence accumulates against them? Pinker argues these three doctrines function as moral scaffolding rather than empirical hypotheses. The Blank Slate appears to guarantee political equality (if we are all blank, we are all equal). The Noble Savage appears to guarantee the possibility of social progress (if humans are naturally good, evil can be eliminated by fixing institutions). The Ghost in the Machine appears to guarantee moral responsibility and human dignity (if the mind is free from biology, people cannot be excused by their genes).

Examining key figures like Stephen Jay Gould, Richard Lewontin, and Leon Kamin, Pinker shows that while they formally deny believing in a strict blank slate, they effectively defend one whenever specific empirical claims are at stake—granting only trivial concessions to biology while insisting that the interesting variation in human behavior is culturally determined. He also addresses the contemporary religious right, which opposes evolutionary psychology for its own reasons: the fear that a Darwinian account of human nature would undermine the ground for morality and divine purpose.

Chapter 8: The Fear of Inequality

The first of four "fear" chapters examines the worry that acknowledging innate human differences would justify discrimination, Social Darwinism, and eugenics. The logic runs: if groups differ biologically in intelligence or temperament, then discrimination against inferior groups could seem rational; if social inequality reflects innate differences, the victims can be blamed rather than helped; and if human traits can be biologically improved, governments might try to do so by coercive means. This reasoning led many intellectuals to insist that human nature must not exist—because if it did, innate differences would be possible.

Pinker argues that this line of reasoning is itself dangerous, because it makes fundamental moral values contingent on a factual claim that could be empirically overturned. The right to equal treatment, he argues, should rest on the intrinsic dignity of persons regardless of their measured capacities—not on the claim that people are biologically identical. He distinguishes between factual equality (the empirically false claim that all people have identical talents) and moral equality (the ethical principle that all people deserve equal rights and equal consideration), arguing that only the latter is both defensible and required by liberal values.

Counterpoints

Philosophers like Elizabeth Anderson and Norman Daniels have argued that Pinker underestimates how easily biological claims about group differences are co-opted in policy debates. Even if moral equality does not logically require factual equality, the history of scientific racism shows that claims about innate differences have repeatedly been used to rationalize discrimination in ways that formal philosophical distinctions cannot prevent. Feminist scholars have also pointed out that the fields Pinker defends have a poor track record of separating the study of differences from the endorsement of them.

Chapter 9: The Fear of Imperfectibility

The second fear: if aggression, selfishness, tribalism, and sexual jealousy are innate, then social reform is futile and the human condition cannot be improved. This fear takes two forms: a practical concern that innate flaws make progress impossible, and a normative concern that labeling these traits "natural" would imply they are good or unavoidable. Pinker traces this anxiety to the Romantic tradition, which believed in the perfectibility of human nature and saw any suggestion of fixed limits as defeatist or dangerous.

He counters with two arguments. First, "natural" does not mean "inevitable"—humans routinely use technology, culture, and institutions to transcend or redirect biological tendencies. We do not tolerate smallpox simply because it is "natural," nor do we abandon eyeglasses because poor vision is part of human variation. Second, evolutionary psychology does not say humans are inevitably violent; it says we have psychological mechanisms that produce violence under some conditions and cooperation under others. Understanding those conditions is the precondition for effective social intervention, not its negation.

Counterpoints

Critics have noted that Pinker's response, while logically sound, does not fully engage with the sociological argument. If institutions systematically suppress expression of certain traits, then showing that traits are "innate" can still function politically as an argument against institutional reform—regardless of what the logic technically allows. Anne Fausto-Sterling and others have argued that evolutionary psychology's "innate tendencies" claims are often poorly defined, making it unclear what would count as "transcending" them and what would count as futile resistance to fixed human nature.

Chapter 10: The Fear of Determinism

The third fear: if behavior is caused by genes, brain states, and evolutionary history, then free will is an illusion and moral responsibility collapses. Pinker draws on philosopher Dan Dennett's compatibilist account to argue that what we actually care about in free will—the capacity to reason, to be influenced by incentives and moral norms, to adjust behavior based on reflection—is fully compatible with physical causation in the brain. An uncaused, truly free agent would actually be worse for morality, not better: it would be immune to deterrence, shame, and moral education, making responsibility and punishment pointless.

The chapter also addresses the fear that biology will be used to excuse criminal behavior—the "my amygdala made me do it" defense. Pinker argues that biological explanations of behavior do not eliminate responsibility; they help us understand the conditions under which people act as they do, which can inform more effective prevention, treatment, and just punishment. The relevant distinction is not between "free" and "determined" actions, but between actions that are responsive to reasons, incentives, and norms and those that are not.

Chapter 11: The Fear of Nihilism

The final fear: if the mind is a product of evolution and brain processes, does life lose its meaning and morality its foundation? Pinker addresses both the religious and secular versions of this worry. The religious version, articulated by Pope John Paul II, holds that if the "spiritual soul" did not emerge by an ontological leap distinct from natural processes, human dignity and the possibility of a relationship with God would be undermined. The secular version fears that a purely biological account of moral intuitions reveals them as arbitrary evolutionary adaptations rather than genuine guides to value.

Pinker argues that neither version of nihilism follows from biological naturalism. The fact that pain and pleasure are products of brain states does not make them less real or less morally relevant. The fact that the moral sense has evolutionary origins does not make moral reasoning a fiction—just as the fact that perception evolved does not make the external world a fiction. Moreover, a secular ethics grounded in the reality of sentient beings' interests, their capacity for suffering and flourishing, and the logic of reciprocity is fully capable of generating robust moral norms without requiring supernatural foundations.

Chapter 12: In Touch with Reality

Part IV opens by defending the human capacity to access objective reality against the postmodern and social constructionist movements that dominated parts of academia in the late 20th century. According to strong versions of social constructionism, science studies, and deconstructionism, there is no objective truth independent of cultural and political presuppositions—all claims to knowledge are exercises of power. Pinker identifies this movement as a natural extension of the Blank Slate: if the mind is empty, it can only recapitulate the ideological assumptions it has absorbed, making genuine knowledge impossible.

He defends the view that our evolved cognitive faculties do provide real, if imperfect, access to the world. Categories, generalizations, and even probabilistic stereotypes are not inherently tools of oppression—they are features of a mind that evolved to track the structure of its environment. The chapter distinguishes between stereotypes as probabilistic generalizations (which can be accurate and updated with evidence) and prescriptions for how to treat individuals (which remain wrong regardless of group-level statistics). Relativism about truth, Pinker argues, is self-defeating: it makes it impossible even to identify lies and misrepresentations, let alone correct them.

Counterpoints

Pinker's treatment of postmodernism has been criticized as a strawman. Few serious scholars actually deny the existence of an external world; strong social constructionism is a minority position even within the humanities. More mainstream standpoint epistemologists like Sandra Harding and Donna Haraway make the more defensible claim that positionality affects what questions scientists ask and which data they consider significant—not that truth is purely a social construction. Critics argue Pinker conflates sophisticated critiques of scientific authority with crude relativism.

Chapter 13: Out of Our Depths

Our cognitive faculties evolved to handle the challenges of ancestral environments: small groups, concrete objects, face-to-face social relationships, and immediate sensory experience. They are poorly suited to many demands of modern life. The chapter presents a taxonomy of cognitive modules: intuitive physics, intuitive biology, intuitive psychology (a "theory of mind"), spatial reasoning, number sense, probabilistic reasoning, intuitive economics, a mental database and logic, and language. Each is based on a core intuition suited to its ancestral domain but prone to systematic errors when applied beyond it.

People systematically misunderstand statistical probability (confusing frequencies with probabilities, falling prey to the base-rate fallacy and availability heuristic), are poor at reasoning about exponential growth and large numbers, and struggle with the counterintuitive findings of physics and economics. These limitations are not evidence for the Blank Slate—a general-purpose blank mind would do no better. They are exactly what a structured mind adapted to a specific ancestral environment would predict: sophisticated in its native domains, confused in novel ones. Acknowledging this is the first step toward building better institutions, education, and decision-making processes that compensate for our cognitive limits.

Chapter 14: The Many Roots of Our Suffering

Drawing on the evolutionary biologist Robert Trivers's elegant theoretical framework, this chapter explains why human social life is inevitably laced with conflict—not because people are malicious, but because evolved interests, even among those who love each other, are not perfectly aligned. Kin selection explains family love but also sibling rivalry and parent-offspring conflict: parents and children share half their genes, so their optima for parental investment differ, creating tension that expresses itself as the universal struggle of children for more than parents are "designed" to give. Reciprocal altruism explains friendship and cooperation but generates vulnerability to defection, cheating, and the psychology of revenge when obligations are violated.

Sexual selection produces romantic love and devotion but also jealousy, infidelity, and domestic conflict, because males and females face different reproductive asymmetries in investment and certainty of parenthood. Even self-deception has evolutionary logic: Trivers argued that deceiving others is easier if you deceive yourself first, so the brain may systematically bias our beliefs in self-serving directions. This does not mean these conflicts are inevitable in every individual relationship, but understanding their evolutionary logic helps explain why they are so persistent and so resistant to purely cultural solutions.

Counterpoints

Evolutionary accounts of family and sexual conflict have been criticized for presenting the outcomes of specific cultural arrangements as if they were biological necessities. Anthropologists point to variation in kinship systems, alloparenting practices, and sexual arrangements across cultures that complicate the standard model. Critics like Sarah Blaffer Hrdy have offered evolutionary accounts of cooperation and alloparenting that are less conflict-centered than Trivers's framework, suggesting the darker picture of human sociality is not the only evolutionarily coherent one.

Chapter 15: The Sanctimonious Animal

The moral sense is not a reliable guide to objective ethics; it is an evolved psychological mechanism prone to systematic error—a "gadget," in Pinker's memorable phrase. Drawing on the psychologist Jonathan Haidt's research on moral dumbfounding, the chapter shows that people routinely condemn acts they cannot coherently explain as harmful (such as a thought experiment involving consensual sibling incest with no consequences), suggesting that moral judgments often precede and do not require moral reasoning. Haidt's model identifies multiple moral "foundations": harm and care, fairness and reciprocity, in-group loyalty, respect for authority, and purity and sanctity.

These foundations can fire in ways that are not defensible by any rational standard, just as optical illusions reveal that vision can systematically deceive us. Moral progress, Pinker argues, consists in part of reasoning our way out of the snap judgments of our evolved moral sense when they lead us astray—overriding in-group favoritism with universal concern, rejecting purity-based condemnations of harmless acts, and separating genuine harm-based ethics from the many other triggers of moral outrage. Acknowledging that the moral sense has evolutionary roots does not make it infallible; on the contrary, it makes its failures predictable and potentially correctable.

Counterpoints

Philosophers including Jesse Prinz and Sharon Street have pushed back on the evolutionary debunking of moral intuitions. If our moral sense evolved, they argue, it might track fitness rather than moral truth—but Pinker's response, that reasoning can correct the evolved moral sense, still presupposes that some moral judgments are more defensible than others, which requires a standard of moral truth not obviously provided by evolutionary biology itself. The claim that "moral progress" is real—not just changing preferences—is harder to justify on a purely naturalistic account than Pinker acknowledges.

Chapter 16: Politics

Political attitudes are substantially heritable: identical twins raised apart show a correlation of .62 in political orientation, suggesting that much of the left-right divide reflects innate temperamental differences rather than purely learned convictions. Liberals and conservatives differ, on average, in personality traits like openness to experience, conscientiousness, and tolerance of uncertainty—traits with clear genetic components. This explains why political arguments so often feel futile: people are not reasoning their way to political conclusions but rather rationalizing pre-existing emotional responses.

More substantively, the chapter maps evolutionary psychology onto political philosophy. The Darwinian view of sociality falls naturally within the social contract tradition (individuals are prior to society, which arises from mutually beneficial agreements) rather than the sociological tradition (society is an organic whole, individuals are merely its parts). This alignment makes evolutionary psychology seem politically conservative. Pinker argues the alignment is incomplete: both left and right have selectively co-opted science of human nature for their purposes, and recognizing innate human tendencies can equally support progressive policies designed to counteract them.

Counterpoints

The heritability of political attitudes is real but contested in its interpretation. Critics note that the traits associated with conservatism and liberalism vary by culture and era, suggesting that what is inherited is a general temperamental disposition, not a specific ideological orientation. Kathryn Paige Harden and others in behavioral genetics have cautioned against reading heritability of attitudes as evidence for the rightness or naturalness of any particular political position. The chapter's alignment of evolutionary biology with individual-centered social contract theory has also been challenged by evolutionary accounts that emphasize group selection and cooperative social norms.

Chapter 17: Violence

One of the book's most empirically rich chapters examines violence through an evolutionary lens. Pinker identifies three Hobbesian causes of conflict: competition (fighting over resources, mates, or status), diffidence (preemptive strikes driven by mutual distrust—what Pinker calls the Hobbesian trap), and glory (aggression to build and defend reputation). Contra the Noble Savage myth, evidence from hunter-gatherer archaeology, primatology, and anthropology consistently shows that violence is not a modern invention. Rates of violent death in pre-state societies, per capita, were often far higher than in modern states.

The chapter also challenges the popular theory that media images or cultural narratives are the primary drivers of violence, reviewing evidence that their effects are modest. Pinker examines specific forms of violence—homicide, warfare, rape, child abuse, and domestic violence—showing that each has distinctive evolutionary logic rooted in reproductive competition, resource acquisition, or parental investment calculus. Understanding this logic does not excuse violence; it enables more targeted and effective interventions than the vague prescription to change culture or reduce exposure to violent media.

Counterpoints

Anthropologists including Brian Ferguson have challenged the archaeological and ethnographic evidence for high rates of pre-state violence, arguing that many of the data points Pinker cites are contested, and that much documented violence among hunter-gatherers occurred after contact with state societies. Douglas Fry's research on nomadic forager societies argues they are generally peaceful. On the "declining violence" thesis that Pinker develops more fully in The Better Angels of Our Nature, critics like John Gray argue the trend is fragile, recent, and driven by contingent political factors rather than any deep biological or cultural ratchet.

Chapter 18: Gender

Acknowledging the profound historical injustice done to women and the ongoing importance of feminist scholarship, Pinker argues that the politically motivated denial of innate sex differences has obscured rather than advanced women's interests. The Three Laws of Behavioral Genetics apply as much to sex differences as to individual differences: cognitive profiles differ on average (men show higher average scores on mental rotation and certain spatial tasks; women on verbal memory and social cognition), and these differences are substantially heritable. Differences in risk-taking, physical aggression, and mate preferences (women tending to prefer status and resources, men tending to prefer youth and physical attractiveness) are found across cultures and show signs of evolutionary origin.

Crucially, Pinker argues that acknowledging these differences is compatible with—indeed required by—an honest feminism. The feminist project of equal opportunity does not depend on the empirical claim that men and women are psychologically identical; it depends on the moral claim that individuals deserve to be evaluated as individuals, not stereotyped by group membership. Moreover, denying real differences can have perverse effects: if the gender gap in engineering is entirely due to discrimination, the cure is a quota; if it partly reflects different average preferences, then equal opportunity may not produce equal outcomes, and forcing equality of outcome may harm the very women it purports to help.

Counterpoints

Cordelia Fine's Delusions of Gender and Testosterone Rex provide extensive critiques of the evidence for innate cognitive sex differences, arguing that many studies are methodologically flawed, their effect sizes are tiny, and the role of socialization is consistently underestimated. Fine and others argue that neurosexism—the tendency to interpret ambiguous neuroscientific findings as confirming sex differences—is pervasive in the field. The cross-cultural universality of mate preferences cited by evolutionary psychologists has also been challenged by studies showing much greater variation in gender-relevant behaviors across societies than the standard model predicts.

Chapter 19: Children

The Three Laws of Behavioral Genetics, as formulated by psychologist Eric Turkheimer, are presented here as among the most robust and underappreciated findings in all of psychology. The First Law: all human behavioral traits are substantially heritable. The Second Law: the effect of being raised in the same family is smaller than the effect of genes. The Third Law: a substantial portion of the variation in complex human traits is not accounted for by the effects of genes or families.

The Second Law has radical implications for parenting: the massive research literature correlating parenting styles with child outcomes is largely uninformative, because parents provide both genes and environment to their biological children, making it impossible to determine which is driving observed correlations. When researchers properly separate genetic and environmental contributions using adoption studies, parenting style turns out to have surprisingly little measurable effect on adult personality. Judith Rich Harris's provocative "peer group hypothesis" proposes that peers, not parents, are the primary socializing force outside the home. The Third Law points to unique individual experiences, developmental randomness, or gene-environment interactions as the dominant shaper of who we become—a finding that is both humbling and liberating for parents anxious about optimizing their children.

Counterpoints

Harris's peer group hypothesis and the dismissal of parenting effects have been vigorously contested. Jay Belsky and others have conducted longitudinal studies showing measurable effects of parenting quality—particularly in extreme cases like harsh discipline or sensitive caregiving—on attachment, emotional regulation, and adult outcomes. Critics argue that behavioral genetic methods measure average parenting effects across a narrow range of environments; the studies were largely conducted on middle-class Western families, and effects of poverty, abuse, and neglect are not well captured. The Third Law's "non-shared environment" component may partly reflect gene-environment interactions that behavioral genetics cannot easily disentangle.

Chapter 20: The Arts

Contrary to the laments of cultural critics, the arts as a whole are flourishing: symphony concerts, art museums, bookstores, libraries, and independent film have all grown. What is in trouble is a narrow slice of high-modernist art—atonal concert music, abstract expressionism, experimental literary fiction—that lost touch with its audience. Pinker traces this disconnect to a specific intellectual error: the belief, derived from the Blank Slate, that aesthetic pleasure is a social construction with no relation to our perceptual and emotional faculties. This led avant-garde artists to deliberately reject the features—melody, narrative, representation, beauty—that our evolved aesthetic responses actually respond to.

Pinker proposes that a renewal of the arts would come from reconnecting with what he calls human aesthetic universals: pleasure in melody and harmony, engagement with narrative and character, response to representations of landscape and the human face, emotional resonance with music that tracks the prosody of speech. These are not arbitrary cultural preferences but expressions of evolved perceptual and emotional faculties. This does not mean art should simply pander to the lowest common denominator; it means that artists who ignore human nature entirely will find their work admired by a dwindling circle of fellow initiates while the broader culture looks elsewhere.

Counterpoints

Pinker's account of modernism has been sharply criticized by art scholars and critics. His claim that modernism failed because it violated evolutionary aesthetic preferences ignores the historical, economic, and institutional forces that shaped 20th-century art. Many modernist works—from jazz to abstract expressionism to literary modernism—found and retained large audiences. Critics including Arthur Danto have argued that Pinker's aesthetic naturalism cannot account for the meaning and significance of art, which involves historical and institutional context that evolutionary psychology cannot capture. The chapter is also criticized for conflating the decline of specific institutions (subsidized classical music, academic literary fiction) with a failure of evolutionary aesthetics.

Part VI: The Voice of the Species

The closing section synthesizes the book's argument and confronts a worry that haunts it: if we abandon the Blank Slate, are we abandoning the moral ideals it seemed to protect? Pinker argues the answer is no—and that the Blank Slate has itself had a dark side that its defenders rarely acknowledge. The doctrine's insistence that human nature is infinitely malleable was precisely what made it attractive to totalitarian regimes: if people are blank slates, they can be written on by the state. Soviet and Maoist social engineering, which resulted in tens of millions of deaths, was premised on the view that the "new socialist man" could be created through the right institutional conditions.

Abandoning the Blank Slate does not mean accepting inequality, excusing cruelty, or surrendering the aspiration to a better world. It means grounding our moral commitments—in human dignity, the reality of suffering, the value of individual liberty—on a more honest foundation than an empirically false doctrine about the nonexistence of human nature. The book ends on a note of measured hope: that intellectual life, reconnected with science and with the human realities that novelists and poets have always taken for granted, can speak more truthfully and serve human flourishing more effectively than it did when it was in the grip of the Blank Slate's comforting but ultimately corrosive illusions.
