The Essays of Warren Buffett

Lessons for Corporate America, Fifth Edition

Warren E. Buffett & Lawrence A. Cunningham (editor)

Introduction

Lawrence A. Cunningham's introduction explains that The Essays of Warren Buffett is a thematically organized compilation drawn from Buffett's annual letters to Berkshire Hathaway shareholders. Rather than reprinting the letters chronologically, Cunningham rearranges them into a coherent manual on management, investment, finance, and accounting. The central theme is that the principles of fundamental business analysis—originally formulated by Buffett's teachers Ben Graham and David Dodd—should guide both investment and corporate management practice.

The introduction surveys Berkshire Hathaway's extraordinary record: from a $19 book value per share in 1965 to over $200,000 per share, compounding at roughly 19% annually. Cunningham describes Berkshire as a holding company with five "groves": a dozen insurance companies generating investable float, scores of operating subsidiaries, a concentrated common stock portfolio, vast cash equivalents, and a handful of investment partnerships. This structure, Buffett argues, results not from a master plan but from focused investing—concentrating on businesses with outstanding economics run by first-rate managers.

Cunningham emphasizes that Buffett's owner-related business principles—treating shareholders as partners, retaining earnings only when doing so creates at least a dollar of value, using debt sparingly, and issuing stock only for equal value received—are the organizing framework for everything that follows. The introduction also previews each of the book's ten thematic sections, from governance and investing to accounting and taxation, setting the stage for the essays themselves.

Prologue: Owner-Related Business Principles

Buffett opens with fifteen numbered principles that define Berkshire's relationship with its shareholders. The foundational idea is that, despite corporate form, Berkshire operates with a partnership mentality: Buffett and Munger view shareholders as owner-partners and themselves as managing partners. Nearly all of both men's personal wealth is invested in Berkshire stock, which they describe as "eating our own cooking."

The principles cover a remarkable range of commitments. Berkshire will measure success by per-share intrinsic value, not total size. It prefers to own diversified, cash-generating businesses outright but will also buy minority stakes at attractive prices. It will use debt sparingly, issue stock only when receiving equal value, retain earnings only when reinvestment delivers at least $1 of market value per $1 retained, and never fill a "managerial wish list" at shareholders' expense. Buffett pledges that accounting consequences will never drive capital-allocation decisions—he would rather buy $2 of unreported earnings than $1 of reported earnings.

Two principles stand out for their candor. Principle 11 acknowledges that Buffett has no interest in selling any good business Berkshire owns, regardless of price—a policy that may slightly penalize returns but preserves relationships and culture. Principle 12 commits to complete candor in reporting: "We will always tell you how many strokes we have taken on each hole and never play around with the scorecard." These principles remain the moral backbone of Berkshire and are reprinted in every annual report.

Counterpoints

The owner-partnership model works elegantly when the controlling partner (Buffett) is both highly capable and perfectly aligned with minority shareholders. Critics note that Berkshire's structure—with no independent compensation committee, no stock splits, and no dividends for decades—relies almost entirely on the personal integrity of its founder. Agency theory scholars would point out that the same structural features that made Berkshire exceptional under Buffett could become liabilities under a less scrupulous successor. The explicit promise to "never sell a good business" may also bind future managers to suboptimal capital allocation in ways that erode long-term returns.

I. Governance

The Governance section opens with Buffett's conviction that managers are stewards of shareholder capital and that the best of them think like owners. He argues that most corporate annual meetings are theatrical wastes of time, while Berkshire's meetings—where he and Munger will answer any question for hours—model what genuine owner-manager dialogue looks like. Buffett insists on full and fair disclosure: Berkshire's annual report is written as if its only reader were an intelligent partner who deserves the same information the CEO uses to run the company. He specifically condemns "selective disclosure," EBITDA cheerleading (calling it a practice that implies the tooth fairy pays for capital expenditures), and CEOs who make long-term earnings predictions.

On boards and managers, Buffett draws a sharp distinction among three governance situations: companies with a controlling owner-manager (where boards are largely powerless), companies with a controlling but non-managing shareholder (where boards can act), and widely-held companies (the most common and most problematic case). In the last situation, he argues that "independence" as typically defined is nearly meaningless: directors whose income depends heavily on board fees are the least independent of all. True independence, Buffett quotes Matthew 6:21, comes from having a substantial financial stake. He advocates small boards of business-savvy, shareholder-oriented outsiders who hold regular meetings without the CEO to evaluate his performance.

The section also addresses stock options (which Buffett calls a form of compensation that creates asymmetric alignment—managers share upside but not downside), the closure of Berkshire's textile business (a painful lesson in the futility of capital investment in a commodity business facing structural decline), the economics of newspapers in the internet age (once "indestructible slot machines," now in irreversible decline), and the "social compact" Berkshire maintains with its regulated utilities and railroad businesses—accepting heavy capital obligations in exchange for fair regulated returns. A final essay on corporate charity explains Berkshire's unique shareholder-designation program, which allowed owners to direct contributions to their chosen causes rather than having management choose for them.

Counterpoints

Buffett's critique of stock options is well-founded but his alternative—cash bonuses tied to individual performance—faces its own measurement problems. In complex conglomerates, defining an individual manager's "own business" and fairly attributing results across interdependent units is notoriously difficult. Some compensation researchers (e.g., Jensen and Murphy) argue that equity-like incentives, even imperfectly designed options, remain necessary to align managers with long-horizon value creation. There is also a counterargument that Buffett's extreme confidence in CEO selection as the primary governance mechanism places too much weight on the judgment of one person—himself—who admits to having made hiring mistakes and to staying silent too often in boardrooms.

II. Investing

The Investing section is Buffett's extended rejoinder to modern finance theory. He argues that the core tenet of efficient market theory—that thinking about individual securities is a waste of time—has led a generation of MBAs and portfolio managers to systematically mismeasure risk. Beta, the theory's preferred risk measure, cannot distinguish a hula-hoop company from a Monopoly franchise, and it perversely labels a stock "riskier" after it falls in price. For Buffett, the relevant risk is not price volatility but permanent loss of capital, which depends on the quality of the underlying business and the price paid.

Drawing on two personal examples—a Nebraska farm purchased in 1986 and a Manhattan retail property bought in 1993—Buffett illustrates that the ideal investment mind-set is identical whether the asset is a farm, real estate, or a stock. The investor should focus on likely future earnings from the asset, pay a price well below that value, and then ignore short-term price fluctuations entirely. He describes the stock market's daily quotations as a neighbor who shouts a price at which he will buy or sell—useful only when irrational, never as a guide for the investor's own judgment.

The section introduces Mr. Market, Ben Graham's allegory of an emotionally unstable business partner who offers daily buy-sell prices reflecting his mood rather than business fundamentals. Buffett endorses Graham's conclusion: Mr. Market is your servant, never your guide. A third pillar is the circle of competence—the instruction to make investments only in businesses one can genuinely evaluate. Finally, Buffett describes risk arbitrage—the short-term use of excess cash to exploit announced corporate events—as a disciplined, commonsense practice that requires answering four questions about probability, timing, opportunity cost, and downside before each commitment.

Counterpoints

Buffett's dismissal of modern finance theory is persuasive as a critique of its extremes but arguably overstated as a general principle. Eugene Fama and others would note that the preponderance of evidence shows that most active managers—including Buffett's own example of the hedge-fund bet—underperform passive indices net of fees, which is precisely what efficient market theory predicts. Buffett's own extraordinary record may reflect a genuinely rare combination of skill, temperament, deal access (negotiated acquisitions unavailable to most investors), and the structural advantages of running a large insurance float rather than a pure stock portfolio. Survivorship bias further complicates how representative his results are for investors attempting to replicate the approach.

III. Alternatives

This section surveys the landscape of investment alternatives available to Berkshire's insurance companies, and develops Buffett's framework for choosing among them. He identifies three broad categories: currency-based investments (bonds, money market instruments), sterile assets that produce nothing (gold being the primary example), and productive assets (businesses, farms, real estate). His analysis is deliberately unflattering to the first two categories.

Currency-based instruments, Buffett argues, are "among the most dangerous of assets" because governments control the value of money and systemic forces periodically lead to inflation that destroys purchasing power. He notes that over the 47 years since he took over Berkshire, the dollar lost 86% of its value—making a tax-exempt investor need 4.3% annual interest just to break even in real terms. Gold receives an even sharper critique: Buffett imagines all the world's gold melded into a 68-foot cube worth $9.6 trillion and asks whether any rational investor would prefer that to the same dollar amount invested in all U.S. cropland plus 16 Exxon Mobils. Gold "will not respond" to being fondled, while productive assets compound in perpetuity.

The section also covers junk bonds (where Buffett acknowledges occasional attractive opportunities but warns that their higher default rates are often underestimated because academic studies use historical data that may not reflect future recessions), zero-coupon bonds (whose useful property of locking in compound rates was exploited by progressively weaker issuers in a classic Wall Street pattern: "what the wise do in the beginning, fools do in the end"), and derivatives—which Buffett famously labeled "financial weapons of mass destruction." His painful firsthand experience unwinding the derivatives book inherited with General Re illustrated their complexity, hard-to-value nature, and the systemic contagion risk he had warned about years before the 2008 financial crisis.

Counterpoints

Buffett's dismissal of gold as an investment ignores its role as a portfolio diversifier and tail-risk hedge. Research by Claude Erb and Campbell Harvey suggests that gold maintains its purchasing power over very long periods (centuries) even if it underperforms equities over decades. For investors without Buffett's ability to identify outstanding businesses or negotiate favorable terms, gold may serve a legitimate function that purely equity-focused frameworks overlook. His critique of junk bonds also sits awkwardly alongside Berkshire's own profitable investments in distressed debt during downturns—the same asymmetry he describes in others.

IV. Common Stock

The Common Stock section opens with Buffett's famous maxim: "We simply attempt to be fearful when others are greedy and to be greedy only when others are fearful." The section develops his long-standing argument that the primary enemy of investment returns is not market risk but frictional costs—the commissions, spreads, management fees, and advisor charges that accumulate as investors trade. Using the "Gotrocks family" allegory, he shows how a group of people who collectively own all of American business will inevitably earn less than the businesses themselves earn, the gap being siphoned off by an ever-growing army of financial intermediaries. He estimates total frictional costs at roughly 20% of aggregate corporate earnings.

His prescription: for most investors, a low-cost S&P 500 index fund will outperform the vast majority of actively managed alternatives over time. Buffett put $500,000 behind this conviction in a celebrated ten-year bet against a fund-of-funds manager, which he won decisively: the S&P 500 index fund gained 125.8% over 2008–2017, while the five funds-of-funds averaged gains of 36.3%. He notes the brutal irony that the manager-helpers received roughly 60% of all gains achieved, even as their investors experienced a "lost decade."

For Berkshire specifically, the section covers the rationale against stock splits (which increase transaction costs, attract short-term traders, and cause prices to depart from intrinsic value), conditions under which share repurchases make sense (only when the stock trades at a meaningful discount to intrinsic value), and dividend policy (Berkshire has not paid a dividend in decades because retained earnings have consistently produced more than $1 of market value per dollar retained). He also describes Berkshire's creation of Class B shares—at 1/1,500th the economic rights of Class A—as a defensive move to prevent imitation products that would have imposed costs on shareholders while misleading investors about Berkshire's true economics.

Counterpoints

Buffett's index-fund recommendation applies to investors who lack his own demonstrated ability to identify and evaluate outstanding businesses. The recommendation implicitly concedes that Buffett himself is an exception—but it does not tell investors how to identify whether they too are exceptions. Behavioral economists such as Terrance Odean and Brad Barber have documented that individual investors systematically underperform even index funds due to overtrading and poor timing, suggesting the practical challenge is less "which strategy" and more "executing any strategy with discipline." Buffett's anti-split stance is also disputed: some evidence suggests that high nominal share prices reduce retail investor participation and liquidity in ways that can slightly depress valuations.

V. Acquisitions

The Acquisitions section opens with Buffett's diagnosis of why most corporate acquisitions destroy value. Three unspoken motives drive most high-premium takeovers: the thrill of the deal, the desire for enhanced size (measured by sales, not profitability), and excessive optimism about synergies ("the toad-kissing problem"—CEOs who believe their managerial kiss will transform a mediocre business into a prince). After making several value-destroying acquisitions early in his career, Buffett revised his approach: "buy good businesses at fair prices rather than fair businesses at good prices."

Berkshire's acquisition policy is the double-barreled approach: buy parts or all of businesses with excellent economic characteristics run by managers Buffett likes, trusts, and admires. The section includes an extended analysis of the economics of stock-for-stock mergers, arguing that issuing shares to buy another business is equivalent to "selling part of yourself." When Berkshire's stock trades below intrinsic value, issuing it is like paying $2 to acquire $1 of value—a deal that may be immediately anti-dilutive to earnings per share while still being deeply destructive to intrinsic value per share. He notes that Berkshire's own stock acquisitions have, in aggregate, slightly reduced shareholder wealth, while cash acquisitions (from National Indemnity onward) have been enormously value-creating.

Buffett describes Berkshire's structural advantages as an acquirer: the willingness to keep all employees and cultures intact, permanent rather than leveraged ownership, and a track record that sellers can verify by calling every previous Berkshire acquisition target. He explicitly positions Berkshire as an alternative to the two standard options for family-business owners selling—sale to a competitor (who will fire associates) or to a private equity buyer (who will pile on debt and flip the business as "merchandise"). Berkshire offers a permanent home, and this positioning has become a durable competitive advantage in deal sourcing.

Counterpoints

Buffett's critique of acquisition premiums is well-documented empirically: studies by Richard Roll and others confirm that acquiring firms on average destroy value, with the "winner's curse" driving overpayment in competitive auctions. However, critics note that Berkshire's own acquisition record is not without blemishes (Buffett himself acknowledges buying Berkshire Hathaway was his costliest mistake). The "permanent home" offer, while appealing to certain sellers, also means Berkshire rarely exits underperforming subsidiaries—creating a portfolio drag that large private equity firms can avoid through active portfolio management. Whether perpetual ownership is truly a better model depends heavily on the quality of the initial selection and on the durability of the competitive moats Buffett identifies.

VI. Valuation

The Valuation section builds a practical toolkit for understanding business value. The organizing principle, borrowed from Aesop, is that valuation is simply counting cash: "a bird in the hand is worth two in the bush." To apply this formula, one must estimate how many birds are in the bush (future cash flows), when they will emerge (timing), and what risk-free rate to use as a discount factor. Buffett calls this his "Inefficient Bush Theory"—the observation that occasionally, even very conservative estimates of future cash flows reveal that an asset is startlingly cheap relative to its quoted price.

The section distinguishes sharply between three measures of value. Intrinsic value—"the discounted value of the cash that can be taken out of a business during its remaining life"—is the only economically meaningful concept, but it is neither easy nor objective to calculate. Book value is easily measurable but often misleading: in 1964, Berkshire's book value overstated its worth because assets were tied up in a failing textile business; today, book value substantially understates it because many controlled businesses are worth far more than their carrying values. Market price fluctuates around intrinsic value but can deviate substantially in either direction.

Two particularly important concepts are introduced. Look-through earnings—Berkshire's net earnings plus its proportionate share of undistributed earnings in major investees—provide a more economically accurate picture of what the enterprise is earning than GAAP-reported figures alone. Owner earnings—operating earnings plus depreciation minus required reinvestment capital (capex needed to maintain competitive position)—correct a common Wall Street error of treating cash flow (which ignores required reinvestment) as a proxy for earning power. For most businesses, required reinvestment exceeds depreciation, making cash flow analysis optimistic and owner earnings analysis more conservative and accurate.

Counterpoints

The intrinsic value framework is theoretically unassailable but practically difficult to apply rigorously. Aswath Damodaran and other valuation scholars note that DCF models are highly sensitive to terminal growth rate assumptions, and that small changes in discount rates or long-term growth assumptions produce wildly different outputs. This "garbage in, garbage out" problem means the framework is most powerful as a filter—identifying clearly cheap or clearly expensive situations—rather than as a precise calculator. Buffett's concept of "owner earnings" also requires judgments about required maintenance capex that are difficult for outside analysts to make reliably, particularly in capital-intensive industries where management has discretion over when to defer spending.

VII. Accounting

The Accounting section opens with a grudging acknowledgment that Buffett would hate to have to devise a better set of rules than GAAP—but proceeds to demonstrate, with considerable wit, just how badly GAAP can mislead investors. The centerpiece is a satire written by Ben Graham in the 1930s, presented in mock-announcement form, in which the fictional U.S. Steel Corporation unveils a "Modernization Scheme" of six accounting maneuvers—writing plant to minus $1 billion, paying wages in stock options, carrying inventory at $1, and establishing a $1 billion "Contingency Reserve"—that would produce magnificent reported earnings without a single real change in operations. The satire remains perfectly apt: accounting chicanery of essentially the same kind recurs in every era.

Buffett identifies two main categories of GAAP manipulation. The first involves well-intentioned managers who stretch accounting conventions to present figures they believe are more accurate—"white lie" techniques such as earnings smoothing or the "big bath" charge that dumps losses into a single restructuring quarter to clear the decks for future earnings beats. The second is deliberate fraud: managers who know that investors treat GAAP results as gospel and exploit that trust through technically compliant but economically misleading presentations. Buffett's mordant observation: "It has been far safer to steal large sums with a pen than small sums with a gun."

Particular attention is given to two abuses. EBITDA as a valuation metric is "particularly pernicious" because it implies depreciation is not a real economic cost—when in fact it represents cash already spent on assets being consumed. Stock option expensing is addressed with a three-sentence logical argument: if options aren't compensation, what are they? If compensation isn't an expense, what is? If expenses shouldn't affect earnings calculations, where should they go? The refusal of many corporate managers and accountants to expense options, Buffett argues, reveals either innumeracy or self-interest.

Counterpoints

Accounting standard-setters have addressed some of Buffett's concerns: FASB ASC 718 now requires expensing of stock options at fair value, and goodwill impairment testing has replaced mechanical amortization schedules. However, critics of mark-to-market accounting (including Buffett himself in insurance contexts) argue that forcing immediate recognition of unrealized gains and losses introduces its own form of earnings volatility that may mislead rather than inform. The debate between historical cost and fair value accounting reflects genuine tension between reliability and relevance—and reasonable people can disagree about which regime better serves investors in different circumstances.

VIII. Taxation

The Taxation section argues that tax consequences, while often overlooked, have a powerful effect on investment returns—and that Berkshire's buy-and-hold strategy is not just philosophically sound but also tax-advantaged. Buffett frames deferred capital gains taxes as an interest-free loan from the U.S. Treasury: by not selling appreciated securities, Berkshire essentially borrows the government's 35% share of the unrealized gain at zero cost, reinvesting it for compounding returns. The longer the holding period, the more powerful this effect.

Using the Li'l Abner parable—in which a character following advice to "double your money 20 times" would net only $22,370 after taxes if realizing each gain separately, versus ~$130 million by holding a single investment that compounds to the same total—Buffett illustrates the mathematical power of long-term compounding without tax friction. He also explores which businesses bear corporate tax increases versus pass them through to customers, concluding that businesses with strong franchise characteristics (pricing power) absorb most of the benefit from tax cuts and most of the burden from tax increases themselves, while purely competitive businesses pass both through to prices.

The section closes with Buffett's frank acknowledgment that Berkshire is among the largest corporate taxpayers in the United States—paying roughly 2.5% of all U.S. corporate income taxes—and with his blunt public critique of the declining share of federal revenue borne by corporations (from a post-war peak of 32% in 1952 to around 7.4% by 2003). He expresses no complaint about his own tax bill ("my class is clearly winning") while calling on other large corporations to pay their fair share.

IX. History

The History section is Buffett's meditation on what he calls The American Tailwind—the extraordinary economic force that has propelled American business and investors for nearly 240 years. Drawing on essays written from 2015 to 2018, Buffett argues that much of Berkshire's own success is simply a product of operating within the most productive economic system in human history, and that humility about this fact is both accurate and appropriate.

The section traces American productivity growth through several industries: farming (from 30 bushels per acre in 1900 to over 150 today, with the farm workforce shrinking from 40% of all workers to 2%), railroads (carrying 182% more freight ton-miles today than in 1947 with 86% fewer workers), and auto insurance (where GEICO's direct-distribution model drove a fundamental disruption of agency-centric incumbents). These examples illustrate a pattern: innovation creates enormous societal wealth while inevitably displacing workers, and the appropriate response is not to restrain productivity improvements but to build social safety nets for those left behind.

In the "Tailwinds" essay, Buffett notes that $114.75 invested in a no-fee S&P 500 index fund in March 1942—when war news was alarming and pessimism was rampant—would have grown to $606,811 by 2019, a gain of 5,288-fold. Had a fearful investor instead bought gold as "protection" against runaway deficits and currency debasement, that same sum would be worth about $4,200. The lesson: betting against America has been a losing wager for 240 years, and pessimists who market gloomy forecasts do well only if they avoid acting on their own predictions.

Counterpoints

The "American Tailwind" argument, while empirically supported over long horizons, risks becoming circular: the United States benefitted from unique geographical advantages, the absence of destructive wars on its soil, the rule of law, and a particular configuration of institutions that are not easily replicated. Historians such as Barry Eichengreen note that other countries with similar institutional frameworks have produced comparable returns for long-term equity investors. More importantly, extrapolating the 20th-century U.S. experience forward as a reliable guide to 21st-century returns ignores structural changes including demographic aging, higher starting valuations, and the possibility of institutional degradation—risks Buffett himself acknowledges but treats as manageable rather than disqualifying.

X. Coda

The book's closing section presents parallel retrospectives from Buffett and Munger on the occasion of Berkshire's 50th anniversary in 2015. Buffett's essay, "Buffett on Berkshire Culture," argues that conglomerates have a well-deserved terrible reputation—built by 1960s promoters who used pooling accounting and p/e arbitrage to manufacture earnings without creating any real value—but that Berkshire's conglomerate structure is genuinely advantageous when used judiciously. The core advantage is rational, low-cost capital reallocation: Berkshire can move money from businesses with limited reinvestment opportunities to those with greater promise without incurring taxes, transaction costs, or the institutional biases that trap most single-industry managers.

Buffett describes the five qualities that make Berkshire "ideally positioned for life after Charlie and I leave the scene": an unmatched collection of businesses with favorable economics, outstanding and devoted subsidiary managers, extraordinary financial strength and liquidity, first-choice status among sellers of outstanding businesses, and a distinctive culture embedded in its people over fifty years. He is candid about the future's limits: Berkshire's percentage returns will inevitably diminish as the capital base grows, and the company will eventually need to distribute earnings rather than reinvest them. His instructions for the successor CEO emphasize character above all: the new leader must be "all in" for the company, not for himself, and must vigilantly fight the "ABCs of business decay—arrogance, bureaucracy, and complacency."

Munger's parallel essay, "The Berkshire System," offers a more systematic and drier analysis of the same subject. He catalogs the specific elements Buffett put in place—extreme managerial autonomy, no headquarters bureaucracy, permanent ownership, no stock options, and casualty insurance as the central financing mechanism—and diagnoses why each element contributed to Berkshire's success. Munger credits four factors for Berkshire's achievement: the constructive peculiarities of Buffett himself, the constructive peculiarities of the Berkshire system, good luck, and the "weirdly intense, contagious devotion" of shareholders and admirers. He predicts that the system, now self-reinforcing and culturally embedded, will ensure Berkshire "will remain a better-than-normal company for a very long time."

Counterpoints

Succession risk at Berkshire remains a genuine concern that both Buffett and Munger treat somewhat optimistically. The "Buffett premium"—the extra market value attributable to the founder's reputation, deal access, and relationship network—is substantial and largely non-transferable. Academic studies of founder-led companies post-succession (Fahlenbrach et al.) show mixed results, with some maintaining outperformance and others reverting to industry norms. Munger's emphasis on the system's stability is perhaps most accurate for the operating subsidiaries, which have genuine autonomy; it may be more fragile at the portfolio level, where Buffett's unique ability to source and evaluate deals under compressed timelines has been a defining competitive advantage that no organizational design can fully replicate.
