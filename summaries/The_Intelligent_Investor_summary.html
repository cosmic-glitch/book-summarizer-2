The Intelligent Investor

A Book of Practical Counsel

Benjamin Graham

Introduction: What This Book Expects to Accomplish

The central problem of intelligent investing is not discovering a secret formula but choosing a sound policy and refusing to let the market drag that policy into speculation. The opening pages reject the fantasy that everyone can become rich through simple stock buying programs such as the 1929 claim that saving $15 a month in common stocks would automatically build a fortune. The arithmetic of later market history showed that such promises ignored valuation, timing, and long stretches of poor returns.

Investing is presented as a discipline of principles, attitudes, and history rather than a mechanical search for hot tips. The discussion gives much less weight to forecasting and much more weight to the distinction between price and value, between sound operations and speculative excitement, and between what a company does and what its shares cost. Even a powerful growth story can produce disappointing results when enthusiasm has already been fully capitalized into the quotation.

Two broad investor types shape the rest of the book. The defensive investor wants safety, simplicity, and freedom from serious error; the enterprising investor is willing to do more work in exchange for a chance at better than average results. Both are warned that common stocks contain both investment and speculative elements, that inflation and business cycles matter, and that behavior often matters more than theory.

A final caution runs through the introduction and foreshadows the whole argument: the investor's chief enemy is usually inside the mirror. The most damaging mistakes come from paying too much, following fashion, confusing a fine company with a fine investment, or treating market action as wisdom. The most durable protection comes from judgment, moderation, and a consistent standard for what constitutes safety.

Chapter 1: Investment versus Speculation: Results to Be Expected by the Intelligent Investor

An investment operation is defined with unusual strictness: it must rest on thorough analysis, promise safety of principal, and offer an adequate return. Operations that fail one of those tests belong in the speculative camp, no matter how respectable they sound. This definition matters because the language of the market habitually disguises speculation by calling nearly every purchase an investment.

Speculation is not condemned outright, but it is dangerous when practiced unintelligently. Three forms of bad speculation stand out: speculating while believing that one is investing, speculating seriously without knowledge or skill, and speculating with money that cannot be afforded to lose. Margin accounts, hot new issues, and market tips typically fall into that territory. The danger increases when rising markets make speculation feel easy and therefore respectable.

Common stocks themselves occupy an ambiguous position. They are legitimate investments when bought with proper standards, but they also contain an unavoidable speculative element because quoted prices fluctuate widely and because public attitudes toward future growth can become extreme. The intelligent response is not denial but control: keep the speculative component minor, deliberate, and emotionally manageable.

Better than average results are possible only under demanding conditions. They require methods that are both sound and unpopular, not merely energetic. The chapter distinguishes the defensive investor, who mainly needs protection from mistakes, from the enterprising investor, who may pursue superior returns through disciplined selectivity, bargain purchases, or special situations. Mere activity, confidence, or forecasting ability is not enough.

Chapter 2: The Investor and Inflation

Inflation weakens every fixed dollar claim, so it cannot be ignored by anyone holding bonds, preferred stocks, cash balances, or pensions. Yet the popular idea that common stocks automatically solve the problem is rejected as much too simple. A world with 3% annual inflation changes the arithmetic of saving and investing, but it does not magically turn every stock certificate into a perfect hedge.

Historical evidence shows that stocks have usually done better than bonds over long stretches, but the connection between inflation and stock-market success is loose and irregular. The late 1960s and the period ending in 1970 produced a sharp reminder: inflation rose, yet corporate profitability and stock prices did not keep pace in a reassuring way. Businesses face rising costs, debt burdens, and competitive pressures; therefore higher prices in the economy do not translate one-for-one into higher shareholder rewards.

The practical answer is balance, not certainty. Gold, real estate, and other supposed inflation hedges each have serious limitations. Bonds remain vulnerable, but an all-stock policy is also risky. The prudent investor should usually keep a material commitment to both stocks and bonds, recognizing that common stocks may be the lesser evil under inflationary conditions without pretending that they eliminate the problem.

Chapter 3: A Century of Stock-Market History: The Level of Stock Prices in Early 1972

A long view of market history is used as an antidote to contemporary excitement. From 1871 onward the record shows repeated booms, collapses, long stagnant periods, and changing valuation standards. Nineteen major bull and bear movements reveal a market that does not travel in a straight line and that periodically persuades each generation that current conditions are unprecedented.

Three broad phases stand out. The period from about 1900 to 1924 featured recurring cycles with recognizable limits. The next era, from the mid-1920s through the Depression and war years, brought exceptional violence in both prices and psychology. The third phase, beginning in 1949, produced the enormous postwar advance that culminated in new valuation problems by the late 1960s, followed by the severe break of 1969-1970.

This history is not offered to support market timing. Its real use is to show how valuation standards change and how easily recent experience dominates judgment. Price/earnings ratios rose, dividend yields compressed, and stocks at times sold on assumptions very different from earlier decades. The comparison between stock yields and bond yields became especially unfavorable when bond rates rose sharply while stock prices remained elevated.

By early 1972 the market did not look attractive for the conservative investor. The case was not that a crash had to happen next, but that the statistical relationship between price, earnings, dividends, and interest rates did not provide a comfortable margin. History teaches less about the precise next move than about the recurring danger of paying ambitious prices after long advances.

Chapter 4: General Portfolio Policy: The Defensive Investor

The defensive investor's first task is to decide on a stock-bond allocation rather than on a list of exciting securities. The recommended range is deliberately broad but firm: never less than 25% and never more than 75% in common stocks. This 25-75 rule, with a 50-50 division offered as the central, easy-to-maintain rule, is not claimed to be optimal in every climate; its value lies in discipline and in resistance to emotional market swings.

Rebalancing supplies the practical mechanism. If market movements carry the portfolio materially away from the chosen mix, the investor should restore approximate balance, using fluctuations as a service rather than a signal. This approach prevents the usual habit of owning the most stocks after long rises and the fewest after big declines. A drift toward something like 55-45 in one direction or the other can be enough to justify action.

The bond side of the portfolio also demands discrimination. U.S. government obligations, savings bonds, strong corporate bonds, and suitable municipal issues all have roles, depending on taxation and circumstance. By contrast, higher yields alone do not justify inferior quality. Long maturities, call features, and preferred-stock complications all require attention because a supposedly safe income instrument can behave badly when rates rise or when quality was insufficient from the start.

The chapter's deeper point is that policy precedes prediction. A workable long-term rule matters more than a convincing short-term opinion. The defensive investor wins by choosing a sensible range, keeping quality high, and making modest, periodic adjustments instead of trying to outguess the next phase of the market.

Chapter 5: The Defensive Investor and Common Stocks

The defensive investor should own common stocks, but only within a narrowed field. Four basic tests govern the selection: adequate diversification across about 10 to 30 issues, purchase of large and prominent companies, strong financial condition, and a long record of uninterrupted dividends. The aim is not brilliance but soundness.

Price discipline is equally important. A stock that meets every quality test can still be a poor investment when bought at too high a multiple. A suggested upper limit is about 25 times average earnings over seven years and not more than 20 times the latest year's earnings. These limits are meant to rule out the glamour names whose business success is real but whose quoted prices already assume too much.

The chapter also gives a practical defense of dollar-cost averaging. A study of repeated ten-year purchase programs in common stocks, ending across many difficult years between 1929 and 1952, showed that steady buying produced favorable results in every case within a reasonable period. The average indicated profit of about 21.5%, excluding dividends, was not a miracle, but it demonstrated how regularity can overcome the danger of choosing one unfortunate entry point.

Risk is redefined in a useful way. It does not mean quotation fluctuation by itself. Real risk lies in overpaying, owning weak businesses, concentrating too narrowly, or depending on future growth that may not arrive. A conservative common-stock policy therefore combines restraint in price with moderation in expectations.

Chapter 6: Portfolio Policy for the Enterprising Investor: Negative Approach

The enterprising investor begins with many of the same materials as the defensive investor, but the first lesson is still mostly negative: avoid the categories where extra effort is unlikely to be rewarded. High-grade preferred stocks are usually more attractive to institutions than to individuals, and lower-grade bonds are rarely worth the trouble unless purchased at clearly bargain prices. A modest yield premium is not enough compensation for serious quality risk.

Foreign government bonds receive a particularly cold verdict. Their long history includes repeated defaults, weak legal remedies, and unreliable protection for the outsider. The same broad skepticism applies to many new issues, which are ordinarily brought out when market conditions favor sellers and underwriters. If the public is being invited to own them enthusiastically, caution is usually the proper first response.

The negative approach therefore defines a clean field by exclusion. The enterprising investor should not spend scarce time reaching for what is fashionable, promoted, or slightly higher yielding. Better opportunities lie in neglected, unpopular, or technically awkward areas where price is clearly favorable and the arithmetic can be checked.

Chapter 7: Portfolio Policy for the Enterprising Investor: The Positive Side

Four broad fields of activity are surveyed for the enterprising investor: market timing, growth-stock selection, bargain issues, and special situations. The first two, which attract the most attention, receive the coolest assessment. Formula timing systems have not held up reliably, especially after their rules become widely known, and growth-stock investing is far more dangerous than it looks because strong past growth often tempts buyers to pay prices that leave no margin for disappointment.

The more promising fields are the less glamorous ones. One is the purchase of relatively unpopular large companies, especially when low multipliers and neglect coexist with sound finances. Another is the purchase of bargain issues selling at large discounts from appraised value. A useful working threshold is value at least 50% above price. In many instances this value comes from current assets, private-owner appraisal, or the market's neglect of dull but solid businesses.

The chapter gives particular attention to net-current-asset bargains. A study of 85 companies of this sort in 1957 showed impressive average gains with little downside when purchased as a diversified group. The logic is simple: if the stock market offers a company for less than its cash, receivables, and inventory after subtracting all liabilities, the buyer may be getting the operating business for free.

Special situations form a final positive field: mergers, liquidations, reorganizations, spin-offs, and other events where a definable transaction may close the gap between price and value. These operations demand care, probabilities, and diversification, not enthusiasm. The underlying message is consistent with earlier chapters: superior results come more often from buying what is demonstrably cheap than from forecasting what will become beloved.

Chapter 8: The Investor and Market Fluctuations

Market fluctuations are inevitable, but they need not be authoritative. The chapter sharply separates forecasting from pricing. Forecasts about the market's next major move have little dependable value for the investor, and popular systems such as the Dow Theory or simple trading formulas often fail precisely when many people begin using them.

The useful part of market movement lies in opportunity. A quoted market can at times offer a bargain purchase and at other times an overgenerous selling price, but it need not dictate action every day. The stock exchange exists to provide service, not wisdom. The investor may ignore it entirely except when it offers a clearly favorable deal.

This principle is crystallized in the famous figure of Mr. Market, an obliging but unstable business partner who appears every day with an offer to buy or sell at some price. Some days that price is sensible; some days it is absurdly gloomy or optimistic. The intelligent investor profits only by using Mr. Market's moods, never by sharing them.

The chapter also reminds the reader that even bonds can fluctuate severely when interest rates move, especially at long maturities. Price instability alone therefore does not turn an investment into a speculation. What matters is whether the quoted movement creates a practical problem or a useful opening, and whether the owner knows the difference.

Chapter 9: Investing in Investment Funds

Investment funds can be useful instruments for the defensive investor, but only after their structure and incentives are understood. Open-end mutual funds redeem shares at net asset value and often carry sales charges; closed-end funds trade in the market and frequently sell at discounts to the value of their holdings. By the end of 1970 there were 383 registered funds with about $54.6 billion in assets, so the category was already large enough to deserve careful analysis.

As a group, funds have probably served the average person better than direct common-stock speculation. They promote saving, diversify automatically, and reduce the temptation to trade or chase low-quality offerings. Yet their aggregate results have generally been close to the market averages, not materially above them. That is not a scandal; it is a reminder that professional management, large staffs, and marketing polish do not create guaranteed superiority.

Past performance can have some value, but only with qualifications. A respectable record over at least five years may indicate skill, especially if it was achieved without reckless speculation during a broad bull market. By contrast, performance funds that shoot ahead through aggressive concentration, glamour issues, or speculative new ventures often achieve their publicity before they achieve durable results.

The warning example is the cult of the performance fund in the late 1960s. Manhattan Fund gained 38.6% in 1967 against about 11% for the market index, but it loaded itself with fashionable stocks such as Teleprompter, Deltona, Polaroid, and National Student Marketing. Within a short period, some major holdings were bankrupt or near collapse, and the fund's record deteriorated badly. The episode showed how easily short-lived brilliance can be manufactured by taking risks that are invisible in good times.

Closed-end funds emerge from the chapter with a structural advantage. If a closed-end fund can be bought at a discount of 10% to 15% from asset value, while an open-end fund imposes a load of roughly 9%, the investor begins with a tangible price benefit. The discount would have to widen dramatically before that advantage disappeared. Balanced funds receive a cooler assessment because investors can usually create a sounder bond component directly for themselves.

Chapter 10: The Investor and His Advisers

Advice is unavoidable because most investors are amateurs, but advice does not create miracles. If normal returns are all that can reasonably be expected, the adviser's proper role is to protect the client from mistakes, maintain sound standards, and help secure the return to which the capital is entitled. Trouble begins when clients demand exceptional profits or advisers imply that such profits can be delivered consistently.

Investment counsel firms and bank trust departments are depicted as the most professionally modest and generally reliable advisers. Their typical practice is conservative: major companies, strong bonds, diversification, and limited ambition. Their real value often lies less in outperformance than in shielding capital from foolish ventures and speculative enthusiasm.

Financial services and brokerage research are more mixed. Their business forecasts and statistical materials can be useful, but their market predictions add little. Brokerage houses also face a conflict that cannot be ignored: commissions rise when customers trade. A broker can be helpful if the investor is explicit about wanting value-minded guidance rather than market tips, but the burden of setting that tone rests with the client.

The chapter closes with a practical concern sharpened by brokerage failures around 1969-1970. Investors are urged to use reputable firms and, for nonprofessionals, to have deliveries and payments pass through a bank when necessary. The main lesson is not institutional paranoia but role clarity: advisers can assist, analyze, and warn, yet ultimate responsibility for standards remains with the investor.

Chapter 11: Security Analysis for the Lay Investor: General Approach

Security analysis is presented as a craft with distinct tasks: describe the business, examine the accounts, identify strengths and weaknesses, estimate future earning power, and judge whether the security is safe or attractively priced. Yet the chapter repeatedly warns that the more valuation leans on distant projections and less on demonstrated history, the more fragile the result becomes.

For bonds and preferred stocks the rules are comparatively firm. A sound industrial bond should show average coverage of interest charges around 7 times before tax, railroads around 5 times, and utilities about 4 times, with corresponding alternatives based on the poorest year. Size, stock-equity cushion, and in some fields asset value also matter. The consistent message is that safety must be demonstrated, not assumed.

Common-stock analysis is less exact. The basic method values future earnings and then applies a capitalization factor, but both steps invite disagreement. The chapter reviews how services such as Value Line projected future Dow earnings and prices, showing that grouped estimates may be respectable while individual forecasts remain highly error-prone. Analysts often become most mathematical precisely where certainty is lowest.

Several factors still guide intelligent judgment: long-term prospects, management quality, financial strength, dividend record, and current dividend policy. A 20-year dividend record is treated as a meaningful sign of quality. Management deserves credit mainly when change has already begun to show up in facts, as in the turnaround of Chrysler. Strong finances and conservative capitalization protect the common stock from shocks that growth alone cannot prevent.

The chapter offers a simplified growth-stock formula: Value = Current Earnings x (8.5 + 2g), where g is the expected annual growth rate over seven to ten years. The formula is illustrative rather than final. Its real use is to expose how wildly different market expectations can become, as when the implied growth rate for Xerox was vastly higher than that for General Motors. A more robust procedure would separate past-performance value from whatever later adjustment future judgment requires.

Chapter 12: Things to Consider About Per-Share Earnings

Short-term earnings figures are influential, but they are dangerous when read naively. One warning says not to take a single year's results too seriously; the second says that if attention is paid to them, the reader must watch for accounting traps. Reported per-share figures can conceal dilution, special charges, tax effects, and policy changes that radically alter what the number really means.

The central example is ALCOA in 1970. The company reported $5.20 per share in primary earnings, $4.32 after special charges, $5.01 on a diluted basis before those charges, and $4.19 after both adjustments. For the fourth quarter alone, the contrast between $1.58 in primary earnings and $0.70 in net income shows how a casual reader could talk himself into a bargain or scare himself unnecessarily depending on which figure he saw first.

Special charges receive especially skeptical treatment. When companies charge future losses, reorganizations, closures, and contract troubles against a single already-bad year, they may clean up future statements artificially. The effect can be to bury losses in the past while allowing later results to look smoother than they really are. Depreciation choices, research-expense treatment, and inventory accounting through FIFO or LIFO can distort results in similar ways.

The corrective is to think in averages and trends. Seven- or ten-year earnings averages absorb most extraordinary items better than headline annual figures do. Growth should also be measured sensibly, such as by comparing recent three-year averages with those from a decade earlier. The point is not to eliminate judgment but to rescue it from quarterly theatrics and accounting cosmetics.

Chapter 13: A Comparison of Four Listed Companies

Four companies are placed side by side to show how radically price can diverge from ordinary business facts: eltra, Emerson Electric, Emery Air Freight, and Emhart. Emerson and Emery command high multipliers because of their growth and market prestige, while eltra and Emhart sell at much more modest levels despite respectable records of profitability, stability, and financial strength.

The comparison reviews several objective measures: profit margins, return on book value, balance-sheet position, dividend continuity, and historical growth. Emerson and Emery score impressively on recent growth; eltra and Emhart still show sound finances, long dividend records, and attractive relationships between price, earnings, and asset value. The gap in market enthusiasm is much wider than the gap in underlying quality.

The chapter's practical conclusion favors the cheaper pair for the value-minded investor. Emerson and Emery may continue to shine, but their valuations leave much less room for disappointment. eltra and Emhart better satisfy the tests of a conservative purchase: adequate size, strong current position, continuous dividends, no recent deficit, earnings growth, and moderate price in relation to both book value and average earnings.

No near-term prophecy is offered. The lesson is subtler: a switch from glamour to value is justified not because the low-multiplier stocks must outperform immediately, but because the cheaper buyer starts with a more dependable arithmetic position. Preference for value over market excitement is a policy choice, not a guess about next quarter's action.

Chapter 14: Stock Selection for the Defensive Investor

The defensive investor's stock list can be assembled either by simply holding a broad sample of leading companies or by applying explicit statistical tests to each candidate. Seven such tests are proposed. An industrial company should have at least $100 million in annual sales, a current ratio of about 2 to 1, no earnings deficit in the past ten years, and an uninterrupted dividend record of at least 20 years.

The next standards govern growth and price. Per-share earnings should have risen by at least one-third in ten years, measured by three-year averages. The current price should not exceed 15 times average earnings of the past three years, and it should not exceed 1.5 times book value. A flexible combined rule caps the product of P/E and price-to-book at 22.5.

Applying these tests to the Dow Jones Industrial Average produces only a handful of full qualifiers, including American Can, American Tel. & Tel., Anaconda, Swift, and Woolworth. The exercise shows that even among blue chips, many issues fail one criterion or another. That is not a defect of the method; it is evidence that price and quality seldom line up neatly across the whole market.

Public utilities emerge as especially suitable candidates in the period examined. Their earnings are comparatively stable, regulation gives them a durable franchise, and many sell at moderate price-to-book ratios with generous dividend yields. Financial stocks require similar arithmetic discipline, while railroads deserve caution because weak economics and chronic industry difficulties make them less necessary holdings for the conservative investor.

Chapter 15: Stock Selection for the Enterprising Investor

The enterprising investor wants more than average results, but the chapter opens with a warning: even that goal is harder than it appears. Large mutual funds with staffs, analysts, and research budgets frequently failed to beat broad market averages. One cited study covering 1960-1968 found that random portfolios actually outperformed mutual funds in the low- and medium-risk classes by roughly 3.7% and 2.5% per year.

Two explanations follow. One is that important stocks are already studied so intensely that mispricing is quickly reduced. The other is that analysts often emphasize the wrong things, especially glamorous growth and superior stories, while ignoring price. Because companies rarely grow rapidly forever, paying almost any price for the best-looking enterprise is a weak strategy masquerading as sophistication.

The chapter then summarizes the methods of a long-running professional program: arbitrages, liquidations, related hedges, and net-current-asset bargains. Arbitrages and liquidations were chosen only when the indicated annual return was around 20% or more and the chance of success seemed at least four out of five. Net-current-asset bargains were bought widely, often in groups of 100 or more issues, at about two-thirds of stripped-down current-asset value.

A more accessible path uses systematic screening. The chapter demonstrates how to comb the Standard & Poor's Stock Guide for low-multiplier stocks, then add tests for financial strength, dividend record, and growth. A low-P/E list can shrink quickly, but still leave a wide field of secondary companies that are statistically attractive and largely ignored. Single-factor studies also reveal that random low-quality stocks did badly, while high-quality or very large companies held up better.

The most attractive field remains the bargain issue, especially when a stock can be purchased below net-current-asset value. Examples such as Cone Mills, Jantzen, National Presto, Parker Pen, and West Point Pepperell show that well-known businesses can at times sell below their working-capital value. Special situations such as Kayser-Roth/Borden, Aurora Plastics/National Biscuit, and Universal-Marion add another possible arena, but this has become increasingly professional and less suitable for random participation.

Chapter 16: Convertible Issues and Warrants

Convertible bonds and preferred stocks promise an appealing combination: the protection of a senior claim and the upside of common stock. In practice that bargain is often less generous than it looks. The investor gives up quality, yield, or both in exchange for the conversion privilege, while the issuing company surrenders part of the future benefit that would otherwise belong solely to common shareholders.

As a group, convertibles are most dangerous when issued late in a bull market, exactly when they are most popular. The stock market then declines, the conversion feature loses much of its charm, and the underlying quality of the issue often proves mediocre. Historical comparisons between 1946 convertibles and straight preferreds, and between samples of convertibles and ordinary issues in 1968-1970, show that convertibles frequently decline more than investors expected from supposedly senior securities.

Even a successful convertible creates a strategic problem. Once it rises well above par, the owner must choose between taking a profit and continuing to act like a common-stock speculator. The Eversharp example dramatizes this trap: a bond bought at 100 rose to the equivalent of about 220, then collapsed with the stock until the value of the original issue was roughly 27. Selling too early hurts pride; holding too long can destroy the protective advantage that made the purchase appealing.

Warrants receive the chapter's harshest verdict. They fabricate market value out of thin air, dilute the common stock, and complicate valuation without giving the company dependable access to new capital. Examples such as the American & Foreign Power warrants and the accounting distortions around National General show why these instruments can make reported per-share figures and market values look much better than the underlying arithmetic justifies.

Chapter 17: Four Extremely Instructive Case Histories

Four case studies are chosen as warnings against financial carelessness and market intoxication. Penn Central showed glaring signs of weakness long before bankruptcy: bond-interest coverage of only about 1.91 and 1.98 times in 1967 and 1968, virtually no income taxes paid for years, and a stock market price that still reached 86 1/2 in 1968. A competent analyst should have recognized that the earnings quality and bond protection were far below conservative standards.

Ling-Temco-Vought demonstrates reckless empire building. Sales rose from only about $6.9 million in 1958 to about $3.75 billion in 1969, but debt exploded with them, reaching roughly $1.865 billion. By 1970 the company reported enormous losses, its stock had collapsed, and its bonds were trading at deep distress levels. The episode is also an indictment of commercial banks that kept supplying credit to a structure already far too weak.

NVF's takeover of Sharon Steel shows how acquisition accounting and capital-structure gimmicks can obscure economic reality. A much smaller company absorbed one about seven times its size, issued huge amounts of debt and warrants, and used unusual accounting entries to make the result look more respectable than it was. AAA Enterprises then supplies the classic hot-issue lesson: franchising stories, mobile homes, income-tax kiosks, an IPO at $13, a run to $28, and bankruptcy within about two years.

The combined lesson is blunt. Analysts and investors often ignore the simplest warning signs because a fashionable narrative has replaced arithmetic. Interest coverage, balance-sheet strength, tax payments, asset support, and the quality of reported earnings are elementary tools. When those are neglected, no amount of sophistication in forecasting or market interpretation can repair the damage.

Chapter 18: A Comparison of Eight Pairs of Companies

Eight side-by-side corporate pairings are used to dramatize how widely character, structure, and valuation can diverge. The first comparison sets a conservative Real Estate Investment Trust, with dividends paid continuously since 1889, against the sprawling and debt-laden Realty Equities Corp. of New York, whose balance sheet had become crowded with preferred stock, three warrant series, and more than $100 million of assorted obligations. The contrast is between steady stewardship and corporate showmanship.

Several pairs explore the tension between quality and price. Air Products commanded a much richer multiple than Air Reduction because of better profitability and growth, yet the cheaper stock still had a serious case when viewed through the lens of value. American Home Products and American Hospital Supply were both billion-dollar good-will companies whose growth records were admirable, but whose market prices had already capitalized a great many years of success.

Other comparisons show fashion at work more openly. H & R Block sold at over 100 times current earnings while Blue Bell sold at about 11 times, despite much larger tangible resources and a long dividend record. International Flavors & Fragrances carried a higher total market value than International Harvester even though Harvester's sales were about 27 times as large, because Flavors earned extraordinary margins while Harvester's profitability was mediocre.

The later pairs reinforce the same point with new ingredients. McGraw-Hill received an exalted valuation from the market despite declining earnings, while McGraw Edison traded on much more moderate terms. National General, swollen with convertibles and warrants, showed how quoted common-stock prices can become meaningless unless adjusted for dilution; National Presto, by contrast, sold near working-capital value despite strong earnings. Whiting and Willcox & Gibbs then display how even smaller exchange-listed companies can be either sensibly priced or irrationally promoted.

The cumulative lesson is that analysis works best when it searches for clear gaps between price and demonstrable value, not when it tries to rank every company by narrative appeal. The market repeatedly overpays for glamour, complexity, and current excitement while underpaying for stability, asset support, and respectable but unexciting profitability.

Chapter 19: Shareholders and Managements: Dividend Policy

Shareholders are urged to take management seriously rather than passively accept whatever policies are announced. Poor operating results, inferior performance versus comparable companies, and long periods of disappointing market price are legitimate reasons to question leadership. In practice, widely scattered shareholders rarely reform bad management by themselves, but takeovers have partially filled the gap by punishing chronic underperformance.

The chapter then turns to dividend policy. Older disputes centered on managements that retained earnings in the name of prudence, while shareholders wanted cash. Modern disputes more often revolve around growth: should profits be retained because reinvestment can produce superior results? Retention is justified only when management can show that withheld earnings are producing satisfactory gains in per-share value and earning power.

A practical standard follows: shareholders are entitled either to a normal payout, roughly on the order of two-thirds of earnings, or to a convincing demonstration that retained funds are being used productively. Weak or mediocre companies deserve especially close scrutiny when they plead for patience. Retention is not automatically wise merely because it sounds modern.

The chapter also distinguishes a true stock dividend from an ordinary stock split. A genuine stock dividend capitalizes specific retained earnings; a split mainly changes the number of shares and the price range. A more systematic use of stock dividends could in some cases serve shareholders better than the common utility practice of paying cash dividends and then reclaiming capital through subscription rights, all while imposing needless tax costs.

Chapter 20: "Margin of Safety" as the Central Concept of Investment

The entire philosophy is condensed into three words: margin of safety. For bonds this concept is straightforward. A railroad or industrial company must earn its fixed charges by a comfortable multiple over a period of years so that future adversity can be absorbed without disaster. The point of the margin is not to prove the future exactly, but to make exact foresight unnecessary.

The same idea applies to common stocks, although less mechanically. A stock may possess bond-like safety when it sells below the amount of debt that its assets and earning power could prudently support. More commonly, safety lies in buying a sound business at a price where the earnings yield stands well above bond yields. When stocks yielded something like 9% against bonds at 4%, the excess was a substantial buffer. When stock prices rose until the earnings yield shrank, that buffer became thinner.

This is why the worst losses often come not from obviously bad times but from fair-weather securities bought at fair-weather prices. Inferior bonds, preferred stocks, and common stocks look safest when prosperity is current and recent earnings are high. Yet if those purchases were made without a genuine cushion in value, they are exposed the moment ordinary adversity returns. A strong narrative without a strong margin is speculation wearing investment clothes.

Growth stocks can satisfy the principle, but only when future expectations are conservatively estimated and the price still leaves room for disappointment. Bargain securities make the concept more visible, because the difference between appraised value and quoted price can be observed directly. Diversification then becomes the operational companion of margin of safety: each individual decision may still fail, but a group of favorable odds should, in the aggregate, produce profits that exceed losses. The roulette illustration makes the same point in numerical form.

The final pages broaden the concept into a business code. Know the business being entered. Do not hand control to others without either supervision or justified trust. Do not commit capital unless the arithmetic shows a reasonable chance of gain with limited downside. Finally, once facts and reasoning support a judgment, act with conviction rather than crowd dependence. Superior results remain difficult, but satisfactory results are readily available to the investor who insists on a real margin of safety.

Postscript

The closing anecdote describes a disciplined investment partnership that favored caution, low prices, and very wide diversification. It avoided overpriced issues, sold quickly when quotations became excessive, and regularly held more than 100 different securities. Over many years this method reportedly produced average results of about 20% per annum on several million dollars of capital, a strong outcome built on patience and restraint rather than constant excitement.

One later purchase transformed the record. A half interest in a growing business was acquired at a moderate price because the current earnings and asset value justified it, even though the industry lacked fashionable appeal. The shares eventually rose by roughly 200 times, and that single decision outweighed decades of painstaking smaller operations. The moral is not that fortune favors hope, but that extraordinary gains become possible only when luck meets preparation, discipline, judgment, and the willingness to act when value is genuinely present.
