The Little Book That Still Beats the Market

Joel Greenblatt

Foreword

The Foreword introduces the author Joel Greenblatt as a highly capable and successful investor, distinct from typical investment authors who profit mainly from book sales. It emphasizes the simplicity and enduring validity of the book's approach to stock market investing, suggesting that while some people may quickly disregard it, those with patience can benefit significantly.

The Foreword highlights the progression in investment vehicles from mutual funds to index funds and positions Greenblatt’s approach as an "index-fund-plus" strategy, adding value by selectively including only quality businesses sold at low valuations.

Finally, it foreshadows that even if the formula becomes widespread, its advantage will diminish only partially, and the market as a whole will become more rational. The foreword encourages readers at all levels, from children to adults, to dive into the book.

Counterpoints

While the Foreword optimistically suggests the formula’s persistence even if widely adopted, skeptics might argue that in highly efficient markets, widespread use of any formula could eliminate the arbitrage opportunity entirely, leaving little to no advantage.

Additionally, the "index-fund-plus" approach assumes that value investing styles consistently outperform, but critics note that value premiums can vary or reverse depending on market cycles, challenging the formula's long-term reliability.

Introduction to the 2010 Edition

The 2010 Edition's Introduction recounts the unexpected success of the first edition, with wide sales and translations. The author expresses initial apprehension about readers potentially misapplying the "magic formula" due to data issues or calculation errors.

To mitigate this, a free resource website (magicformulainvesting.com) was established to offer reliable data and calculations, underscoring the importance of using proper tools to implement the strategy effectively.

The introduction stresses that investing is challenging, requiring a disciplined, long-term strategy that must resonate with the individual investor to maintain commitment through volatile periods. It also points out that the core lessons of the original book remain valid despite market changes.

Counterpoints

Some investors might question the reliance on a specific website for implementing the magic formula, concerned about data transparency, accessibility, or the potential for over-reliance on an automated tool without understanding underlying principles.

Moreover, the emphasis on patience and discipline presupposes investors' behavioral consistency, which is often unrealistic; many investors may struggle emotionally and financially to adhere strictly to any long-term formula.

Introduction to the Original Edition

This Introduction reveals the author's motivation in writing the book: to provide his children—and by extension, any reader—with an understandable and practical framework to successfully invest in the stock market.

He posits that despite the apparent simplicity of the formula and concepts, the results can be significant for beginners and seasoned investors alike if applied correctly, emphasizing self-reliance since professionals and academics typically cannot help individuals outperform the market.

The author previews the "magic formula" as a straightforward, logical tool that can outperform market averages with low risk, provided the user comprehends its rationale and maintains the effort.

Counterpoints

While the promise that "you can learn to beat the market" is encouraging, it contrasts the prevailing Efficient Market Hypothesis which suggests that consistently outperforming market averages is unlikely due to market efficiency and competition.

The author's call to individual investors to do it themselves may overlook structural disadvantages individual investors face compared to professionals, such as access to information, trading costs, and behavioral biases.

Chapter One

Chapter One presents a relatable story about Jason, a sixth-grader who operates a profitable gum-selling business. Through a conversation between the author and his son, the chapter explores fundamental investment concepts: profit calculation, estimating future earnings, and valuation.

The narrative introduces the concept of buying part ownership (shares) in a business and the importance of paying a reasonable price relative to expected profits, highlighting the time value of money and uncertainty about future earnings and risks.

Finally, it frames the challenge of valuing businesses and recognizing undervalued investment opportunities, setting up the premise for the magic formula and underscoring the need for belief and understanding in investment strategies.

Counterpoints

The analogy with Jason’s gum business simplifies investment valuation but may understate complexities involved in real markets, such as fluctuating competition, macroeconomic shifts, and behavioral influences on pricing.

Also, the emphasis on believing the formula might be seen as an appeal to faith over empirical skepticism, potentially downplaying the need for critical analysis and diversification.

Chapter Two

Chapter Two begins by illustrating the difficulty of saving money and the importance of doing so for future needs. It uses personal anecdotes to motivate reader engagement with saving behavior.

The chapter systematically discusses traditional options for money storage and growth: saving at home (mattress), bank deposits, corporate bonds, and government bonds. It highlights the trade-offs between risk, return, and liquidity, and introduces the concept of a baseline risk-free return, exemplified by the U.S. government bond rate.

This foundation establishes the minimum acceptable return that any attractive investment should exceed, setting up a benchmark against which other investment returns should be compared.

Counterpoints

While establishing 6% as a minimum baseline return makes conceptual sense, critics might note that such a fixed rate assumption overlooks varying interest rate environments and inflation impacts, which can alter the risk-free rate substantially over time.

Moreover, the discussion largely ignores the potential benefits of diversified portfolios or equity investments that, while more volatile, may offer superior returns over the long term.

Chapter Three

Chapter Three explores more engaging investment alternatives beyond saving and bonds, humorously dismissing gambling as an effective strategy.

It introduces the concept of investing directly in a business through ownership shares, using Jason’s matured gum shop business as an example. Details like share division, share price, and income statements are explained to illustrate how investors derive returns and how to assess the fairness of the price paid.

The chapter highlights the importance of earnings, business expenses, taxes, and the distinction between last year’s earnings and future potential, pointing to the complexities behind valuing stocks. It also underscores investment risks stemming from unpredictable earnings and market competition.

Counterpoints

The focus on buying shares of well-understood businesses simplifies but may gloss over deeper market factors affecting stock prices, such as investor sentiment and macroeconomic variables.

The use of last year’s earnings as a valuation baseline is practical but might overlook fluctuations and cyclical effects, possibly misleading naïve investors.

Chapter Four

Chapter Four investigates the volatility of stock prices compared to the relative stability of business values. Using well-known companies and the ‘‘Mr. Market’’ metaphor from Benjamin Graham, it illustrates that stock prices swing widely due to emotional investors, not always rational adjustments in company value.

It stresses the concept of a margin of safety—buying shares at a significant discount to intrinsic value to protect against errors in valuation or business setbacks. The chapter encourages patience and rationality when dealing with the stock market’s mood swings.

Yet, three critical challenges are highlighted: accurately valuing businesses, knowing whether those valuations are correct, and competing with many knowledgeable professional investors. Despite these, the chapter emphasizes persevering with logical, margin of safety–based investing.

Counterpoints

Some practitioners argue that the margin of safety can be difficult to define or quantify in practice, especially in dynamic industries, and that Mr. Market’s emotional swings can sometimes last longer than investors’ patience.

The debate remains whether average investors can realistically outguess institutional investors and the market efficiently, given access to information and tools.

Chapter Five

Chapter Five introduces two essential metrics for investing: earnings yield and return on capital. Using Jason’s Gum Shops, it shows that high earnings yield (earnings relative to price) indicates a bargain price, and high return on capital (earnings relative to invested capital) indicates a good business.

Through simple examples, it proves that a higher earnings yield is preferable and that a business earning a higher return on its capital is more desirable than one earning a low return, even if priced similarly.

Combining these two factors—the goodness of the business and the attractiveness of its price—is presented as the secret to achieving superior investment returns, which the magic formula formalizes to systematically select stocks.

Counterpoints

While focusing on these two metrics gives clarity, some critics may assert that solely relying on historical earnings and return on capital might miss emerging risks or opportunities, such as intangible assets, technological disruption, or regulatory changes.

Also, the approach assumes consistent application of these metrics across industries, which may not always hold true.

Chapter Six

Chapter Six formally introduces the magic formula, inspired by Benjamin Graham’s original formula that sought stocks priced below net liquidation value. Greenblatt explains that modern markets rarely present such extremely cheap stocks, prompting a new formula combining return on capital and earnings yield.

The chapter details how companies are ranked based on these two factors and how their combined rankings identify those with the best mix of being good businesses sold at bargain prices.

Historical testing over 17 years reveals striking outperformance of the magic formula portfolio versus the market, turning relatively modest investments into huge gains, thus validating the intuitive logic and empirical success of the formula.

Counterpoints

Some may question the backtesting methodology and point out that past performance does not guarantee future results, especially given changing market conditions and increased awareness of such strategies.

Additionally, critics might note that practical considerations like transaction costs, taxes, and limits on liquidity could reduce real-world returns compared to the backtested figures.

Chapter Seven

Chapter Seven examines possible critiques of the magic formula's performance and tests its robustness. It addresses concerns about small company size by limiting the universe to larger companies, finding that the formula retains strong performance.

Extensive testing over thousands of selections shows the results are unlikely due to luck, supporting the formula's validity. Further analysis dividing stocks into deciles by formula ranking reveals a clear gradient of returns, reinforcing the predictive power of the rankings.

The chapter also anticipates concerns about the formula’s future efficacy once widely adopted, noting that plenty of top-ranked stocks will always be available due to its ranking nature.

Counterpoints

Despite these reassurances, the historic success does not assure future results, especially as market participants adopt similar strategies, potentially arbitraging away benefits.

The persistent underperformance periods could discourage investors, and the methodology’s assumption of stable factors may not hold in structural market shifts.

Chapter Eight

Chapter Eight draws a parallel between the historical success yet eventual downfall of traditional British military tactics and the potential risks for the magic formula if it becomes widely used and arbitraged away.

The chapter highlights the erratic short-term performance of the formula, including months and years of underperformance, underscoring the difficulty investors face in adhering to a long-term strategy.

Examples from professional fund managers illustrate how clients often abandon successful strategies during downturns, emphasizing the necessity of belief and patience to achieve long-term success with the magic formula.

Counterpoints

This candid admission of the formula’s flaws might discourage investors who prefer more consistent short-term returns, raising legitimate concerns about psychological and financial feasibility.

Additionally, critics might contend that the persistence of the magic formula’s efficacy amidst widespread availability is optimistic, given market efficiency considerations.

Chapter Nine

Chapter Nine aims to ground confidence in the magic formula by focusing on timeless mathematical and logical principles. It states the formula systematically identifies above-average companies available at below-average prices, which is both sensible and reassuring.

The chapter revisits Jason’s business example to explain why companies that earn high returns on capital are special, likely possess competitive advantages, and can reinvest profits efficiently to sustain earnings growth.

It concedes that competition might erode these returns eventually but that the magic formula seeks companies with enduring advantages that prevent profits from being driven down to average levels, ensuring the portfolio consists of good businesses bought inexpensively.

Counterpoints

One might argue that high return on capital does not always indicate sustainable competitive advantage, as market conditions and technological disruptions can quickly erode profitability.

Moreover, the assumption that markets misprice such companies sufficiently to provide bargains might not persist indefinitely.

Chapter Ten

Chapter Ten shares a personal sailing anecdote to illustrate that loving an endeavor does not imply expertise, paralleling the challenges of stock investing.

It then presents enhanced performance data of the magic formula over various time horizons, notably how the strategy outperforms market averages with lower risk over three-year periods.

The chapter discusses the importance of a long investment horizon given the emotional volatility of Mr. Market, reassuring that despite short-term unpredictability, prices converge toward intrinsic value, rewarding patient investors.

Counterpoints

While emphasizing long-term horizons is sound, many investors with shorter timelines or liquidity needs may find adhering to the formula challenging.

Also, the guarantee that Mr. Market will eventually correct prices assumes no systemic disruptions or persistent market inefficiencies.

Chapter Eleven

Chapter Eleven addresses investors who reject formulas, advocating instead for individual stock picking with intelligence and discipline.

The chapter warns of the dangers of uninformed stock selection likened to reckless behavior in a dynamite factory and stresses that serious, knowledgeable research is required to successfully apply magic formula principles independently.

It emphasizes that while the formula uses last year’s earnings as a proxy for normal earnings, serious investors should estimate normalized earnings and own a concentrated portfolio of high-confidence picks.

For those less capable, combining magic formula rankings with subjective selections from top-ranked stocks is suggested as a compromise.

Counterpoints

This chapter reveals the limits of formula-based investing for individual stocks, highlighting the complexity and expertise needed, which many investors lack.

Critics might observe that some investors will rely blindly on formulas without sufficient understanding, and those adept at analysis might still struggle with uncertainties inherent in forecasting.

Chapter Twelve

Chapter Twelve discusses the harsh realities of Wall Street, uncovering that there is no external savior or magical solution, emphasizing personal responsibility in investing decisions.

The chapter critiques stockbrokers for conflicts of interest and mutual funds for high fees and difficulties in consistently outperforming the market post-costs.

It explains hedge funds' exclusivity and high fees, while recognizing the appeal of index funds for average returns with low fees, yet clarifies that beating the market remains elusive for most.

The author ultimately advocates the magic formula as the only realistic path to outperforming the market with low risk, accompanied by the caveat that understanding and belief in the formula are crucial.

Counterpoints

The chapter downplays the potential value of professional managers and alternative investments, which some investors might consider important components to a diversified portfolio.

Also, it assumes investors possess or are willing to acquire the necessary discipline and understanding, which may not be universally true.

Chapter Thirteen

Chapter Thirteen contemplates the use of wealth for meaningful purposes beyond personal gain, urging consideration of societal issues such as education reform.

It highlights the inefficiencies in public school systems compared to capitalist business processes, noting the lack of penalties and incentives leads to misallocation of resources.

The author draws analogy to "Just Broccoli" — a poor business — applying those lessons to advocate for reforms that would improve capital efficiency in education.

This broader perspective situates investment success within a wider social and economic context.

Step - by - Step Instructions

The Step-by-Step Instructions chapter provides practical guidelines for implementing the magic formula strategy in real life, replicating the tested successful approach.

Key aspects include diversifying in 20 to 30 stocks, staggered purchasing over a year, adhering to one-year holding periods with tax-aware selling strategies, and consistent rebalancing.

The chapter reviews available stock screening tools, recommending the author’s website for most accurate adherence to the formula, but offering general guidelines for others, including minimum return on assets, low price/earnings ratios, and sector/region exclusions.

Counterpoints

Despite the instructions’ clarity, executing such a disciplined and time-intensive approach may be daunting for many investors.

Also, some may question the reliability and comprehensiveness of the tools and data sources, particularly for individual investors without access to professional platforms.

Afterword to the 2010 Edition

The Afterword to the 2010 Edition reaffirms the fundamental principles and efficacy of the magic formula after five years, despite volatile markets and economic challenges.

It explains the importance of combining margin of safety with buying good businesses at bargain prices, inspired by Graham and Buffett, and how the magic formula operationalizes these ideas.

The author acknowledges the formula’s flaws: periods of underperformance and short-term unpredictability, but argues these are inherent to any successful long-term strategy and maintain that the formula still works as a logical, disciplined approach.

Finally, it cautions against selective picking of individual formula stocks since the portfolio works effectively on average like insurance, balancing out winners and losers.

Counterpoints

The persistence of shortcomings such as multi-year underperformance may deter many investors and challenge the practical adoption of the formula.

And while advocating patience, some might note that the psychological burden and time commitment remain significant hurdles.

Appendix

The Appendix offers advanced readers background and technical details on the magic formula, focusing on two key metrics: return on capital and earnings yield.

Return on capital is calculated as EBIT divided by tangible capital employed (net working capital plus net fixed assets), carefully excluding intangibles like goodwill and adjusting for differences in debt and tax rates to compare companies equitably.

Earnings yield is defined as EBIT divided by enterprise value (market value of equity plus net interest-bearing debt), chosen over simple P/E ratios to account for capital structure differences, improving valuation comparability.

The appendix also reviews academic criticisms of market-beating strategies and demonstrates that the magic formula’s tested methodology avoids common biases and consistently performs well, even against more complex models and in large-cap stocks.
