The Structure of Scientific Revolutions

Kuhn, Thomas S.

Introductory Essay by Ian Hacking

The Introductory Essay by Ian Hacking sets the stage for understanding The Structure of Scientific Revolutions as a passionate and transformative work about how science progresses. Hacking emphasizes Kuhn’s original focus on the physical sciences of the mid-20th century and the radical shifts that have since occurred in fields like biotechnology and information science, which affects the extent to which the book’s theses apply today.

Key concepts introduced include the central notions of normal science, puzzle-solving, paradigm, anomaly, crisis, and scientific revolutions. Kuhn’s famous idea of incommensurability, meaning the fundamental incompatibility between successive paradigms that disrupts straightforward comparison and rational theory choice, is explained here. Hacking stresses Kuhn's nuanced view that progress in science is not simply linear toward truth but a replacement of less adequate worldviews with better ones. The sequence Kuhn proposed—normal science with a paradigm, encountering anomaly that leads to crisis and ends in revolution and a new paradigm—is described as both insightful and subject to controversy.

The essay discusses the historical context of Kuhn’s work, its initial reception, and its influence on the philosophy and sociology of science. It also cautions readers to carefully interpret Kuhn’s claims, especially given contemporary shifts in scientific fields and the popularization (and debasement) of terms like 'paradigm shift' in public discourse.

Counterpoints

While Hacking presents Kuhn’s work as groundbreaking, some scholars argue that the book overemphasizes revolutions at the expense of cumulative scientific progress. Critics point out that Kuhn’s focus on physics may limit the applicability of his model to other sciences such as biology or social sciences, where paradigms are less clearly defined and change more continuously. Others caution that Kuhn’s notions of incommensurability and paradigm shifts can lead to a form of relativism that undermines rational theory choice and the objective basis of science. There is also debate about Kuhn's sidelining of experimental and instrumental novelty, emphasizing theory over practice. Finally, the popular use of 'paradigm shift' detached from Kuhn’s original meaning invites misinterpretations and superficial applications.

Preface

The Preface offers a personal account by Kuhn on how exposure to the history and philosophy of science led him from theoretical physics to an in-depth study of scientific development. His surprise at historical scientific theories undermining traditional conceptions of science prompted a career shift and motivated the project culminating in this book. Kuhn acknowledges the interdisciplinary influences, ranging from psychology and philosophy to sociology, which shaped his approach.

He emphasizes the schematic and tentative nature of this essay, noting limitations in scope (mostly physical sciences), and anticipates a fuller treatment in a future expanded work. Key methodological concerns include the role of social, intellectual, and technological factors in scientific change, which are recognized but not fully explored here due to space constraints.

The preface highlights the collaborative and evolving nature of the project, thanking mentors, colleagues, students, and family for their support and intellectual contributions. It situates the work both as a historical study and a philosophical investigation into the nature of scientific knowledge, with the aim of urging a conceptual reorientation towards understanding scientific development.

I: Introduction: A Role for History

Chapter I, “Introduction: A Role for History,” critically examines the traditional, textbook-based image of science as linear accumulation of facts and theories. Kuhn argues that this portrayal misleads by ignoring the historical integrity and internal coherence of earlier scientific views, which were themselves scientific within their own contexts.

He points out the insufficiency of methodological prescriptions alone to decide among competing scientific views and stresses the arbitrary elements—personal, historical, and accidental—that influence scientific development. Scientists operate within frameworks that shape what questions are deemed legitimate and what techniques applicable, and these frameworks are deeply taught and institutionalized.

Kuhn introduces normal science as a tradition-bound, puzzle-solving activity that assumes the community’s current worldview is correct, suppressing fundamental novelties. When anomalies accumulate and resist resolution, the community faces scientific revolutions, involving wholesale theory replacement and dramatic shifts in standards, problems, and imagination.

The chapter foreshadows later analyses of the dynamics of normal science and revolutionary change and addresses concerns about mixing descriptive, sociological, historical, and normative philosophical claims, acknowledging the complexity and interconnectedness of these perspectives in scientific practice.

Counterpoints

Some historians argue that portraying science as dominated by paradigms and revolutions underestimates the variety and messiness of scientific practice, including incremental changes, multiple coexisting traditions, and the role of social, economic, and political factors in shaping science. Others suggest that Kuhn’s emphasis on arbitrariness and the subjective element risks undermining the aim of science to objectively represent nature, and that the rigid distinction between normal and revolutionary science oversimplifies the fluid and continuous nature of many scientific advances. Moreover, the blending of historical description with normative claims can conflate what science is and what it ought to be, making Kuhn’s historiography less clear.

II: The Route to Normal Science

Chapter II, “The Route to Normal Science,” articulates the concept of normal science as research conducted within the bounds of an accepted scientific achievement, or paradigm. The paradigm is characterized by two essential features: it is unprecedented enough to attract lasting adherents and is open-ended, providing a set of problems for further inquiry. This concept explains how mature scientific fields exhibit consensus and shared standards that define legitimate problems and methods.

Kuhn contrasts this with pre-paradigm periods, exemplified by the history of physical optics and electrical research before Newton and Franklin, marked by competing, incompatible schools with no consensus paradigm. During these periods, scientific inquiry is more dispersed, and the compilation of facts lacks coherence, resembling a natural history more than science proper.

The emergence of a paradigm brings a sharp narrowing and institutionalization of a scientific community, along with increased specialization, journal publication, and professional communication. Paradigms are thus crucial to the development of science as a coherent enterprise, enabling focused, cumulative research, whereas pre-paradigm periods are characterized by multiplicity, competition, and lack of agreed frameworks.

Counterpoints

Some critics argue that Kuhn’s emphasis on paradigms and stages underplays the role of ongoing diversity and heterogeneity in science. They question the neat dichotomy between pre-paradigm and paradigm phases, noting that multiple schools or theories often coexist and interact even in mature sciences. Additionally, the paradigm concept risks obscuring sociopolitical influences on scientific consensus and the role of external factors. Others note that Kuhn’s use of paradigms as defining scientific communities can be sociologically difficult to operationalize, challenging the clarity and usefulness of the concept without more precise empirical elaboration.

III: The Nature of Normal Science

Chapter III, “The Nature of Normal Science,” explores what scientists do when working under an accepted paradigm. Kuhn emphasizes that paradigms are not static patterns to be replicated but starting points for further articulation, extension, and detailed problem-solving. A new paradigm often begins as an incomplete set of successes, a promise to resolve acute problems, which normal science works to actualize.

Normal science largely consists of three problem classes: precise determination of significant facts, comparison of these facts with paradigm predictions, and theoretical articulation to resolve ambiguities and explore implications. These problems engage most scientists most of the time, and through them the paradigm’s promise is fulfilled and extended. Scientists resist new theories or phenomena that challenge the paradigm, demonstrating that normal science is conservative and aims at puzzle-solving within fixed conceptual commitments.

Besides laws and theories, normal science depends on a network of rules, including preferred instruments, methodologies, and even metaphysical commitments. Such rules structure the research problems and define acceptable solutions, supporting the puzzle-solving metaphor. Yet Kuhn argues that these rules are generated from and subordinate to paradigms, which guide research even when explicit rules are lacking.

Counterpoints

Some commentators critique Kuhn’s characterization of normal science as too restrictive and theoretical, underestimating the creative and novel aspects of experimental, instrumental, and observational work. Historians of science have shown that experiments themselves can introduce significant novelty and that the interplay of theory and experimentation is more complex than Kuhn suggests. Furthermore, the treatment of metaphysical commitments as rigid rules may not capture the fluidity and contestation in scientists' conceptual frameworks. Finally, the analogy of normal science as puzzle-solving may underplay the diversity of scientific activity and motivation.

IV: Normal Science as Puzzle-solving

Chapter IV, “Normal Science as Puzzle-solving,” draws a famous analogy equating normal scientific research problems to puzzles with known solutions and fixed rules. Kuhn highlights that normal science aims not at novelty but at extending, clarifying, and completing what is already known within the framework of an accepted paradigm. The expected outcomes are tightly bounded; failures to conform to expectations are seen as errors rather than scientific breakthroughs.

The motivation for tackling such problems lies in the challenge of solving the intricate technical, conceptual, and mathematical difficulties posed by the puzzle, not in discovering radical new phenomena or theories. This explains the devotion scientists show to incremental, detailed work such as building better instruments or refining calculations, tasks which nonetheless underpin scientific progress.

Kuhn stresses that puzzle-solving is governed by rules that limit acceptable solutions—a scientist cannot simply construct any explanation but must conform to the paradigm’s established laws, methodologies, and standards. These rules govern scientific concepts, instrumentation, and higher-level methodological commitments, forming the framework within which normal science operates.

Despite the apparent rigidity of these rules, Kuhn emphasizes that paradigms precede and generate rules; explicit formulations of rules may be absent, and the coherence of normal science ultimately depends on shared exemplars or models rather than abstract rules alone.

Counterpoints

Critics argue that Kuhn’s puzzle analogy risks trivializing normal science and neglects the production of meaningful theoretical advances within 'normal' research. Some highlight the creativity involved in designing experiments and interpreting data even within paradigms. Indeed, technological innovations and experimental breakthroughs often emerge during so-called ‘normal’ phases. Furthermore, emphasizing rules and puzzle constraints may mask the flexibility scientists exhibit when addressing anomalies. The metaphor also undervalues the social dynamics and cognitive diversity in scientific communities.

V: The Priority of Paradigms

Chapter V, “The Priority of Paradigms,” clarifies the distinction and relationship between paradigms and rules in scientific communities. Kuhn stresses that paradigms are not reducible sets of abstract rules but rather coherent wholes formed by shared exemplary problem-solutions, symbolic generalizations, metaphysical commitments, and values. These elements together guide research and problem-solving even without formally articulated rules.

Kuhn draws on linguistic philosophy, especially Wittgenstein’s concept of "family resemblance," to explain how scientists recognize and apply paradigms through resemblance to shared models rather than fixed rule application. Scientific education reinforces paradigms by immersing novices in exemplars and applications rather than abstract rules, resulting in tacit, intuitive mastery more than explicit knowledge of research rules.

Disagreements and debates about fundamentals typically occur during periods of crisis or pre-paradigm heterogeneity, while agreement and neglect of fundamental questions characterize periods of settled normal science. Kuhn also accounts for specialization within sciences as partial sharing of paradigms, with subspecialties having distinctive sets of paradigmatic exemplars and commitments.

Finally, Kuhn argues that paradigms, by their nature and mode of transmission, exert a form of scientific rationality that coexists with the limits of rule-based knowledge, emphasizing exemplars and implicit knowledge as central to scientific practice.

Counterpoints

Some philosophers and historians argue that Kuhn’s diminished role for explicit rules obscures the importance of logical and methodological norms in guiding scientific practice. By privileging tacit knowledge and exemplars, Kuhn’s account risks relativism and makes scientific rationality appear subjective or community-dependent. Critics note that many scientists explicitly and critically engage with rules and logic, questioning whether exemplars alone suffice. Moreover, the broad and elastic usage of the term 'paradigm' may dilute its heuristic value, complicating empirical application and theory of science.

VI: Anomaly and the Emergence of Scientific Discoveries

Chapter VI, “Anomaly and the Emergence of Scientific Discoveries,” focuses on the critical role of anomalies—phenomena or results that conflict with the expectations of an existing paradigm—in generating novel scientific discoveries. Kuhn defines discovery as a process initiated by recognizing an anomaly, followed by continued investigation and eventual assimilation into a revised or new paradigm.

The examples of oxygen, X-rays, and the Leyden jar illustrate different modes of discovery: extended conceptual assimilation requiring paradigm change (oxygen), accidental discovery triggering crisis (X-rays), and theory-induced discovery in a pre-paradigm context (Leyden jar). These examples highlight that discoveries are not instantaneous but extended episodes involving conceptual shifts and community acceptance.

Kuhn stresses that anomalies often remain unrecognized or are suppressed until the paradigm’s predictive framework is sufficiently precise to register them. Normal science’s detailed and rigid puzzle-solving thus inadvertently sets the stage for revolutionary discoveries. Resistance to anomalies initially shields paradigms but eventually promotes deep scrutiny, critical for revolutionary change.

The chapter also draws parallels from psychological experiments showing how perception is initially constrained by expectations and only adjusts, often painfully, to new categories or concepts, illustrating the cognitive dimension of scientific discovery.

Counterpoints

Some scholars argue that Kuhn’s emphasis on anomalies as precursors to discovery underestimates the role of creativity, serendipity, and social factors influencing scientists’ readiness to attend to unexpected phenomena. Others point out that not all anomalies lead to paradigm change, and many are resolved within existing frameworks or ignored. The neat categorization of discoveries and their association with paradigm shifts can over-simplify the complex, contingent, and sometimes nonrational aspects of scientific innovation. Moreover, the psychological analogy may not capture the full social and institutional dynamics at play.

VII: Crisis and the Emergence of Scientific Theories

Chapter VII, “Crisis and the Emergence of Scientific Theories,” addresses the larger-scale paradigm changes arising not only from discoveries but more profoundly from the invention of new scientific theories prompted by sustained crises. Kuhn illustrates how prolonged awareness of anomalies leads to a state of crisis, marked by professional insecurity and the search for new explanatory frameworks.

Selected historical crises—the disintegration of Ptolemaic astronomy before Copernicus, the problems faced by phlogiston chemistry before Lavoisier, and late 19th-century physics tensions leading to relativity—show how theories emerge in response to unsolvable problems within an existing paradigm. Crisis is characterized by failure of normal problem-solving, proliferation of competing articulations, and erosion of consensus.

Kuhn notes that such revolutions are not merely accumulative but involve reconstruction from new fundamentals, altering problems, methods, and goals. The analogy to a gestalt switch reflects the radical transformation scientists undergo in adopting new paradigms. The chapter also highlights that new theories sometimes had early anticipations and stresses the role of social, intellectual, and practical pressures in generating crises.

Counterpoints

Some historians and philosophers argue that Kuhn’s crisis model may be too narrow or deterministic, neglecting instances where theoretical change arises gradually, from multiple influences, or without a clear pre-existing crisis. Others stress the role of non-epistemic factors such as institutional politics or personal ambition in theory change. The revolution-as-gestalt-switch metaphor has been criticized for implying abrupt, discontinuous breaks inconsistent with the often gradual and contested nature of scientific development. Additionally, the parallel drawn between major historical revolutions and smaller-scale theory changes can obscure important differences.

VIII: The Response to Crisis

Chapter VIII, “The Response to Crisis,” explores scientists’ typical behavior when confronted with persistent anomalies and crisis. Kuhn rejects the idea that scientists simply discard theories upon falsification; instead, rejection only occurs when an alternative paradigm is available to replace the old one, making theory choice simultaneously an act of acceptance and rejection.

Kuhn argues that crises loosen normal research rules, creating a more open, debate-heavy atmosphere with competing articulations. Nevertheless, scientists strive to preserve paradigms so long as possible because their commitment to prevailing paradigms underpins normal science. Counterinstances (falsifications) themselves are constant, but science endures by treating many as puzzles, demonstrating the resilience of paradigms.

He highlights the psychological tension scientists face and underscores that major anomalies must be significant enough to induce crisis. Responses to crisis include intensified investigation, speculative theorizing, and sometimes recourse to philosophy, which can clarify problems not addressable by normal science.

Ultimately, crises prepare the ground for paradigm shifts, and while some new paradigms emerge early in crisis, others may arise only after prolonged uncertainty and extraordinary research.

Counterpoints

Critics argue that Kuhn underestimates the diversity of responses to anomalies and crises among scientists—some may abandon paradigms without immediate alternatives, or continue working within crisis for long periods. Popperian philosophers suggest that falsification plays a more decisive role than Kuhn allows. Also, Kuhn’s account may attribute too much permanence and social cohesion to paradigms, downplaying internal dissent and diversity of scientific opinions even within ‘normal science.’ Furthermore, the idea that anomalies alone induce crisis oversimplifies the complex social and cognitive factors influencing scientific change.

IX: The Nature and Necessity of Scientific Revolutions

Chapter IX, “The Nature and Necessity of Scientific Revolutions,” directly addresses what scientific revolutions are and why the term is appropriate. Kuhn compares scientific revolutions to political ones, highlighting that both arise from widespread recognition that current institutions (or paradigms) fail to address pressing problems and must be replaced by incompatible new frameworks.

He explains that paradigm shifts are necessarily extrainstitutional in nature, involving circular reasoning since proponents use their paradigms to defend themselves, leading to debates resolved more by persuasion than logic or empirical proof. Revolutions change not only ontology but also methods, problems, and standards of science, producing incommensurability between competing paradigms and making communication and comparison difficult.

Kuhn argues that scientific development is generally noncumulative; rather, new theories arise by replacing and conflicting with older ones, challenging the traditional view of science as steadily approximating truth. He critiques logical positivist claims of compatibility between successive theories, especially noting differences between Newtonian and Einsteinian mechanics that require reinterpretation of fundamental concepts.

Counterpoints

Some philosophers affirm a more cumulative view of science, arguing that newer theories often subsume or approximate older ones and that scientific revolutions are less radical replacements and more paradigm refinements or expansions. Logical empiricists hold that scientific theories can be related via correspondence or limit relations, preserving rationality and progress. Critics also question Kuhn’s analogy with political revolutions, cautioning against conflating social/political dynamics with scientific theory change. Finally, some argue that apparent incommensurability and communication breakdowns can be overcome through translation and conceptual analysis.

X: Revolutions as Changes of World View

Chapter X, “Revolutions as Changes of World View,” develops the idea that scientific revolutions involve profound transformations in the way scientists perceive and interact with their research world. Kuhn uses metaphorical and psychological experiments on perception to argue that paradigm changes alter what scientists 'see' even when looking at the same phenomena with the same instruments.

These shifts go beyond mere reinterpretation; they involve reeducation of perception and cognitive frameworks acquired through training and exemplars. Examples from astronomy, electricity, chemistry, and dynamics illustrate how objects and phenomena once seen and categorized in one way are perceived differently after a paradigm shift, effectively creating a new scientific world for practitioners.

Kuhn contrasts this view with traditional epistemology, which treats observations as fixed and paradigms merely as interpretive overlays. He argues for a more radical connection between experience, perception, and conceptual frameworks, suggesting that sensations and measurement themselves are paradigm-determined and thus vary between scientific communities.

The chapter acknowledges difficulties with these claims, noting the importance of shared neural and cultural factors while emphasizing the socially and historically constructed nature of scientific perception and knowledge.

Counterpoints

Critics caution that Kuhn’s notion of 'different worlds' risks metaphysical confusion and relativism, challenging the objective reality of scientific observations. Many philosophers and scientists defend the idea that while theories influence interpretation, empirical data remain independent. Others argue that cognitive and perceptual psychology provides only limited support for Kuhn’s more radical claims. Additionally, Kuhn’s claim that scientists do not reinterpret observations but see fundamentally different things is contested as overstating the case; many consider paradigm change as better understood in terms of reinterpretation, theory-ladenness, and conceptual framework differences rather than distinct sensory worlds.

XI: The Invisibility of Revolutions

Chapter XI, “The Invisibility of Revolutions,” examines why scientific revolutions, despite their profound impact, often remain invisible or disguised in scientific textbooks and popular narratives. Kuhn argues that textbooks, popularizations, and the philosophy of science focus on the stable outcomes of revolutions, presenting science as a smooth, linear, cumulative enterprise of fact and theory accumulation.

This selective portrayal omits or distorts the complexities, conflicts, and ruptures of actual scientific development, thereby creating an unhistorical and misleading image for students and the general public. Textbooks trace a chain of continuous progress, ignoring how past scientific problems, methods, and standards differed from today’s paradigms.

Kuhn illustrates these points with examples, including misrepresentations of Boyle’s chemical element concept as the origin of modern elements. The result is a persistent attribution of cumulative, incremental growth to science, despite the discontinuities and paradigm changes documented by historians.

Counterpoints

Some historians and philosophers acknowledge Kuhn’s critique but argue that textbooks necessarily simplify for pedagogical clarity and that more nuanced, historically accurate treatments exist and are improving. Others suggest that the cognitive and practical efficiency of teaching a coherent, cumulative narrative outweighs concerns about historical fidelity. Moreover, revisionist histories themselves may run risks of overcomplicating or fragmenting the story of scientific development, making it less accessible. Finally, critics argue that the invisibility of revolutions is not unique to science but common to many disciplines.

XII: The Resolution of Revolutions

Chapter XII, “The Resolution of Revolutions,” tackles how scientific communities transition from an old paradigm to a new one. Kuhn stresses that paradigm testing and acceptance occur as a communal process of competition between rival paradigms, not as isolated verification or falsification of a single theory.

He draws parallels and critiques to philosophical models of theory verification and falsification, arguing that both oversimplify the complex social and normative dimensions of paradigm choice. Kuhn highlights the role of persuasion, shared values, and nonlogically compelling arguments during revolutionary debates, noting that scientific decisions about paradigms incorporate personal, aesthetic, and social factors beyond empirical problem-solving.

Kuhn analyzes the difficulties of communication between proponents of incommensurable paradigms, likening it to translation between language communities. Conversion to a new paradigm is framed as a gestalt-like cognitive shift that cannot be forced by argument alone but involves socialization and reeducation.

The chapter concludes by emphasizing the community basis for scientific knowledge, the inevitability of disagreement and resistance, and the importance of communal consensus for progress and continuation of science as a coherent endeavor.

Counterpoints

Critics question Kuhn’s emphasis on persuasion and social dynamics over rational criteria, suggesting that scientific theory choice involves more objective evaluation than Kuhn allows. Some argue that the translation metaphor exaggerates communication barriers and that scientific debates are often resolved through clarification and shared conceptual frameworks. Popperians uphold falsification as central, dismissing the notion of gestalt-like conversion as metaphysical. Others warn Kuhn’s community-centric approach risks exclusion and conformity pressures, suppressing individual critical thinking and alternative views. Finally, Kuhn’s portrayal of the scientific community as closing ranks after adoption of a paradigm is challenged as idealized and neglecting ongoing pluralism.

XIII: Progress through Revolutions

Chapter XIII, “Progress through Revolutions,” addresses the problem of how scientific progress occurs despite periodic paradigm shifts and fundamental ruptures in continuity. Kuhn notes that ‘science’ is often defined in terms of observable progress, raising questions about the nature and direction of that progress given his model.

Kuhn compares scientific progress with that in other creative fields like art and theology, which historically emphasized cumulative improvement but ultimately experienced ruptures in style and orientation. He stresses the extraordinary insulation of scientific communities from lay society, the rigorous educational initiation, and shared standards as factors facilitating rapid problem solving and cumulative gains during periods of normal science.

Kuhn concedes that revolutions involve losses, narrowed scope, and increased specialization, but argues that the resulting new paradigms enable broader and deeper problem solving in the long run. He critiques goal-directed, teleological notions of scientific progress and truth, favoring an evolutionary model without a pre-established endpoint, analogous to Darwinian evolution.

Concluding, Kuhn stresses the importance of understanding both the special nature of scientific communities and the characteristics of nature that permit human knowledge to grow through these discontinuous but adaptive episodes.

Counterpoints

Critics maintain that Kuhn underappreciates the role of cumulative knowledge and incremental advancement in science and that his rejection of scientific truth risks veering into relativism or skepticism. Others argue that Kuhn conflates epistemic progress with social or institutional change, neglecting the normative and methodological continuity in scientific development. The analogy with biological evolution is contested as inadequate since scientific theories have distinct epistemic criteria, and the notion of entire loss during revolution is challenged by examples of retention and refinement. Finally, some question Kuhn’s underplaying of external social, economic, and cultural influences on scientific progress.

Postscript—1969

The Postscript (1969) reflects Kuhn’s matured views, addressing criticisms and clarifying ambiguities from the original book. He focuses on disentangling the concept of paradigm from scientific community structure, proposing to analyze communities first and then paradigms through shared commitments within these communities.

Kuhn distinguishes two senses of paradigm: the broader sociological constellation of beliefs, values, and techniques shared by a community, and the narrower set of concrete exemplars or problem-solutions serving as models for normal science puzzle-solving. He clarifies that the latter is philosophically the deeper concept, though it also caused the most misunderstandings and allegations of irrationality.

The Postscript elaborates that tacit knowledge embedded in exemplars is not subjective improvisation but a systematic, corrigible, and time-tested form of knowledge essential to scientific practice. Kuhn discusses the limits of rational argument in paradigm choice, the role of translation between incommensurable scientific language communities, and the communal and social nature of scientific knowledge and change.

Finally, Kuhn acknowledges the relativistic interpretations of his work but argues that scientific development is nevertheless progressive, marked by puzzle-solving and methodological advancement, highlighting the distinctive features that separate science from other human activities and calling for comparative studies of community dynamics.

Counterpoints

Some critics view Kuhn’s postscript clarifications as insufficient to dispel concerns about the vagueness and multiplicity of paradigm meanings, leaving the concept too broad for empirical use. Philosophers caution that the emphasis on community and tacit knowledge may downplay individual critical reasoning and the possibility of objective progress. Others argue that Kuhn’s sociological framework risks relativism, conflating scientific truth with social consensus. The pragmatic but non-absolute nature of paradigm choice can undermine the ideal of rational, evidence-based theory evaluation. Additionally, some express concern that community boundaries and shared commitments are difficult to define and may mask internal diversity and conflict within science.
