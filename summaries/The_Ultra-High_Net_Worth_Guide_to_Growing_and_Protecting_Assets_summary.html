The Ultra-High Net Worth Guide to Growing and Protecting Assets

Richard P. Rojeck

Chapter 1: Wealth

Financial independence is the state of being able to enjoy one's desired standard of living while engaging in one's desired activities, without regard to the financial rewards of those activities. Two conceptual frameworks exist for determining whether one has accumulated "enough." Under the endowment approach, capital is sufficient when its earnings — income plus appreciation, net of inflation — sustain the desired lifestyle indefinitely without consuming principal. At a 3% after-tax return, a $1 million annual lifestyle requires $33.3 million in invested capital. Under the annuity approach, capital is consumed over an anticipated lifespan; the same lifestyle requires roughly $21 million over 30 years, but carries the risk of outliving assets.

The sequence of returns — the particular order in which annual gains and losses occur — significantly affects whether a portfolio sustains a spending rate, independent of the average return. Monte Carlo simulation quantifies this uncertainty by running roughly 1,000 iterations to produce a probability distribution of outcomes. For a $50 million portfolio targeting a $1 million annual spend with a 7% return assumption, Monte Carlo yields near-zero probability of depletion over 30 years, a 62% probability of preserving the original sum, and a median ending balance of $66 million (inflation-adjusted).

The utility of wealth obeys diminishing marginal returns: each additional unit of wealth produces less satisfaction than the previous one, and carrying excessive debt or concentration to pursue more wealth can endanger financial independence. A practical litmus test: in the next financial crisis, will one be a buyer of distressed assets or a forced seller? Answering that question clarifies both the adequacy of one's capital base and the prudence of its structure.

Chapter 2: Estate Planning

Estate planning for high-net-worth individuals has three core challenges: defining a legacy, building institutional infrastructure to sustain it across generations, and minimizing transfer taxes. Research attributes the failure of roughly 70% of multi-generational wealth plans to trust and communication breakdown (60%), failure to prepare heirs (25%), and the absence of a shared family mission (10%). Wealth itself is not merely financial: it encompasses the family's history, education, intellectual capacity, and shared values — all of which require deliberate cultivation.

The U.S. transfer tax system systematically favors lifetime transfers over bequests at death. The annual gift tax exclusion ($15,000 per recipient in 2019; adjusted for inflation) allows a couple with eight descendants to transfer up to $240,000 per year free of gift tax. Unused exclusions cannot be carried forward. The lifetime exemption ($11.4 million per person through 2025 under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017, reverting to ~$5.6 million thereafter) enables large transfers that remove future appreciation from the taxable estate. Gifts to irrevocable trusts rather than outright — particularly trusts designed to last multiple generations (“Dynasty Trusts”) — provide asset protection, third-party management, and removal from the transfer tax system for generations to come.

Advanced transfer techniques leverage these exemptions further. A sale to an Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust (IDGT) transfers an asset via installment note at the Applicable Federal Rate; since the trust is “defective” for income tax purposes, no gain is recognized, and appreciation above the note rate accumulates in the trust gift-tax-free. A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT) transfers future appreciation above 120% of the Applicable Federal Midterm Rate to beneficiaries; the donor must survive the trust term. A Qualified Personal Residence Trust (QPRT) transfers a home at a discounted gift value, with parents retaining the right to live there during the trust term and then leasing it at fair market value. A preferred LLC/partnership freeze separates a fixed preferred interest (frozen in value) from a growth interest gifted to the next generation, halting estate growth on the frozen portion.

Federal estate taxes, which can reach 40%, are generally due nine months after death. The four ways to fund payment carry very different costs: cash ($1.00 per dollar), asset liquidation (~$1.25 per dollar, often under time pressure and adverse market conditions), bank borrowing (~$1.26 per dollar after interest), and life insurance (~32¢ per dollar for a healthy 65-year-old couple). Family governance structures — mission statements, family meetings, wealth coaches, Trust Protectors, co-trustees, and distribution committees — are essential to sustaining multi-generational plans once the founding generation is gone.

Chapter 3: Charitable Planning

Philanthropic planning converts what would otherwise be “involuntary philanthropy” (estate and income taxes paid to the government) into directed giving while generating meaningful tax benefits. Gifts of appreciated property are generally superior to cash gifts: as a tax-exempt entity, the charity pays no capital gains tax on sale, making the full pre-tax value available for charitable purposes while eliminating the donor's embedded gain.

A Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT) — structured as a CRAT (annuity) or CRUT (unitrust) — pays income to the donor (and spouse) for life or a term of years, then distributes the remainder to charity. Because the trust is tax-exempt, a highly appreciated asset can be sold inside the trust and fully reinvested. The donor receives an upfront income tax deduction equal to the present value of the charitable remainder. A Charitable Lead Trust (CLT) is the mirror image: charity receives income first, remainder passes to children. A Testamentary CLAT can be structured for a zero estate tax result by calibrating the charity's income interest to consume the entire estate tax liability. Both GRAT and CLAT produce similar transfer-tax leverage but cannot efficiently skip a generation (GST exemption cannot be allocated at inception).

A Private Foundation is a family-controlled tax-exempt entity that institutionalizes a family name in philanthropy. Cash contributions are deductible up to 30% of AGI (vs. 60% for public charities); appreciated property up to 20% (vs. 30%). Foundations must distribute 5% of investment assets annually and pay a 2% excise tax on net investment income. Self-dealing restrictions rigorously govern transactions with family members. A Donor Advised Fund offers similar philanthropic outcomes at lower cost and administrative burden. A Supporting Organization achieves public charity status (higher deduction limits) but requires a majority of the governing board to be appointed by the supported charity. Other tools include Conservation Easements (current income tax deduction for restricting development), Retained Life Estate with Gift Annuity (converts a primary residence into income), and the charitable IRA rollover (up to $100,000 annually, excluded from income). Effective philanthropic strategy favors focus, measurable impact, leverage of other donors, and alignment of giving with explicit family values.

Chapter 4: Business Succession

For the closely-held business owner, the enterprise typically constitutes the dominant asset of the estate and succession planning is among the most consequential planning decisions. Four scenarios cover most situations: sale to a co-owner, transfer within the family, sale to employees, and sale to an outside party. Each requires advance planning, ideally several years before the intended transition.

A buy-sell agreement creates a contractual market for interests that have no ready buyers. It must address a full range of triggering events (retirement, disability, death, divorce, bankruptcy, involuntary termination, loss of professional license), the structure of the buy (entity purchase vs. cross-purchase, and their respective income tax implications), the valuation method, and financing. Life insurance and disability buyout insurance are commonly used to fund the obligation. A hybrid (“wait and see”) agreement gives the entity a right of first refusal before individual owners may purchase, optimizing tax efficiency while preserving flexibility.

Family business succession requires lifetime transfer strategies drawn from estate planning (annual exclusions, exemption gifts, IDGT note sales, freeze structures), careful treatment of “inside” vs. “outside” children (those working in the business vs. those who are not), and governance structures capable of outlasting the founders. An Employee Stock Ownership Plan (ESOP) allows a sale to employees with a Section 1042 tax-deferred rollover of gains into qualified replacement securities; if held to death, the gain is eliminated via basis step-up. A sale to outside buyers (private equity, strategic acquirers, IPO) requires readying the business — earnings history, systems, physical condition — typically over one to two years. IRC Section 6166 permits deferral of estate tax attributable to a closely-held business at favorable interest rates, but its senior IRS lien and installment burden make it a financing method of last resort.

Chapter 5: Asset Protection Planning

Asset protection is not about avoiding legitimate obligations; it is about insulating wealth from frivolous or excessive claims and the asymmetric litigation risk that accompanies high net worth. The three lines of defense are risk prevention (sound personal and business practices, periodic risk management audits), adequate insurance (property, casualty, umbrella, and specialty coverage through a high-net-worth specialist), and judicious structuring of business entities and trusts. Under federal law, qualified retirement plan assets and most IRA rollover funds are protected from creditors. State law may extend protection to home equity, life insurance, and annuity products, though not from divorce or IRS liens.

The guiding principle — if you don’t own it, they cannot take it — motivates the use of limited liability companies (LLCs) to hold business and investment assets. Personal creditors can generally obtain only a charging order (a lien on future distributions) rather than seizing the underlying LLC assets; if the LLC simply does not distribute, the judgment creditor receives nothing. For self-protection, three specialized trust structures are available. A Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT) is funded by one spouse for the benefit of the other; assets are beyond the beneficiary-spouse’s creditors and excluded from the estate. A Beneficiary Defective Irrevocable Trust (BDIT), created by a parent for the beneficiary, allows the beneficiary to sell personal assets into the trust via an installment note, shifting value while achieving creditor protection. A Domestic Asset Protection Trust (DAPT) is a self-settled irrevocable trust domiciled in a favorable state, with an independent trustee and the grantor as a discretionary beneficiary.

A captive insurance company allows a closely-held business to insure risks not covered by commercial carriers, with premium payments deductible as business expenses and underwriting profits tax-free up to $2.2 million (the “mini-captive” threshold). The captive must operate as a genuine insurance company — pricing risks, issuing policies, maintaining reserves, paying claims — or risk IRS disallowance of premium deductions.

Chapter 6: Life Insurance

Life insurance is amenable to mathematical rather than philosophical analysis: either its economics are favorable in a given situation or they are not. The two fundamental forms are term insurance (temporary, with premiums rising sharply after the level period) and permanent insurance (whole life, universal life, equity-indexed universal life, variable universal life), which builds tax-deferred cash value funded by excess early premiums. The crossover point — where permanent insurance becomes more economical than term plus an invested side fund — typically occurs between years 15 and 20. Because insurance charges are paid from pre-tax earnings inside the policy, the effective after-tax cost is lower than it appears.

The economics of estate-tax funding illustrate life insurance’s advantage: a healthy 65-year-old couple can obtain survivorship permanent coverage for approximately 30¢ per dollar of death benefit. This compares with ~80¢ for a forced asset sale (net of discount and closing costs) and ~$1.26 per dollar for bank borrowing (including interest in a 35% bracket). The equivalent taxable return on premiums for a 60-year-old couple paid through joint life expectancy is approximately 10.87% — far above what tax-free municipal bonds (then ~2.5%) or other liquid, guaranteed, income-tax-free alternatives can provide. Variable universal life policies with well-selected subaccounts can capture equity-like returns within this tax-favored structure, though investment underperformance risks a policy lapse.

Policy ownership and beneficiary arrangements require care. Death benefits are included in the insured’s taxable estate if “incidents of ownership” are retained; owning the policy inside an irrevocable life insurance trust (ILIT) excludes it from both income and estate taxation. Additional uses include leveraged gifts (annual exclusion gifts fund a trust-owned policy, compounding tax-free across generations), leveraged charitable gifts (a $1 million donation split between cash and life insurance can generate over $3.6 million for charity and family combined), buy-sell agreement funding, key-person indemnification, and ESOP repurchase obligations.

Chapter 7: Investment Management

Wealth preservation investing differs fundamentally from wealth accumulation. Building wealth requires intense personal labor, narrow focus, and high risk tolerance. Preserving it requires delegation to professional managers, broad diversification, lower leverage, and a focus on return of capital rather than maximum return on capital. The intellectual foundation is Modern Portfolio Theory (MPT), developed by Harry Markowitz in 1952 (Nobel Prize in Economics, 1990). His central insight: portfolio risk is not the weighted average of individual asset risks but something less, because assets do not move in perfect lockstep. Risk consists of systematic (undiversifiable) risk — shared across an asset class — and unsystematic (diversifiable) risk, which diminishes as uncorrelated assets are added. Investors are not compensated for unsystematic risk because it can be diversified away.

The Efficient Frontier plots portfolios offering maximum return for each level of risk; no rational investor would accept a portfolio below it. Adding asset classes (foreign stocks, real estate, commodities) shifts the frontier up and to the left — higher expected return with less risk. The landmark Brinson/Hood/Beebower study (1986, updated 1991) quantified that asset allocation determines 93% of portfolio return variation; security selection and market timing together explain only 7%. A 20-year tabulation of seven asset classes (1999–2018) confirms that top performers one year are typically near the bottom a year or two later, and that missing the market’s best days is devastatingly costly (missing the best 10 days of 2002–2017 reduced a $10,000 investment from $41,332 to $20,873).

Historical data on asset classes support a multi-asset approach. Over 210 years (1802–2012), common stocks returned a real (after-inflation) 6.6% annually; the worst 20-year real return was still positive (+1%). Small-cap stocks have historically outperformed large-cap (17.03% vs. 9.28% annualized for the smallest vs. largest decile, 1926–2012). Value stocks have outperformed growth across size categories, with the gap most pronounced in small-cap. High-dividend stocks returned 12.58% vs. 10.13% for the S&P 500, with lower beta. U.S. stocks are approximately 65% correlated to international developed-market stocks (1970–2012), providing meaningful diversification. Factor investing extends this framework: market risk (beta), size, value, quality (profitability, financial leverage, earnings stability), and momentum each explain incremental return variation beyond beta alone. Smart Beta strategies — screening for factors rather than passive market-cap weighting — have historically outperformed pure indexing.

Fixed income spans four major categories: U.S. Treasuries, agencies, municipals (tax-exempt for residents of the issuing state), and corporates. For high-bracket investors, the taxable-equivalent yield on municipals generally exceeds comparable taxable bonds. Real estate constitutes roughly 17% of the global investable market but is underrepresented in most investor portfolios outside of the real estate industry. REITs provide diversified, liquid access and pass through depreciation deductions; the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 added a 20% deduction on qualified REIT dividends. Commodities (energy, metals, agricultural items) provide diversification benefits due to low correlation with stocks and bonds, accessible via commodity company stocks, MLPs, oil and gas partnerships, futures, and ETFs.

Portfolio construction requires quantifying risk tolerance, return objectives, time horizon, liquidity needs, income tax rate, ESG considerations, and complexity preferences. A portfolio optimizer identifies the efficient allocation, which is then implemented through a combination of passive indexing (low cost, ~0.10% for S&P 500 ETF) and active management for segments where alpha generation is plausible. Alternative investments — private equity, private debt, and hedge funds — offer diversification, potential return enhancement, and lower correlation to public markets, but require minimum commitments of $250,000–$1 million and multi-year lock-ups. An Investment Policy Statement (IPS) formalizes the allocation, benchmark, rebalancing rules, manager monitoring criteria, and duties of each party. Family offices (single-family requiring ~$100–$250 million; multi-family at $25 million or less) extend investment management to include estate, tax, philanthropic, and concierge services.

Chapter 8: Income Tax Planning

Four categories organize all income tax planning opportunities: Defer (postpone when income is reported, exploiting the time value of money), Convert (shift income to more favorably taxed forms), Divert (shift income to entities or persons taxed at lower rates), and Deduct (reduce reportable income). A taxpayer in a 50% combined bracket who invests $100,000 annually at 8% pre-tax accumulates $4.58 million over 20 years with full deferral vs. $2.98 million after annual taxation — a deferral advantage of $1.6 million, or 54%.

Major deferral tools include qualified retirement plans (401k, profit-sharing, defined benefit — which also provide a deduction); self-employed defined benefit plans (deductible contributions of ~$130,000 annually for a 60-year-old earning $250,000); non-qualified deferred compensation for corporate executives (no government-imposed limits); Charitable Remainder Trusts (no capital gains on asset sale inside the trust); deferred annuities (no contribution limit); ESOP Section 1042 rollovers (gain deferred into long-dated corporate bonds, basis-stepped-up at death); Health Savings Accounts (triple tax benefit); installment sales; Private Placement Life Insurance (hedge-fund-like investments inside an insurance wrapper with no income tax until withdrawal); Qualified Opportunity Zone investments (defer and potentially eliminate gains on 10-year holds); and Qualified Small Business Stock under Sections 1045 (gain rollover) and 1202 (50–100% gain exclusion).

Key conversion strategies include tax-exempt municipal bonds (taxable-equivalent yields exceeding corporates for high-bracket investors), Roth IRA conversions (“back door” conversions available without income limit), and corporate loans to shareholders in lieu of salary. Diverting strategies include gifting income-producing assets to children (subject to the Kiddie Tax below age 18/24) and structuring compensation as pass-through distributions rather than salary to reduce payroll taxes (subject to reasonable compensation requirements). Key deduction strategies include real estate depreciation (27.5 and 39-year standard schedules; cost segregation studies accelerate components to 5/7/15-year periods), oil and gas programs (80–90% first-year deduction via intangible drilling costs), charitable contributions (60% of AGI for cash to public charities), home mortgage interest (deductible on balances up to $750,000 under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act), and Incomplete Non-grantor Trusts (capture state and local tax deductions beyond the $10,000 cap via trusts domiciled in no-income-tax states). The Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT), curtailed through 2025, remains relevant for certain taxpayers. Stock options (ISOs and Non-Quals) require disciplined exercise and disposition planning, including margin loans, collars, and charitable gift strategies.

Chapter 9: Selecting a Financial Planner

Wealthy individuals typically retain capable specialists — estate attorneys, CPAs, investment advisors, insurance professionals — but their arrangements have been assembled at different times, with different advisors, often without coordination. A qualified financial planner functions as the integrating “quarterback” of the advisory team: identifying objectives and values, assessing current arrangements for gaps and inconsistencies, introducing strategies not previously considered, coordinating all advisors, reducing the plan to writing, implementing it, and monitoring it on an ongoing basis.

Financial planners operate under a layered regulatory framework: the SEC governs investment advisory activities (Registered Investment Advisor status); FINRA governs securities sales (registered representative status); state insurance departments govern insurance product sales. Three credentials are recognized as industry-leading. The CFP® (Certified Financial Planner) requires a bachelor’s degree, a CFP Board-registered curriculum, a six-hour comprehensive examination, three years of qualified experience, a background check and ethics agreement, and 30 hours of biennial continuing education. The ChFC (Chartered Financial Consultant) requires the American College curriculum, three years of experience, and 30 hours biennial CE. The CPA-PFS (Personal Financial Specialist) is held exclusively by CPAs, requires an examination, and must be recertified every three years. CFPs and CPA-PFS holders are held to a fiduciary standard — a legal obligation to act in the client’s best interest — whereas FINRA-regulated brokers are not.

Selecting the right planner involves evaluating professional credential, years of experience, educational background, specialization (ideally aligned with the client’s situation — business owners, real estate, corporate executives), disciplinary history (verifiable through FINRA BrokerCheck, SEC IAPD, and state insurance departments), firm affiliation (continuity and resources), and references. Compensation method — fee-only, fee-and-commission, or commission — is neither inherently superior nor inferior but should be clearly understood at the outset. The CFP Board’s prescribed six-step planning process covers scope definition, information gathering, analysis, recommendations, implementation, and monitoring.

Chapter 10: Tying It All Together

A comprehensive self-assessment framework integrates all prior topics into a single action checklist. Financial independence requires knowing the capital needed to sustain one’s desired lifestyle after tax and inflation, maintaining adequate liquidity for the next financial crisis, keeping debt at a prudent level, and deploying surplus wealth purposefully for family and society. Vision and values — the non-financial dimensions of wealth — must be articulated and communicated to heirs, with values embedded in the structure of legal documents and family governance practices.

Effective multi-generational estate planning means opportunity-based distributions rather than entitlements, active gifting programs that remove assets from the transfer tax system entirely, and regular modeling of current and future estate tax exposure. Philanthropic planning is most effective when giving is focused on explicit causes, structured for accountability and measurable outcomes, and coordinated with wealth preservation tools (CRTs, CLATs, TCLATs, conservation easements, private foundations). Business succession plans should be written, regularly reviewed, address the inside/outside-children question, maintain current business valuations, and identify a clear exit strategy. Asset protection requires periodic risk audits, appropriate entity structures, and trust provisions that protect future generations from creditors, judgments, and divorce.

Life insurance policies should be periodically re-illustrated for premium adequacy, correctly owned to avoid estate inclusion, and aligned with buy-sell obligations. Investment portfolios should be governed by an Investment Policy Statement, managed with attention to tax efficiency and cost minimization, and benchmarked against suitable standards. Income tax planning should systematically pursue the full menu of deferral, conversion, diversion, and deduction strategies. Across all of these disciplines, a qualified financial planner who integrates the strategies, coordinates the advisory team, and maintains the plan as circumstances evolve is the single most important resource for sustainable wealth preservation across generations.
