The Vital Question

Why Is Life the Way It Is?

Nick Lane

Introduction: Why Is Life the Way It Is?

Lane opens by identifying what he calls a black hole at the heart of biology: all complex life shares a common ancestor, yet that ancestor appears only once in the history of the planet. Bacteria arose early and remained enormously successful, but they never crossed into the same kind of structural complexity seen in animals, plants, fungi, and protists. The author treats this as the book’s guiding puzzle: why do all eukaryotes inherit such a distinctive package of traits — a nucleus, sex, elaborate internal architecture, and even programmed death — when bacteria and archaea do not independently evolve close equivalents?

He argues that modern biology often answers detailed mechanistic questions while leaving the deepest historical one untouched. For Lane, understanding disease, evolution, and even the prospects for life elsewhere depends on asking why cells came to work this way in the first place. The introduction therefore lays out the book’s central hypothesis: the rise of complex life was tied to a singular endosymbiosis between an archaeal host and the bacterium that became the mitochondrion, and the common thread linking the book’s chapters will be energy — especially the power stored in proton gradients across membranes.

Chapter 1: What Is Life?

Lane begins with the search for extraterrestrial life to make an uncomfortable point: questions about aliens are really questions about Earth, because biology still lacks a predictive theory of why life takes its observed form. He reviews the broad history of life and emphasizes the asymmetry between the two great kinds of cells. Prokaryotes — bacteria and archaea — dominate Earth in biochemical versatility, yet all visibly complex organisms descend from one eukaryotic lineage. That fact makes complexity monophyletic, not a repeated evolutionary outcome.

The chapter presses hard against the old idea that a rise in oxygen simply liberated complexity. If oxygen alone were the decisive trigger, Lane argues, many bacterial lineages should have independently become larger and more intricate once oxygen accumulated in the atmosphere and oceans. Instead, the planet shows billions of years of bacterial success followed by one rare transition to eukaryotic complexity. By the end of the chapter, he has reframed the question of life from “What are the ingredients?” to “Why did only one lineage escape the bacterial condition?”

Counterpoints

Lane’s critique of oxygen-centered explanations is sharp, but many evolutionary biologists would treat oxygen as necessary but not sufficient, not as a complete rival theory. On that view, ecological changes, new predator-prey dynamics, and contingent genomic innovations could have made the eukaryotic transition extraordinarily rare without implying that oxygen should have produced multiple independent bacterial-to-animal trajectories.

Chapter 2: What Is Living?

Where the first chapter asks about life’s large-scale pattern, the second asks what it means to be alive rather than merely organized. Lane uses viruses, spores, membranes, and thermodynamics to argue that living systems cannot be defined only by inheritance or structure. Living requires a continuous traffic with the environment: food, electrons, waste, and the maintenance of delicate internal order. In that sense, life is never a static thing; it is a process of staying away from equilibrium for as long as energy continues to flow.

The chapter’s core claim is that all known life runs on the same fundamental logic. Redox chemistry moves electrons; electron flow is coupled to proton gradients across membranes; and those gradients drive the universal turbine-like enzyme ATP synthase. Building on Chapter 1’s question about the singular path to complexity, Lane now identifies the most ancient common machinery behind all cells. Life, in his telling, is inseparable from electrons falling toward lower-energy states and from membranes converting that fall into usable work.

Chapter 3: Energy at Life’s Origin

Lane next turns from living cells to the problem of the first cells. He dismisses simple “primordial soup” imagery as inadequate because life needs more than a stockpile of molecules. It requires a steady source of reactive carbon, a reliable input of free energy, catalysts to channel reactions, compartments that keep chemistry from dispersing, waste disposal, and eventually heredity. The key challenge is not merely making organic molecules, but sustaining a proto-metabolism robust enough to keep generating more of them.

The chapter argues that alkaline hydrothermal vents best satisfy these conditions. In Lane’s account, vents offer hydrogen-rich fluids, carbon dioxide from the ocean, mineral catalysts, microporous rock compartments, and naturally occurring proton gradients. These conditions could have driven the reduction of CO2 to simple organics in a way that resembles the core biochemistry of modern autotrophs. Building on Chapter 2’s emphasis on electrons and protons, Lane presents the origin of life as a geochemical energy problem before it became a genetic one.

Counterpoints

The alkaline hydrothermal vent model is influential, but it is not a settled consensus. Researchers working on RNA world scenarios, wet-dry cycling, or prebiotic surface chemistry argue that some crucial molecules and polymers may form more readily outside submarine vents, so the origin-of-life field still lacks a single experimentally decisive winner.

Chapter 4: The Emergence of Cells

Having argued that natural proton gradients could power prebiotic chemistry, Lane asks how true cells emerged from that setting. He uses conserved genes, universal bioenergetics, and the contrast between bacterial and archaeal membranes to suggest that the last universal common ancestor, or LUCA, was already strongly chemiosmotic. Rather than inventing modern respiration from scratch, the earliest cells may have sat within natural proton gradients and exploited them using simple proteins such as ATP synthase and related ion-translocating enzymes.

The chapter’s distinctive idea is that early membranes had to be relatively leaky. A tightly sealed membrane would have destroyed the advantage of living in a natural proton gradient, whereas a leaky membrane could let that external disequilibrium do useful work. From there, selection for sodium-proton exchange and active pumping let cells internalize power generation and gradually sever their dependence on vents. Building directly on Chapter 3, Lane presents the divergence of bacteria and archaea as a story about membrane chemistry, ion gradients, and how life learned to carry its energy source with it.

Counterpoints

The reconstruction of a leaky LUCA powered by natural gradients is elegant, but it remains an inference from modern cells and geochemical analogy rather than a direct historical record. Some origin-of-life researchers agree with the chemiosmotic emphasis while remaining skeptical that the exact membrane properties, ion couplings, and order of innovations can be recovered with such specificity.

Chapter 5: The Origin of Complex Cells

Lane now arrives at the book’s central argumentative summit. He reviews common explanations for the rise of eukaryotes — especially the loss of the cell wall and the emergence of phagocytosis — and argues that none of them explains why complexity appeared only once. Bacteria can lose their walls without becoming dynamic predators, and many complex eukaryotes still possess cell walls. The real asymmetry, he says, is not one of basic cellular architecture alone but of bioenergetic capacity.

The chapter introduces the book’s signature concept, energy per gene. Prokaryotes respire across their outer surface, so their energetic capacity scales with membrane area and quickly hits a ceiling as cells get larger. Eukaryotes broke that constraint by acquiring mitochondria, which brought thousands of internal respiratory membranes into the cell. A eukaryote can therefore support a much larger genome, more proteins, more regulation, and a more dynamic internal skeleton without starving each gene of energy. In Lane’s framing, mitochondria do not merely decorate a pre-existing complex cell; they make complexity economically possible.

That energetic surplus helps explain why eukaryotes can accumulate new genes, shift genes from the endosymbiont to the nucleus, and experiment with structures that bacteria cannot sustain at scale. Building on the membrane constraints of Chapter 4, Lane argues that the singular origin of eukaryotes follows naturally from a singular successful endosymbiosis. Once a cell could carry many tiny powerhouses inside itself, the path opened to the nucleus, trafficking systems, shape-changing behavior, and the rest of the eukaryotic toolkit.

Counterpoints

Lane’s energy per gene argument is one of the book’s most famous claims, but it is also debated. Critics note that the size of the energetic gulf depends on how metabolism, scaling, and gene counts are compared, and discoveries around Asgard archaea have strengthened the view that some host-side complexity may have preceded fully modern mitochondria. The broad importance of bioenergetics is widely respected; the exact sequence of events remains contested.

Chapter 6: Sex and the Origins of Death

Once Lane has established a cell within a cell, he asks how that unstable partnership could generate the universal features of eukaryotes. One major answer is genomic conflict. The bacterial endosymbiont brought with it mobile genetic elements and introns, turning the host genome into a far messier place than prokaryotic genomes had been. Lane argues that the nucleus arose as a solution to this crisis: transcription and splicing could happen in one compartment, while translation stayed outside, protecting ribosomes from unprocessed RNA.

The chapter also argues that sex arose early, not late. Large, unstable genomes in small populations are vulnerable to mutational meltdown, and recombination provides a way to preserve function while permitting innovation. Because all major eukaryotic groups appear to share core sexual machinery, Lane treats sex as part of the original eukaryotic package rather than as a later embellishment. In his account, a cell energized by mitochondria gained the freedom to expand genetically, but it also inherited the burden of managing much more error-prone heredity.

From there, Lane extends the same logic to two sexes, uniparental inheritance of mitochondria, and the division between germline and soma. If mitochondria from both parents competed inside one cell, selfish lineages could spread even when they harmed the larger organism. Restricting inheritance to one parent helps selection eliminate bad mitochondria; setting aside a protected germline helps preserve mitochondrial quality across generations. Building on Chapter 5, the chapter presents sex, mortality, and the sacrifice of the body as downstream consequences of the energetic windfall and internal tensions created by endosymbiosis.

Counterpoints

The origins of sex, mating types, and the germline remain among the most contested problems in evolution. Red Queen parasite models, DNA repair models, and ecological explanations all illuminate part of the evidence, so many researchers would treat mitochondrial conflict as a major contributor rather than a single master explanation for every universal eukaryotic trait Lane groups together here.

Chapter 7: The Power and the Glory

The final main chapter turns from ancient transitions to present-day physiology. Lane describes mitochondria as literal and figurative mosaics: their respiratory complexes are assembled from proteins encoded partly in mitochondrial DNA and partly in the nucleus. Electron transfer depends on exquisite physical spacing, so even very small mismatches between subunits can impair respiration. This means that every eukaryotic cell depends on a constantly negotiated partnership between two genomes that evolve at different rates and are inherited in different ways.

That arrangement explains, for Lane, why mitochondrial problems radiate outward into disease, infertility, hybrid breakdown, and apoptosis. When mismatched components slow electron flow, the respiratory chain becomes over-reduced, free radicals accumulate, cytochrome c is released, and the cell can be pushed into controlled self-destruction. He also emphasizes the asymmetry of maternal inheritance: because mitochondrial genes pass mainly through mothers, selection has limited power to remove variants that harm males, a theme that links metabolism to sex differences and reproductive trade-offs.

Yet Lane does not present free radicals only as damage. Building on Chapter 6’s emphasis on conflict and control, he shows how reactive oxygen species can also act as local signals that tune mitochondrial biogenesis, energy demand, and cellular decision-making. The chapter closes the book’s loop by bringing the ancient logic of proton gradients and endosymbiosis into everyday physiology: ageing, disease, development, and even athletic capacity still bear the mark of the bargain struck when one cell took up residence inside another.

Counterpoints

The chapter’s account of free radicals and mitochondrial control is rich, but the older free-radical theory of ageing no longer commands universal support. Ageing is shaped by many interacting processes — including proteostasis, nutrient sensing, stem-cell dynamics, inflammation, and DNA damage — so many biologists would treat mitochondrial ROS as one important signaling and damage pathway within a broader causal network.

Epilogue: From the Deep

Lane ends with the strange case of Parakaryon myojinensis, a deep-sea cell that looks tantalizingly intermediate between a prokaryote and a eukaryote. It has internal membranes and apparent endosymbionts, yet lacks the clean, canonical organization of known eukaryotes. The specimen becomes a narrative mirror of the book’s larger question: if complexity arose only once, what would an incomplete or alternative transition even look like? The epilogue uses that ambiguity to revisit the rarity and difficulty of the eukaryotic leap.

Lane’s tentative conclusion is that the deep-sea organism may represent a peculiar modern echo of the ancient problem rather than a fully fledged new domain of life. More importantly, he reiterates the book’s broad claim that all life is fundamentally chemiosmotic and that complex life depends on the rare success of a cell within a cell. By the end, the “vital question” is answered not with a single magical invention, but with a chain of energetic constraints and opportunities that runs from hydrothermal vents to mitochondria, sex, death, and the possibility that complex life may be uncommon in the universe.

Counterpoints

The epilogue’s deep-sea organism is fascinating, but without a genome sequence it cannot bear much interpretive weight. It may be an artefact of preparation, a highly derived eukaryote, or an unusual symbiosis that says little about the original rise of eukaryotes, so the chapter’s strongest conclusion is really about the possibility of repeated transitions rather than their demonstration.
