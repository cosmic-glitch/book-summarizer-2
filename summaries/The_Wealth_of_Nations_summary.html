The Wealth of Nations

Adam Smith

Introduction and Plan of the Work

Smith opens by defining the wealth of a nation as the annual produce of its land and labour—the fund of necessaries and conveniences that its people consume each year. He argues that a nation's prosperity depends on two factors: the skill, dexterity, and judgment with which its labour is applied, and the proportion of productive to unproductive workers. Of these two, Smith considers the first more important, noting that even in civilized nations where many people do not labour at all, the produce is so great that even the poorest workman enjoys more necessaries than any savage could acquire.

The Introduction serves as a roadmap for the entire work. Book I examines the causes of improvement in productive powers of labour and how produce is distributed among different classes. Book II treats the nature and accumulation of capital stock. Book III explores why different nations have followed different paths to opulence. Book IV examines rival systems of political economy—the mercantile and agricultural systems. Book V addresses the revenue of the sovereign, covering public expenses, taxation, and public debts.

Book I, Chapters 1–3: The Division of Labour

Smith opens with what is perhaps the most famous passage in economics: the pin factory example. A single worker making pins alone might produce one pin per day, but ten workers dividing the eighteen distinct operations among themselves can produce upwards of 48,000 pins per day—4,800 per person. This dramatic increase in productivity arises from three causes: increased dexterity in each worker through repetition, the saving of time otherwise lost in passing between tasks, and the invention of machines that facilitate and abridge labour. Smith observes that this specialization, extended across society, means that even the coarsest woollen coat on a labourer's back is the product of the cooperation of thousands of workers.

In Chapter 2, Smith argues that the division of labour is not the product of deliberate human planning but rather the natural consequence of the propensity to truck, barter, and exchange—a disposition unique to humans. His famous declaration follows: "It is not from the benevolence of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own interest." Self-interest, channeled through exchange, drives specialization and mutual benefit. Smith contends that the differences in talent between individuals—between a philosopher and a street porter, say—arise more from habit, custom, and education than from innate nature.

Chapter 3 establishes that the extent of the division of labour is limited by the extent of the market. In remote highland villages, every farmer must be his own butcher, baker, and brewer. Water-carriage, by opening wider markets, is the great enabler of specialization—Smith notes that a ship with six men can transport as much as fifty wagons drawn by four hundred horses. This explains why the earliest civilizations arose along coasts and navigable rivers: Egypt on the Nile, Bengal on the Ganges, China along its interconnected canal systems.

Counterpoints

Smith's pin factory example, while compelling, presents an idealized view of specialization. Karl Marx would later argue that extreme division of labour alienates workers from the product of their labour and reduces them to performing mindless, repetitive tasks. Modern research on job enrichment (Hackman and Oldham) suggests that excessive specialization can reduce motivation and productivity. Smith also underestimates the role of deliberate institutional design in organizing labour—later economists like Ronald Coase showed that firms exist precisely because conscious coordination can sometimes outperform market-mediated specialization.

Book I, Chapters 4–5: Money and the Measure of Value

Smith traces the origin of money from the inconveniences of barter. When the division of labour is established, a person needs goods they do not produce. But barter requires a double coincidence of wants—the butcher must want what the baker has, and vice versa. To solve this, every prudent person naturally seeks to keep a universally accepted commodity on hand. Various societies used cattle, salt, shells, dried cod, tobacco, or nails. Eventually, metals prevailed because they are durable, divisible, and uniform. Coins arose to solve the problems of weighing and assaying metals, with public stamps certifying their weight and purity—though Smith notes that princes have routinely debased their coinage throughout history.

Chapter 5 develops Smith's labour theory of value. He argues that labour is the real measure of the exchangeable value of all commodities. The real price of anything is the toil and trouble of acquiring it. "It was not by gold or by silver, but by labour, that all the wealth of the world was originally purchased." Smith introduces the distinction between value in use and value in exchange—water has great use-value but little exchange-value, while diamonds have the reverse. He argues that while money (gold and silver) serves as the nominal measure of value, labour is the ultimate and real standard, since equal quantities of labour are always of equal value to the labourer, whereas the value of gold and silver fluctuates with the discovery of new mines.

Counterpoints

Smith's labour theory of value was later refined and challenged from multiple directions. David Ricardo attempted to make it more rigorous but encountered the problem of different qualities of labour. The marginalist revolution of the 1870s (Jevons, Menger, Walras) replaced the labour theory with subjective utility theory, resolving Smith's water-diamond paradox through the concept of marginal utility—diamonds are valuable not because they cost more labour, but because the marginal unit of water is abundant while the marginal diamond is scarce. Smith's treatment of money also understates the role of credit and trust in monetary systems, which later theorists like Georg Friedrich Knapp emphasized.

Book I, Chapters 6–7: Components of Price and Natural vs. Market Price

Smith identifies three component parts of every commodity's price: wages (the return to labour), profits (the return to capital), and rent (the return to land). In a primitive society without accumulated stock or private land, the whole produce belongs to the labourer. But once capital accumulates and land becomes private property, profits and rent emerge as additional claims on the product. Smith stresses that profits are not merely wages for the labour of supervision—they are proportional to the capital employed, not to the difficulty of management. He also notes that wages, profits, and rent are the three original sources of all revenue in society.

Chapter 7 introduces the crucial distinction between natural price and market price. The natural price is what is just sufficient to pay rent, wages, and profits at their ordinary rates—it is the central price towards which all commodity prices gravitate. The market price fluctuates around the natural price according to supply and demand: when supply falls short of effectual demand (the demand of those willing to pay the natural price), the market price rises; when supply exceeds effectual demand, it falls. Smith notes that monopolies, trade secrets, and corporation privileges can keep market prices above natural prices for extended periods, while perfect liberty allows market prices to quickly return to the natural level.

Counterpoints

Smith's tripartite division of income into wages, profits, and rent was criticized by later economists for being overly schematic. Alfred Marshall pointed out that in practice these categories blur—a self-employed farmer earns a combination of all three. The concept of a gravitational "natural price" also assumes free competition and perfect mobility of labour and capital, conditions that rarely obtain fully. Institutional economists like Thorstein Veblen argued that markets are shaped by power relations, customs, and institutions that prevent prices from gravitating to any natural level. Modern economists recognize persistent information asymmetries and transaction costs that Smith largely abstracts away.

Book I, Chapters 8–9: Wages of Labour and Profits of Stock

Smith argues that wages are determined by the bargaining power of workers relative to employers, but fundamentally depend on whether society is advancing, stationary, or declining. In a growing economy, the increasing demand for labour drives wages above bare subsistence. Smith is notably sympathetic to workers, observing that masters have natural advantages in bargaining—they can hold out longer, they are fewer in number and can more easily combine, and the law generally favours them over workers. He argues that liberal wages are both a sign and a cause of national prosperity: well-fed workers are more productive, and high wages encourage population growth which further fuels economic expansion.

Chapter 9 examines profits of stock, which Smith finds are inversely related to wages in a growing economy. As capital accumulates, competition among capitalists drives profits down while raising wages. Smith uses interest rates as a proxy for profit rates, noting that interest in England had fallen from 10% under Henry VIII to 5% by Queen Anne's time. He observes that profits are naturally low in rich countries (like Holland) and high in poor ones (like Bengal), and warns that the merchant class tends to seek monopoly privileges that raise profits at the expense of the public. In a striking passage, Smith notes that high profits raise prices through a compound interest effect, making them more inflationary than high wages.

Counterpoints

Smith's claim that wages and profits move inversely was challenged by Nassau Senior and later by neoclassical economists, who argued that both can rise simultaneously when productivity improves through technological change. Smith's use of interest rates as a proxy for profit rates is also problematic, as Keynes later showed that interest rates are influenced by liquidity preference and monetary policy, not just the profitability of enterprise. Moreover, Smith's view that profits tend to fall as nations grow richer has not been consistently borne out—Thomas Piketty has documented that returns on capital have remained remarkably stable over centuries, sometimes exceeding the rate of economic growth.

Book I, Chapter 10: Inequalities of Wages and Profit

Smith identifies five circumstances that cause wages to differ across occupations, even when workers are free to choose their employment: the agreeableness or disagreeableness of the work (dirty and dangerous jobs pay more), the cost of learning the trade (skilled professions command higher wages as compensation for education), the constancy of employment (seasonal workers like masons earn more per day to compensate for idle periods), the degree of trust required (goldsmiths and physicians are paid more for the responsibility they bear), and the probability of success (the law and other liberal professions are under-compensated on average because hopeful entrants overestimate their chances). Smith memorably observes that humans universally overvalue their chances of gain and undervalue their chances of loss.

The second part of this chapter is a powerful critique of European policy that distorts the natural equality of advantages across occupations. Smith attacks corporation privileges and the Statute of Apprenticeship, which restrain competition and keep wages artificially high in some trades. He argues that long apprenticeships are unnecessary and counterproductive—they breed idleness rather than skill. In one of his most quoted passages, he declares: "People of the same trade seldom meet together, even for merriment and diversion, but the conversation ends in a conspiracy against the public." He also criticizes how towns, through their corporate regulations, systematically exploit the countryside.

Counterpoints

Smith's five circumstances of wage inequality remain broadly accepted, but his blanket opposition to apprenticeships has been questioned. German-style apprenticeship systems have proven highly effective at producing skilled workers and reducing youth unemployment. Smith's assumption that free markets would naturally equalize advantages across occupations also underestimates the role of discrimination, social capital, and information barriers that prevent equal access to opportunities. Modern labour economists like Alan Krueger have shown that minimum wage regulations, which Smith would likely have opposed, can sometimes increase employment by counteracting employer monopsony power.

Book I, Chapter 11: Rent of Land

Smith devotes his longest chapter to rent, which he defines as the price paid for the use of land and characterizes as a monopoly price. Unlike wages and profits, which are causes of price, rent is an effect—it is what remains after paying the costs of cultivation and ordinary profits. Land producing food will always yield some rent, because food is always in demand and fertile land produces more than is needed to compensate the farmer. The chapter examines at great length how the relative prices of different types of produce—corn versus cattle, for instance—change as society advances, and includes a massive historical digression on fluctuations in the value of silver over several centuries.

Smith concludes the chapter by identifying three great orders of society: those who live by rent (landlords), those who live by wages (labourers), and those who live by profit (capitalists). He argues that the interests of landlords and labourers are naturally aligned with the general interest of society, since both benefit from economic growth. But the interest of merchants and manufacturers is always somewhat different from, and even opposite to, that of the public—they seek to widen their own markets and narrow competition. Smith warns that any proposal from this class "ought always to be listened to with great precaution" and examined with "the most suspicious attention."

Counterpoints

David Ricardo later developed a more rigorous theory of rent based on differential fertility of land, showing that rent arises from the difference between the productivity of a given plot and the least productive plot in cultivation. Henry George took Smith's insight about rent as monopoly price and proposed a single tax on land values, arguing that since land is not produced by human effort, its rent is an unearned increment that should be taxed away. Smith's characterization of landlords as having interests aligned with society also contradicts his own observation that they are often too indolent to understand those interests—a tension Marx later exploited in his critique of the rentier class.

Book II, Introduction & Chapter 1: The Nature of Stock and Capital

Smith opens Book II by arguing that the accumulation of stock (capital) is a prerequisite for the division of labour. A weaver cannot dedicate himself entirely to weaving unless he has a stock of provisions, materials, and tools to sustain him until his product is sold. As the division of labour advances, ever-larger stocks of capital are required. Smith then distinguishes between the different portions of a person's stock: that reserved for immediate consumption (which yields no revenue), fixed capital (machines, buildings, land improvements, and acquired skills that yield revenue without changing hands), and circulating capital (provisions, materials, finished goods, and money that yield revenue only through exchange).

The distinction between fixed and circulating capital is applied to the economy as a whole. Smith emphasizes that the sole purpose of both types of capital is to maintain and augment the stock reserved for immediate consumption—the real wealth that feeds, clothes, and houses people. Fixed capital requires circulating capital to support it: the finest machines produce nothing without materials and workers. Smith also introduces an important hierarchy: lands, mines, and fisheries provide the raw materials that ultimately replenish all other forms of capital.

Book II, Chapters 2–5: Money, Capital Accumulation, and Employment of Capitals

Chapter 2 examines money as a branch of the general stock of society. Smith distinguishes between the gross revenue of a nation (its total annual produce) and the neat revenue (what remains after replacing fixed and circulating capital). Money, while essential for circulation, is merely a tool—it is the great wheel of commerce, not the goods it circulates. Smith discusses how paper money and banking can replace gold and silver coins, freeing up precious metals for productive use. He praises the Scottish banking system and its use of the cash account (a form of overdraft facility), though he warns against excessive issuance of paper money and argues that banks should lend only for short-term commercial purposes, not for speculative projects.

In Chapter 3, Smith draws a fundamental distinction between productive and unproductive labour. Productive labour adds value to the materials it works on (a manufacturer's work), while unproductive labour, however honourable or useful, produces nothing that could afterwards purchase an equal quantity of labour (servants, soldiers, churchmen, lawyers). What is saved from revenue and added to capital employs productive labour; what is spent on consumption employs unproductive labour. Parsimony, not industry, is the immediate cause of capital accumulation. Chapters 4 and 5 examine interest rates and the different employments of capital. Smith ranks capital employed in agriculture as most beneficial to society, followed by manufacturing, then wholesale trade, and finally the carrying trade—each putting successively less domestic labour into motion.

Counterpoints

Smith's distinction between productive and unproductive labour has been widely criticized. John Stuart Mill observed that a teacher who produces educated citizens is surely "productive" in a meaningful sense. The modern service economy would be almost entirely "unproductive" by Smith's definition, yet services constitute the majority of GDP in developed nations. Smith's ranking of capital employments, with agriculture at the top, reflects a physiocratic bias that his own analysis elsewhere undermines—he demonstrates repeatedly that merchants and manufacturers generate enormous wealth. Keynesian economics later challenged Smith's emphasis on parsimony as the driver of growth, arguing that excessive saving can cause economic depression through insufficient demand.

Book III: The Different Progress of Opulence in Different Nations

Smith argues that the natural course of development should proceed from agriculture to manufacturing to foreign commerce. Capital naturally flows first to agriculture (the safest and most productive investment), then to manufacturing, and finally to trade. But in Europe, this natural order was entirely inverted by historical accident and misguided policy. After the fall of the Roman Empire, feudal institutions concentrated land in vast estates under the law of primogeniture and entails, which prevented division and sale. Great proprietors were poor improvers—they lacked both inclination and ability. Their tenants, first slaves, then metayers (sharecroppers), had no incentive to improve land they did not own. Oppressive taxation (like the French taille) further discouraged agricultural investment.

The transformation came through commerce and manufactures, which gradually furnished the great lords with luxury goods they could consume personally. In Smith's memorable phrase: "For a pair of diamond buckles, perhaps, or for something as frivolous and useless, they exchanged the maintenance of a thousand men for a year." As lords dismissed retainers and raised rents, tenants gained independence through longer leases. Commerce thus introduced order, good government, and individual liberty into the countryside—a revolution brought about by two classes of people (vain lords and self-interested merchants) who "had not the least intention to serve the public." Smith considers this unintended consequence of commerce the most important of all its effects.

Counterpoints

Smith's account of feudal decline and commercial rise is a powerful narrative, but it oversimplifies a complex historical process. Marc Bloch and other medieval historians have shown that feudal agriculture was more dynamic and innovative than Smith suggests. The role of plague, demographic change, and state formation in ending serfdom is given insufficient weight. Smith's claim that the natural order of development proceeds from agriculture to manufacturing has been challenged by development economists like Ha-Joon Chang, who argues that many successful developing nations (including Britain itself) industrialized through deliberate state intervention rather than by following any "natural" sequence.

Book IV, Chapters 1–2: The Mercantile System and the Invisible Hand

Book IV opens with Smith's sustained critique of the mercantile system—the prevailing economic doctrine that equated national wealth with the accumulation of gold and silver. Smith demonstrates that this is a fundamental confusion: wealth consists not in money but in what money purchases. The mercantilist obsession with maintaining a "favourable balance of trade" led to absurd policies: prohibiting the export of gold and silver, erecting tariff barriers, and granting monopoly privileges to favoured industries. Smith argues that gold and silver naturally regulate themselves according to effectual demand—a nation that has goods to exchange will always have sufficient money, just as a nation that has goods to trade for wine will always have wine.

Chapter 2 contains perhaps the most famous passage in all of economics: the invisible hand. Smith argues that every individual, by pursuing his own interest, is "led by an invisible hand to promote an end which was no part of his intention." Each person naturally prefers domestic to foreign industry (for security) and directs his capital to produce the greatest value (for profit). In doing so, he unintentionally maximizes national income. Smith argues that no statesman or lawgiver can better direct capital than the individual who knows his own local situation. Import restrictions that give domestic producers a monopoly are either useless (if domestic goods are already cheaper) or harmful (if foreign goods are cheaper). What is prudence in the conduct of every private family cannot be folly in a great kingdom: buy from abroad what others can produce more cheaply.

Counterpoints

The "invisible hand" is arguably the most contested idea in economics. Smith himself acknowledged exceptions—he supported the Navigation Acts for national defence. Market failure theory has identified numerous cases where individual self-interest diverges from social welfare: externalities (pollution), public goods (lighthouses, national defence), information asymmetries (used car markets), and monopoly power. Friedrich List argued that free trade benefits advanced nations at the expense of developing ones, justifying infant industry protection. Modern trade theory (Krugman, 1979) shows that comparative advantage alone cannot explain trade patterns—economies of scale and strategic trade policy also play important roles. Smith's own historical analysis shows that Britain industrialized behind tariff walls, not through unilateral free trade.

Book IV, Chapters 3–5: Trade Restraints, Drawbacks, and Bounties

Smith continues his critique of mercantilist trade policy by examining specific instruments. Extraordinary restraints on imports from nations with which the balance of trade is supposedly unfavourable are doubly unreasonable—the balance of trade between any two nations proves nothing about the overall wealth of either. Drawbacks (refunds of import duties on goods subsequently re-exported) are generally justified, as they allow a country's merchants to compete in foreign markets without being burdened by domestic taxation. However, Smith is far more critical of bounties on exportation, which he considers harmful subsidies that force trade into unprofitable channels.

The chapter on bounties includes Smith's devastating analysis of the corn bounty. He argues that the bounty does not raise the real price of corn (the quantity of labour it commands) but merely raises its nominal price (the quantity of silver it exchanges for). By artificially raising the money price of corn—the commodity that regulates the prices of labour, raw materials, and manufactures—the bounty effectively degrades the value of silver domestically, making all British goods more expensive relative to foreign goods. The bounty thus hurts manufacturers, benefits only corn merchants, and taxes the entire population twice: once through the direct cost of the bounty, and again through higher food prices.

Counterpoints

Smith's analysis of bounties and trade policy, while powerful, assumes that free markets always allocate resources efficiently. Modern agricultural economists recognize that food security can justify some degree of agricultural protection—relying entirely on imports for basic sustenance creates dangerous strategic vulnerabilities, as wartime blockades can attest. Smith's blanket opposition to export subsidies also conflicts with the historical experience of East Asian development, where strategic subsidies to export-oriented industries (South Korea, Taiwan, Japan) proved spectacularly successful, as documented by Alice Amsden and Robert Wade.

Book IV, Chapters 6–8: Treaties of Commerce, Colonies, and the Conclusion of Mercantilism

Smith examines treaties of commerce, arguing that they typically serve the interests of merchants and manufacturers rather than the public. He then devotes his longest chapter in Book IV to colonies, tracing their origins from ancient Greece and Rome through the European colonization of the Americas. The ancient colonies were established from clear necessity—overcrowding at home. But European colonies arose from no necessity, driven instead by the search for gold, the accident of Columbus's voyage, and subsequent commercial ambition. Smith acknowledges that colonies have generated enormous wealth, but argues that the monopoly of colonial trade has been harmful to both the mother country and the colonies, diverting British capital into less profitable channels and enriching a small class of merchants at the expense of the broader public.

Chapter 8 concludes the analysis of mercantilism by examining how the system discourages the export of raw materials while encouraging their import, and prohibits the export of manufacturing tools. Smith observes that these policies uniformly benefit merchants and manufacturers at the expense of workers and primary producers. He delivers a scathing indictment of legislation obtained by manufacturers—penalties for exporting wool, for example, once included cutting off the offender's hand. Smith argues that "it is the industry which is carried on for the benefit of the rich and the powerful, that is principally encouraged by our mercantile system. That which is carried on for the benefit of the poor and the indigent is too often either neglected or oppressed."

Counterpoints

Smith's analysis of colonialism focuses almost entirely on its economic inefficiency while paying relatively little attention to its moral dimensions—the dispossession and exploitation of indigenous peoples. Later critics, from Eric Williams to Sven Beckert, have argued that the wealth generated by colonial slavery and resource extraction was not merely a misallocation of capital but a foundational element of European industrialization. Smith's argument that colonies would be better off with free trade also underestimates the structural inequalities that colonialism created—dependency theorists like Andre Gunder Frank showed how colonial economic structures locked developing nations into subordinate roles in the global economy.

Book IV, Chapter 9: The Agricultural Systems (Physiocracy)

Smith examines the Physiocratic school—the French economists who argued that land is the sole source of wealth and that only agricultural labour is truly "productive." Led by Quesnay and his followers, the Physiocrats divided society into three classes: proprietors of land, cultivators (the "productive class"), and artificers and merchants (the "barren" or "unproductive" class). They argued that manufacturing merely transforms existing value without creating new value, since the manufacturer's output simply replaces his costs of subsistence and materials. Only agriculture produces a net surplus (rent) over and above all costs.

Smith is respectful but ultimately critical. He agrees that the Physiocrats provided a useful corrective to Colbert's mercantile policies, which had favoured towns over countryside and depressed French agriculture. But their system, like the mercantile one, overvalues one sector at the expense of others. Smith argues that if the Physiocratic prescription were fully implemented—taxing only landowners and freeing all trade—it would come closer to the truth than mercantilism, but would still misrepresent the economy. Manufacturing and commerce clearly create wealth, even if they do not produce a physical surplus in the way agriculture does. The Physiocratic system, Smith concludes, "with all its imperfections, is perhaps the nearest approximation to the truth that has yet been published upon the subject of political economy."

Counterpoints

While Smith rightly criticizes the Physiocrats for undervaluing manufacturing, some modern ecological economists have revived elements of their insight. Herman Daly and others in the ecological economics tradition argue that all economic activity ultimately depends on natural resources and energy flows from the earth—a perspective closer to the Physiocratic view than to the classical or neoclassical framework. The Physiocratic emphasis on a single tax on land was revived by Henry George and continues to attract advocates who argue it would be more efficient and equitable than taxes on labour or capital.

Book V, Chapter 1: The Expenses of the Sovereign

Smith identifies four legitimate expenses of government. First, national defence: Smith traces the evolution of military organization from hunter-gatherer societies (where every man is a warrior at no public cost) through shepherding nations (whose entire populations march as armies) to commercial societies, where the division of labour makes standing armies necessary. A militia of citizens cannot match the discipline of professional soldiers, because in commercial society, the art of war has become too complex to be mastered part-time. Second, the administration of justice: Smith argues that government itself arose from the need to protect property, and that civil government is "in reality instituted for the defence of the rich against the poor."

Third, public works and institutions that benefit society but would never be profitable for any individual or small group to maintain—roads, bridges, canals, harbours, and educational institutions. Smith advocates that these should be funded, where possible, by user fees rather than general taxation, to ensure they are maintained according to actual demand. He devotes considerable space to education, arguing that the division of labour, while increasing productivity, tends to make the common worker stupid and ignorant by confining his attention to a few simple operations. Public education is therefore necessary to counteract this tendency. Fourth, Smith discusses the expense of maintaining the dignity of the sovereign. He argues that each of these expenses should be funded in proportion to the benefit received, with local expenses funded locally.

Counterpoints

Smith's advocacy for standing armies over citizen militias has been challenged by republican theorists from Machiavelli to modern scholars who argue that professional armies can become instruments of tyranny. His admission that government exists to protect the rich is remarkably candid but sits uneasily with his broader framework of natural liberty. Smith's arguments about education are prescient but his preference for user-funded public works has been criticized by economists who emphasize network effects and positive externalities—the benefits of roads and education extend far beyond their direct users, justifying broader public funding. Amartya Sen has argued that Smith underestimates the government's role in promoting human capabilities beyond mere literacy.

Book V, Chapter 2: Sources of Public Revenue and Principles of Taxation

Smith examines how government should raise revenue, first from public property (crown lands, which he generally recommends selling) and then from taxation. He sets out his famous four maxims of taxation, which remain foundational to public finance: (1) Equality—subjects should contribute in proportion to the revenue they enjoy under the state's protection; (2) Certainty—the tax should be certain and not arbitrary, as "a very considerable degree of inequality is not near so great an evil as a very small degree of uncertainty"; (3) Convenience—taxes should be levied at the time and in the manner most convenient for the contributor; and (4) Economy—taxes should take as little from the people beyond what they bring into the treasury as possible.

Smith then conducts a detailed review of taxes on rent (both land rent and house rent), profits, wages, and consumable commodities. He generally favours taxes on land rent (since they cannot be passed on to tenants or consumers) and consumption taxes on luxuries (since they fall on voluntary spending). He opposes taxes on necessities, which ultimately raise wages and thus fall on employers. Smith is deeply sceptical of taxes on profits, which he argues are easily evaded and tend to drive capital abroad. Throughout, he illustrates his principles with examples from England, France, Holland, and the ancient world, consistently showing how violations of his four maxims lead to economic distortion and injustice.

Counterpoints

Smith's four maxims are widely accepted in principle but difficult to implement in practice. His preference for proportional taxation (equal rates regardless of income) has been challenged by advocates of progressive taxation, from John Stuart Mill onward, who argue that the marginal utility of income decreases as income rises, making it equitable to tax higher incomes at higher rates. Smith's opposition to taxes on profits underestimates the difficulty of capital flight in practice—modern states successfully tax corporate profits, albeit imperfectly. His arguments about consumption taxes on luxuries also neglect the regressive nature of many consumption taxes, which tend to burden the poor disproportionately when applied to common goods.

Book V, Chapter 3: Public Debts

Smith's final chapter examines the growing burden of public debt, which he views with deep alarm. He traces how feudal sovereigns, who had to save for wars from their ordinary revenues, have been replaced by modern commercial states that borrow lavishly. The same commercial prosperity that makes borrowing necessary (because sovereigns spend freely on luxuries rather than saving) also makes it easy (because merchants and manufacturers have surplus capital to lend). Governments, seduced by the facility of borrowing, dispense themselves from the duty of saving. Smith traces the evolution of British public finance from short-term "anticipations" of tax revenue to the "ruinous practice" of perpetual funding—borrowing against permanent annuities that are never repaid.

Smith describes how the sinking fund, originally established to retire old debts, has been repeatedly raided to finance new borrowing. He warns that the enormous debts of European nations "will in the long-run probably ruin" all of them, either through outright default or through the stealthy default of currency debasement. He notes that nations throughout history have resorted to raising the denomination of the coin—paying debts with debased currency. Smith's sobering conclusion is that a nation burdened by excessive debt faces a grim choice between honest bankruptcy and the dishonest practice of inflation, and that the latter has almost always been preferred because it is less visible.

Counterpoints

Smith's alarm about public debt, while prescient in some respects, has been challenged by subsequent economic thought. Keynesian economics argues that government borrowing during recessions is not only acceptable but essential for maintaining employment and preventing economic collapse. Modern Monetary Theory goes further, arguing that sovereign nations that issue their own currency face no inherent constraint on borrowing in that currency. Historically, Britain's national debt continued to grow after Smith's warning but did not lead to the ruin he predicted—instead, the country entered its greatest period of prosperity during the Industrial Revolution. Abba Lerner's concept of "functional finance" suggests that the purpose of government borrowing should be judged by its effects on the economy, not by the size of the debt itself.
